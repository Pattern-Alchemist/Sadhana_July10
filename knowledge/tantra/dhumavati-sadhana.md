# Dhūmāvatī Sādhana

> The seventh of the Daśa Mahāvidyās, Dhūmāvatī ("she who is composed of smoke") is iconographically an aged, smoke-complexioned widow riding a horseless chariot bearing a crow banner, holding a winnowing basket. High caution — an antinomian Mahāvidyā rite whose iconography deliberately encodes inauspiciousness (widowhood, hunger, decay, smoke, the crow) as a vehicle of liberating insight. Preserved here as heritage / source material. Not instructional.

Dhūmāvatī is the seventh Mahāvidyā in the canonical ordering given in the *Todala Tantra* and reproduced in the *Śākta Praamoda* (Rājadevanandan, 19th c. Banaras digest) and the *Mahāvidyā Tantra Sāra*. The independent *Dhūmāvatī Tantra* survives principally through the recension embedded in these Śākta digest compilations; the SanskritDocuments text `bhagavatIdhUmAvatIsAdhanA.itx` (transcribed by volunteers from a printed paddhati) preserves a representative witness of the dhāraṇā and japa framework. The Sanskrit digital editions digitised on the Internet Archive (the Goswami Prahlad Giri Hindi *Dhumāvatī Mahāvidyā*, the Ashok Gaud *Dhumāvatī Rahasya*, and the *Śrī Dhumāvatī Sādhana aura Siddhi*) give the full paddhati — nyāsa, dhāraṇā, mūla-mantra, yantra, homa — in print form.

Kinsley's 1997 monograph *Tantric Visions of the Divine Feminine* remains the standard scholarly treatment of the Mahāvidyā cycle, and devotes an entire chapter (pp. 165–181 of the UC Press edition) to Dhūmāvatī. Kinsley reads her iconography as a deliberate sacralisation of all that classical Hindu ritual culture marks as inauspicious — widowhood, poverty, hunger, old age, the crow, the horseless cart, smoke — and demonstrates that her position in the Mahāvidyā cycle is structurally antinomian: she is the goddess encountered *after* beauty, power, and fecundity have been exhaustively enumerated, and she encodes the negation of every positive signifier. Goudriaan and Gupta (1981, §IV on Śākta literature) list the *Dhūmāvatī Tantra* among the late-medieval independent Mahāvidyā Tantras that survive mainly through the digests.

The *Devī-Bhāgavata Purāṇa* 3.5 contains the narrative frame in which Satī, in anger at Dakṣa's sacrifice, manifests the ten Mahāvidyās prior to her death; Dhūmāvatī appears as the smoke-clad widow form in that list. The mythic cycle positions her as a "Dakṣa-sacrifice aftermath" goddess — she is, in the Śākta reading, the Śakti who survives the dissolution of the sacrificial world.

## II. Iconography (per sources)

According to the dhāraṇā preserved in the *Śākta Praamoda* and summarised in Kinsley (1997, pp. 167–170), Dhūmāvatī is described as an aged (*vṛddhā*), smoke-complexioned (*dhūmra-varṇā*) widow, with dishevelled hair, sunken eyes, missing teeth, sagging breasts, dressed in dirty white widow's rags, restless and trembling. She holds a winnowing basket (*surpa*) in one hand and bestows boons or knowledge (*varada*) with the other. She stands or sits in a horseless chariot (*aśva-ratha*); above the chariot flies a banner (*patākā*) marked with a crow (*kāka*). The crow is also sometimes her *vāhana* (mount), and at other times her banner-emblem; the textual witnesses vary on whether she rides the crow or merely displays it.

The commentary tradition, as summarised by Kinsley (1997), notes the deliberate contrast with the iconography of Lakṣmī: where Lakṣmī rides the lotus and the owl, Dhūmāvatī rides a horseless cart and a crow; where Lakṣmī is golden, Dhūmāvatī is the colour of smoke; where Lakṣmī is young, fecund, and ornamented, Dhūmāvatī is aged, widowed, and bare. The *Mahāvidyā Tantra Sāra* extends this contrast to the symbolic register: Dhūmāvatī's winnowing basket (*surpa*) — an instrument that separates grain from chaff — is read by the commentarial tradition as the goddess who winnows the phenomenal world, retaining only the unmanifest residue. The crow (*kāka*) is ritually associated in Hindu culture with the *śrāddha* rite and with the pitṛs (ancestors); the widow-iconography associates her with the dissolution of the marital-fecund axis of dharma.

The Wisdom Library entry on Dhūmāvatī (citing the *Śākta Praamoda* tradition) describes her as "the Śakti that destroys the structure of all things" and the goddess "of poverty" — a reading consistent with Kinsley's antinomian interpretation. No youthful or benign form of Dhūmāvatī is attested in the primary sources surveyed; her iconography is uniformly *ugra* and *alakṣmī-pragmaṇā*.

## III. Mantra & Method (as documented in sources)

> **Editorial note.** The mantra and method below are reproduced from published textual sources for scholarly preservation. This is documentation, not instruction.

- **Bīja mantra:** The mūla-mantra is documented in the *Śākta Praamoda* (Rājadevanandan and Devnandan Singh editions) and in the *Dhumāvatī Rahasya* of Ashok Gaud. The exact form varies across textual lineages; the publicly attested bīja-frame incorporates the *dhūṃ* / *hrīṃ* phonemes prefixed to the goddess's name-bīja, with the characteristic *phaṭ* termination. The specific mūla-mantra is dīkṣā-restricted in published editions; the per-recension exact form is not reproduced here.
- **Mantra count:** Paddhati literature documents puraścaraṇa counts in multiples of 100,000; specific numbers vary by recension.
- **Duration:** Puraścaraṇa cycles documented in the *Dhumāvatī Rahasya* run to multiple weeks of daily japa and homa; the exact count is recension-specific.
- **Direction facing:** South or South-west, per paddhati convention (Dhūmāvatī's directional association in the *Mahāvidyā Tantra Sāra* is *nairṛtya* — the south-west, the quarter of Nirṛti / dissolution).
- **Mālā:** Black or dark-coloured beads (a black-wood or *śyāma* rudrākṣa); the textual witnesses vary. Some paddhatis specify a crow-bone (*kāka-asthi*) mālā; this is recorded as attestation only.

The procedure described in the *Śākta Praamoda* and the *Dhumāvatī Rahasya* opens with the standard nyāsa sequence (kara-nyāsa, aṅga-nyāsa, ṣaḍaṅga-nyāsa, dig-bandhana) followed by the dhāraṇā of the goddess, japa of the mūla-mantra on the prescribed mālā, and homa of black sesame (*kṛṣṇa-tila*), mustard seeds (*sarṣapa*), and *durvā*-grass into a square or triangular kuṇḍa. The practitioner's clothing, food, and resting-place are stipulated to be of white or smoke-grey colour for the duration of the puraścaraṇa. The *Mahāvidyā Tantra Sāra* records aṣṭa-māsika (eight-month) and dvādaśa-māsika (twelve-month) variants of the puraścaraṇa.

Kinsley (1997, pp. 175–181) notes that Dhūmāvatī's worship in living Śākta tradition is comparatively rare and almost exclusively the preserve of *vīrācārī* and *sahajācārī* lineages; the goddess is treated as the deity of *vairāgya* (renunciation) and *jñāna* (insight-through-negation) rather than of worldly boons. Her textual framing consistently warns that her japa, undertaken without the requisite antinomian preparation, is said to produce the very inauspiciousness she embodies — widowhood, poverty, isolation.

## IV. Yantra (if attested)

A yantra is described in the *Śākta Praamoda* (Rājadevanandan and Devnandan Singh editions) and in the *Dhumāvatī Rahasya*. It consists of a square *bhūpura* with four gates, enclosing an eight-petalled lotus (*aṣṭadala-padma*), which in turn encloses a downward-pointing triangle (*adho-mukha-trikoṇa*) housing the goddess's bīja. The triangle is the *yoni-mudrā* orientation, consistent with Dhūmāvatī's Mahāvidyā framing. The yantra is described as inscribed on smoke-grey cloth or on *bhūrja* (birch-bark) using a paste of *kuṅkuma*, *gorocanā*, and the ash of *dhūma*-grass. Specific bīja placements are documented in the *Dhumāvatī Rahasya* and are not reproduced in detail here.

## V. Warnings and Contraindications (per sources)

- The *Śākta Praamoda* paddhati literature explicitly restricts the Dhūmāvatī mūla-mantra to practitioners who have received dīkṣā in an authorised Śākta lineage; the textual tradition is uniform on this point.
- Kinsley (1997, pp. 175–181) documents the textual caution that Dhūmāvatī's japa, undertaken without adequate preparation, is said to produce precisely the inauspicious conditions the goddess embodies — widowhood, poverty, decay. This is presented in the paddhati literature not as moralistic warning but as an operational caveat about the *ugra* nature of the deity.
- The *Mahāvidyā Tantra Sāra* and the *Dhumāvatī Rahasya* both record dietary and purity observances for the duration of the puraścaraṇa: white or smoke-grey clothing, plain food, abstention from meat and alcohol, and a secluded residence (often described as the cremation ground, a deserted house, or a forest hermitage).
- Kinsley (1997) notes that Dhūmāvatī is the only Mahāvidyā who is iconographically a widow and who is consistently associated with the absence of a male consort; her practice is described in the paddhati literature as requiring the practitioner to have internalised the antinomian stance of *vairāgya* prior to undertaking japa.
- The Hindu Dhūmāvatī tradition has no living equivalent in mainstream Brahminical ritual; her worship is uniformly Śākta-Tantric and dīkṣā-gated.
