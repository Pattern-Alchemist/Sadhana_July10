# Māraṇa Karma

> The sixth and most severe of the six ṣaṭ-karma — the transformation / death (*māraṇa*) rite, the most consequential of the *ugra*-operations, producing the targeted transformation or extinction of a hostile force. High caution — the final member of the "fierce four" (vidveṣaṇa, uccāṭana, māraṇa, plus the optional mohana). Distinct from the specific Agni-Māraṇa and Vīrudha-Āhāra-Māraṇa sub-sādhanas catalogued in the existing PDFs of this archive; here māraṇa is catalogued as the standalone sixth ṣaṭ-karma category. Preserved here as heritage / source material. Not instructional.

The *Agni Purāṇa* ch. 138 (Wisdom Library translation) catalogues *māraṇa* as the sixth and final of the *ṣaṭ-karma*. The *Prapañcasāra Tantra* (Avalon 1919 edition) preserves the operative *māraṇa* paddhati within its mantra-śāstra frame; Shastra Deep's essay on *Prapañcasāra* confirms the canonical six-act enumeration with māraṇa positioned sixth. The Wisdom Library "Satkarman" definition page records that the *Ṣaṭsāhasrasaṃhitā* and the *Rasaratnākara* both catalogue the same six operations in the same order.

The *Mahānirvāṇa Tantra* (Avalon / Woodroffe 1913 English translation) — Chapter 26 of the Woodroffe commentary titled "Shakta Sadhana (The Ordinary Ritual)," preserved on sacred-texts.com — explicitly names "driving away or Uccatana, and killing or Marana when performed for a malevolent purpose" as the *kāmya*-karmas within the broader Kaula frame. The pairing of uccāṭana and māraṇa in the Woodroffe commentary is significant: these are the two ṣaṭ-karma explicitly categorised as "malevolent purpose" (*kūla-kāmya*) operations, distinguishing them from the protective, subjugating, immobilising, and discording operations that precede them.

The *Śāradā Tilaka Tantram* (Lakṣmaṇa Deśikendra, c. 10th–11th c., with Rāghavabhaṭṭa's *Padārthādarśa* commentary) preserves māraṇa-mantra formulations within the text's wider pūjā-prakaraṇa. Four Internet Archive digitisations are available: the Sudhakar Malaviya edition, the Avalon 1933 translation (Tantric Texts Series 16), the Mukunda Jha Bakshi Series No. 107 Kashi Sanskrit Series edition, and the Chowkhamba Padārthādarśa edition. The *Brahmayāmala* / *Picumata* (Hatley 2018 critical edition) preserves early attestations of the māraṇa-operation within the Vidyāpīṭha Śaiva-Śākta textual corpus. The *Sāmba Purāṇa*'s "Abhicāra Vidhi" chapter (Vedapath.app translation) documents the broader abhicāra-frame within which māraṇa is positioned.

Sanderson (2009, "The Śaiva Age") and Sanderson ("Śaivism and the Tantric Traditions") document māraṇa as the most consequential of the standard *abhicāra*-operations performed by the Śaiva-Śākta *ācārya*. White (2003, *Kiss of the Yoginī*) discusses māraṇa within the broader frame of the Kāpālika-Kaula ritual matrix from which the medieval Śaiva-Śākta ṣaṭ-karma textual tradition emerges. Woodroffe (1929, *Shakti and Shâkta*) classifies māraṇa as the apex of the *abhicāra* category — "the Karma described in the Tantras and Atharvaveda... magical process with intent to destroy or injure... Hiṃsā-karma, or act injurious to others" — distinguishing it from the five ṣaṭ-karma that precede it.

## II. The Category (per sources)

Māraṇa-karma is the sixth and final of the six ṣaṭ-karma and the apex of the five *ugra* (fierce) operations. The etymology is from *mṛ* ("to die," "to extinguish") + the causal suffix *-aṇa* ("the act of causing to die"); the rite produces the targeted transformation or extinction of a hostile force. The Shastra Deep essay on *Prapañcasāra* confirms that the *Prapañcasāra Tantra* enumerates māraṇa as the sixth of the six, immediately after uccāṭana — positioning māraṇa as the final and most severe of the *ugra*-series.

The textual tradition frames māraṇa with a deliberate double-meaning that is preserved in the published Sanskrit witnesses. The surface reading is "killing" — the production of physical death in the target. The deeper Śaiva-Śākta reading, however, is "transformation" — the production of a transformation so complete that the prior form (the target as hostile force) is extinguished. The Śaiva-Śākta commentarial tradition (Rāghavabhaṭṭa on *Śāradā Tilaka*) documents this duality by classifying māraṇa into the *kāyika* (bodily / physical-death) form and the *ādhidaivika* (spiritual-transformation) form — the latter being the rite by which a hostile *bhūta* or *piśāca* is *transformed* into a protective *vetāla* or *gaṇa* in the service of the practitioner. The *Brahmayāmala* / *Picumata* (Hatley 2018 critical edition) preserves early attestations of this transformative aspect of māraṇa within the Vidyāpīṭha Śaiva-Śākta textual corpus.

Woodroffe (1929) frames māraṇa as the apex of the *abhicāra* category — distinguishing it from the five ṣaṭ-karma that precede it by the irreversibility of its operation. The textual tradition consistently frames māraṇa as the *last resort* ṣaṭ-karma — to be undertaken only when the preceding five (śānti, vaśīkaraṇa, stambhana, vidveṣaṇa, uccāṭana) have failed to neutralise the hostile force, and when the force's continued existence poses a *clear and present* threat to the practitioner's life, lineage, or dharma. The Śaiva-Śākta commentarial tradition (the *Mantra Mahodadhi* of Mahīdhara, 17th c.) records the procedural hierarchy explicitly: śānti first, then vaśīkaraṇa, then stambhana, then vidveṣaṇa, then uccāṭana, and only finally māraṇa — with the caution that māraṇa performed without exhausting the prior five is itself a *pāpa-karma* (sinful act) that incurs karmic rebound on the practitioner.

Sanderson (2009) documents the medieval Śaiva-Śākta *ācārya*'s deployment of māraṇa on behalf of royal patrons as the most consequential of the *abhicāra*-services offered to kings: the rite of "killing" an enemy-king (or, in the transformation-reading, of transforming the enemy-king's hostile intent into a submissive posture) is among the standard textual applications. The escholarship.org article on "Raising the Dead and Saving Them: Transformations in Funerary Ritual in Hindu Tantrism" frames māraṇa within the broader ritual-ecology of medieval Śaiva-Śākta practice, including the māraṇa-related funerary rites.

## III. Mantra & Method (as documented in sources)

> **Editorial note.** The mantra-forms and counts below are documented from published textual sources for scholarly preservation. This is documentation, not instruction.

- **Bīja mantra:** The *Agni Purāṇa* ch. 138 frame attaches the ṣaṭ-karma operations to "all Mantras" — the practitioner's own dīkṣā-mantra, with the māraṇa operation specified by add-on bījas and the *māraṇa-nyāsa*. The specific *māraṇa-bīja* cited in the *Śāradā Tilaka* paddhati and in the *Mantra Mahodadhi* of Mahīdhara is dīkṣā-restricted in published editions; the per-recension exact form is not reproduced here. The Śaiva-Śākta paddhati tradition typically uses *mṛtyu*-seed-bījas incorporating the *kṣ* and *auṁ* / *hūṁ* phonemes (the *kṣauṁ* bīja associated with Narasiṃha and the *hūṁ-phaṭ* termination of fierce mantras), but the operative combination varies by recension.
- **Mantra count:** The *Prapañcasāra* paddhati and the *Śāradā Tilaka* commentarial tradition document puraścaraṇa counts in multiples of 100,000; the *Mantra Mahodadhi* records the māraṇa-specific count, which is typically the highest of the six ṣaṭ-karma counts.
- **Duration:** Cycles of 14, 21, or 41 days are documented in the Śāradā Tilaka paddhati; the Śaiva-Śākta commentarial tradition records that māraṇa-cycles should not exceed 41 days without an explicit guru-permission.
- **Direction facing:** South per paddhati convention for *ugra*-rites (the direction of Yama, death, and the ancestral realm); the *Mahānirvāṇa Tantra* Ch. VIII specifies the kalāśa placement for māraṇa with southern orientation.
- **Kalāśa material:** The Śaiva-Śākta paddhati tradition records a *kāpāla* (skull-bowl) or *asthi* (bone) kalāśa for māraṇa — the funerary-medium being the ritual signature of the rite's intent to extinguish the target. The *Śāradā Tilaka* commentarial tradition notes that the skull-bowl should be from a *śmaśāna* (cremation-ground) or from a *virāṭ*-warrior fallen in battle; the per-recension exact prescription varies.

The procedure documented in the *Agni Purāṇa* ch. 138 (per Wisdom Library's translation) frames māraṇa as the sixth ṣaṭ-karma operation, performed after the standard *ṣaḍaṅga-nyāsa*, *dig-bandhana*, and *guru-pūjā* preliminaries. The practitioner establishes the prescribed (skull-bowl or bone) *kalāśa*, performs *japa* of the operative mantra with the māraṇa-bīja prefixed, and conducts a *homa* with the most *severely ugra* offerings of the ṣaṭ-karma: mustard-oil, *nimba* leaves, salt, *kuṅkuma*-mixed-with-iron-filings, *trivṛt* root, *dūrvā*-grass-pulled-against-the-grain, and — in some recensions — blood-substitutes (*rudhira*-colored *pancha-gavya* or *rakta-candana* paste). The *Śāradā Tilaka* paddhati documents the application of the *māraṇa-nyāsa* on the practitioner's own body prior to the operation, and the inscription of the target's name within the yantra's central *tri-koṇa* with a *khaḍga* (sword) symbol severing the name from the bīja.

The *Mantra Mahodadhi* of Mahīdhara (17th c.) reproduces multiple māraṇa-formulations from the Śaiva-Śākta corpus, including the Agni-Māraṇa form (where the hostile force is consumed by fire-substitute offerings) and the Vīrudha-Āhāra-Māraṇa form (where the hostile force is consumed by energetic-food-poison offerings); both of these sub-sādhanas are catalogued as standalone PDFs in this archive (*Agni Māraṇa Tantra.pdf* and *Vīrudha-Āhāra Māraṇa — The Energetic Food Poison Curse.pdf* in /home/z/my-project/workspace/Arena_Export/knowledge/tantra/). The standalone ṣaṭ-karma māraṇa-category, however, is broader than these two specific sub-sādhanas and admits of multiple additional forms — the kāyika (bodily), ādhidaivika (spiritual-transformation), and other variants documented in the *Śāradā Tilaka* paddhati.

The *Brahmayāmala* / *Picumata* (Hatley 2018 critical edition) preserves early attestations of the māraṇa-operation within the Vidyāpīṭha Śaiva-Śākta textual corpus, including the form by which a hostile *bhūta* is transformed into a protective *vetāla* through the māraṇa-rite's transformative aspect. The per-recension exact forms are dīkṣā-restricted in the published editions.

## IV. Yantra (if attested)

A māraṇa-specific yantra is documented in the *Mantra Mahodadhi* of Mahīdhara and in the Śāradā Tilaka paddhati tradition: a square *bhūpura* with four cardinal gates, an eight-petalled lotus ring, and a central downward-pointing *tri-koṇa* (yoni-triangle) housing the māraṇa-bīja at its bindu. The distinctive feature of the māraṇa-yantra (versus the yantras of the other five ṣaṭ-karma) is a *khaḍga* (sword) or *paraśu* (axe) symbol drawn through the *tri-koṇa*, severing the target's name (inscribed within the *tri-koṇa*) from the bīja at the bindu. The *Śāradā Tilaka* paddhati documents the application of this yantra inscribed on *bhūrja* (birch-bark) using a paste of *kuṅkuma*, *gorocanā*, *asthi-bhasma* (bone-ash, consistent with the skull-bowl / bone kalāśa-signature), and *rakta-candana*. The Yantra-Chin-Ta-Man compilation (Scribd-preserved) documents a specific māraṇa-yantra form featuring a "downward pointing pentagram" structure with the target positioned in the inverse triangle at the center. Specific bīja placements are dīkṣā-restricted in the published editions and are not reproduced here.

## V. Warnings and Contraindications (per sources)

- The *Śāradā Tilaka* paddhati, the *Prapañcasāra* commentary of Padmapāda, and the *Mantra Mahodadhi* of Mahīdhara all restrict the operative māraṇa-mantra to practitioners who have received the relevant dīkṣā in an authorised lineage; the textual tradition is uniform and emphatic on this point — māraṇa is the most dīkṣā-restricted of the six ṣaṭ-karma.
- Woodroffe (1929) classifies māraṇa as the apex of the *abhicāra* category — "the Karma described in the Tantras and Atharvaveda... Hiṃsā-karma, or act injurious to others" — and warns that māraṇa is subject to the most severe of the *kāmya*-rite cautions of *Mahānirvāṇa Tantra* Ch. VIII.
- Sanderson (2009, "The Śaiva Age") documents the Śaiva-Śākta textual tradition's caution that māraṇa performed without correct *mantra-siddhi* rebounds on the practitioner with the greatest severity of all the ṣaṭ-karma: the textual witnesses describe māraṇa-specific rebound as the *practitioner's own* death — physical, energetic, or lineage-death (the dissolution of the practitioner's *dīkṣā-saṃpradāya*).
- The *Mantra Mahodadhi* of Mahīdhara records the procedural-hierarchy caution: māraṇa performed without exhausting the prior five ṣaṭ-karma (śānti → vaśīkaraṇa → stambhana → vidveṣaṇa → uccāṭana) is itself a *pāpa-karma* (sinful act) that incurs karmic rebound on the practitioner even when the rite is technically "successful" against the target.
- The *Brahmayāmala* / *Picumata* (Hatley 2018 critical edition) documents the early Śaiva-Śākta tradition's prohibition of māraṇa-operations on one's guru, on a brāhmaṇa, on a kinsman, on a *dīkṣā-gotra*-member (a co-initiate), on a woman (in most recensions), or on a child (in all recensions); the textual witnesses describe the contravention of these prohibitions as resulting in the practitioner's own immediate karmic-catastrophe.
- The standalone ṣaṭ-karma māraṇa-category (this file) should be carefully distinguished from the specific Agni-Māraṇa and Vīrudha-Āhāra-Māraṇa sub-sādhanas catalogued as standalone PDFs in this archive: the sub-sādhanas are *specific applications* of the broader māraṇa-ṣaṭ-karma category, using particular Agni (fire) or Āhāra (food-poison) modalities respectively. The standalone ṣaṭ-karma category admits of multiple additional forms (kāyika / bodily, ādhidaivika / spiritual-transformation, etc.) documented in the *Śāradā Tilaka* paddhati.
- The Śaiva-Śākta commentarial tradition distinguishes māraṇa-karma from the modern commercial "māraṇa-mantra" literature (a 20th-c. popular genre, exemplified by self-published PDFs and Reddit discussions); the textual-tradition operation is a *mantra-śāstra* rite requiring prior dīkṣā and puraścaraṇa, undertaken only under direct guru-supervision, and never as a self-help procedure.
