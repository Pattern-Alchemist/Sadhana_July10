# Śānti Karma

> The first of the six ṣaṭ-karma — the pacification (*śānti*) rite for calming hostile forces, neutralising disturbance, and restoring equilibrium. Moderate caution (the only saumya member of the six-act framework). Preserved here as heritage / source material. Not instructional.

The *Agni Purāṇa* (a medieval encyclopaedic Purāṇa, dated by Hazra to c. 8th–11th c.) preserves in its chapter 138 the most accessible single-text enumerations of the *ṣaṭ-karma* framework. The Wisdom Library English translation of this chapter (titled "The six accessory acts relating to all Mantras") catalogues śānti alongside vaśīkaraṇa, stambhana, vidveṣaṇa, uccāṭana, and māraṇa as the six ritual operations attachable to any mantra. The chapter is quoted at length in the Dharmavidya index of Agni Purāṇa chapters, which lists chapter 138 as "The six accessory acts relating to all mantras."

The *Prapañcasāra Tantra* — attributed to Śaṅkarācārya by the Śākta tradition, but dated by Goudriaan and Gupta (1981) to c. 8th–9th c. — preserves the full ṣaṭ-karma operational chapter within its mantra-śāstra frame. Avalon's 1919 edition (Tantric Texts Series 18, Agamanusandhana Samiti, Calcutta) is preserved in multiple digitised copies on the Internet Archive. The Shastra Deep essay on *Prapañcasāra* explicitly enumerates the six esoteric acts "known as Ṣaṭkarma (षट्कर्म) — śānti (pacification), vaśīkaraṇa (controlling), stambhana (immobilizing), vidveṣaṇa (causing enmity)" and the remaining two (uccāṭana, māraṇa), confirming the *Prapañcasāra* as a complete ṣaṭ-karma textual witness.

Sanderson (2009, "The Śaiva Age") documents the *ṣaṭ-karma* framework as a defining feature of the Mantramārga Śaiva-Śākta textual corpus from the 7th–8th c. onward, with śānti consistently positioned first as the *saumya* (benign) member of the six. The Wisdom Library "Satkarman" definition page notes that the *Ṣaṭsāhasrasaṃhitā* (an expansion of the *Kubjikāmatatantra*) and the 13th-century *Rasaratnākara* of Nityanātha Siddha both catalogue the six rites.

## II. The Category (per sources)

Śānti-karma is the first and only *saumya* (benign, pacific) member of the six-act ṣaṭ-karma framework. Woodroffe (1929, *Shakti and Shâkta*) frames the ṣaṭ-karma within the broader category of *abhicāra* — defined by Woodroffe as "the Karma described in the Tantras and Atharvaveda" — but distinguishes śānti from the five *ugra* (fierce) operations that follow. Śānti-karma is the rite of pacification: it is performed to calm hostile forces (*śānti-vighna*), neutralise disturbances (whether astrological, planetary, daivic, or bhūta-related), purify the field after a prior fierce rite, and restore ritual and energetic equilibrium. The Hinduwebsite and Aghori.it English excerpts of *Mahānirvāṇa Tantra* confirm that the text distinguishes *kāmya-karma* (desire-motivated rites, including the *ugra* ṣaṭ-karma) from the *śānti*-oriented purification rites that frame every dīkṣā.

The *Sāmba Purāṇa* chapter on "Tantric Rituals, Mantra Application, and Ascetic Practices" — surfaced through the Vedapath.app verse-by-verse translation — prescribes for *Śānti-karma* the use of *bilva* (Aegle marmelos), *palāśa* (Butea monosperma), *durvā* grass (Cynodon dactylon), sesame seeds (*tila*), *darbha* grass (Desmostachya bipinnata), and various flowers. The prescription is consistent with the broader Śaiva-Śākta textual convention by which the *saumya* rite uses white / pale / sweet-smelling offerings, in contrast to the *ugra* rites which use red pāyasam, blood-substitutes, and reverse-flowing offerings.

Śānti-karma differs from the other five ṣaṭ-karma in that its direction is *restorative* rather than *transitive*: where vaśīkaraṇa acts on another person's will, stambhana arrests their motion, vidveṣaṇa creates division between parties, uccāṭana drives them out, and māraṇa extinguishes them, śānti operates on the *field* — the practitioner's own environment, the planetary configuration, the disturbed dīkṣā-cakra — restoring a prior equilibrium rather than producing a new effect in an external object. Sanderson (2009) documents this *kṣema*-oriented function as śānti's distinctive signature within the Mantramārga system.

## III. Mantra & Method (as documented in sources)

> **Editorial note.** The mantra-forms and counts below are documented from published textual sources for scholarly preservation. This is documentation, not instruction.

- **Bīja mantra:** The textual witnesses vary. The *Agni Purāṇa* ch. 138 frame attaches the ṣaṭ-karma operations to "all Mantras" — i.e. the practitioner's own dīkṣā-mantra, with the operation specified by add-on bījas and nyāsa-orientation. The specific *śānti-bīja* cited in the *Śāradā Tilaka* paddhati tradition (with the *Padārthādarśa* of Rāghavabhaṭṭa) is *dīkṣā-restricted* in the published editions; the per-recension exact form is not reproduced here.
- **Mantra count:** The *Prapañcasāra* paddhati and the *Śāradā Tilaka* commentarial tradition document puraścaraṇa counts in multiples of 100,000; the *śānti* count is typically recorded as a multiple of ten thousand.
- **Duration:** Cycles of 7, 21, or 41 days are documented in the *Śāradā Tilaka* paddhati and the *Prapañcasāra*; the *Mahānirvāṇa Tantra* Chapter VIII documents the *kalāśa*-based śānti operation with daily japa over the cycle.
- **Direction facing:** East, per Śaiva-Śākta paddhati convention; the *Mahānirvāṇa Tantra* confirms that the kalāśa for *śānti* is established in the east of the maṇḍapa.

The procedure documented in the *Agni Purāṇa* chapter 138 (per Wisdom Library's translation) frames śānti as the first *ṣaṭ-karma* operation, performed after the standard *ṣaḍaṅga-nyāsa*, *dig-bandhana*, and *guru-pūjā* preliminaries. The practitioner establishes a *kalāśa* (consecrated pot) — *Mahānirvāṇa Tantra* Ch. VIII specifies a clay (*mṛt*) kalāśa for all-purposes including śānti — performs *japa* of the operative mantra, and conducts a *homa* with the *saumya* offerings (ghee, *tila*, white flowers, sweet *pāyasam*). The *Sāmba Purāṇa* prescription of *bilva*, *palāśa*, *durvā*, *tila*, and *darbha* is consistent with this frame.

The *Śāradā Tilaka* commentarial tradition (Rāghavabhaṭṭa's *Padārthādarśa*) documents the application of śānti in the specific context of *graha-śānti* (planetary pacification), where the rite is performed to neutralise adverse planetary transits, and in the context of *dīkṣā-śānti*, where it is performed after an intense puraścaraṇa to restore equilibrium. Woodroffe (1929, *Shakti and Shâkta*) frames śānti as the appropriate response to suspected *abhicāra* attacks — the only ṣaṭ-karma operation that can legitimately *counter* the other five.

## IV. Yantra (if attested)

No specific yantra is uniquely prescribed for śānti-karma across the textual witnesses surveyed. The *Agni Purāṇa* ch. 138 and the *Prapañcasāra* paddhati indicate that the śānti operation uses the general Śākta yantra framework — the practitioner's own dīkṣā-devatā's yantra, with the *śānti-bīja* installed in the central bindu and the saumya-orientation (east-facing, white-akṣata, ghee-lamp) applied to the pūjā. The *Śāradā Tilaka* commentarial tradition documents a *śānti-cakra* form (a six-petalled lotus with the practitioner's iṣṭa-devatā bīja at the centre and the śānti-bīja at the pericarp) in the context of *graha-śānti* operations, but the per-recension exact form is dīkṣā-restricted in published editions.

## V. Warnings and Contraindications (per sources)

- The *Śāradā Tilaka* paddhati and the *Prapañcasāra* commentary of Padmapāda restrict the operative śānti-mantra to practitioners who have received the relevant dīkṣā in an authorised lineage; the textual tradition is uniform on this point — śānti-karma, despite being the *saumya* member of the six, is still a *mantra-śāstra* operation requiring prior *mantra-dīkṣā*.
- Woodroffe (1929) notes that śānti-karma, while protective, can be misapplied: performed without correct identification of the disturbance-source (graha, bhūta, daiva, or human *abhicāra*), the rite may suppress symptoms without addressing the cause, leaving the underlying disturbance to resurface.
- The *Mahānirvāṇa Tantra* Chapter VIII frames the *kāmya* (desire-motivated) ṣaṭ-karma — including śānti when performed for personal protection rather than field-restoration — as requiring the prior Kaula-dīkṣā and the observance of the *pañca-tattva* frame; without that frame, Woodroffe notes, the rite is *niradhāra* (without foundation).
- The *Sāmba Purāṇa* prescription of specific offerings (*bilva*, *palāśa*, *durvā*, *tila*, *darbha*) carries the implicit caution that substitution with inappropriate materials (e.g. red flowers, salt, or other *ugra*-oriented offerings) inverts the rite's valence from śānti to one of the *ugra* ṣaṭ-karma, with potentially destabilising effects on the field.
- Śānti-karma should not be confused with the yogic *ṣaṭ-karma* of Haṭhayoga (the six purificatory techniques — neti, dhauti, nauli, basti, kapālabhāti, trāṭaka); the tantric śānti-karma is a mantra-śāstra rite, not a bodily purification technique.
