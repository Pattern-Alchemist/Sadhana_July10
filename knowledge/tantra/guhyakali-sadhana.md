# Guhyakālī Sādhana

> Guhyakālī ("the Secret Kālī") is the esoteric form of Kālī documented in the *Guhyakālī Khaṇḍa* of the *Mahākāla Saṃhitā* — a many-faced, many-armed goddess manifesting in forms with one to one hundred faces, the principal *daśavaktra* (ten-faced) form having 27 eyes and 54 arms, including human, animal, and bird faces. High caution — the most secret and elaborate of the Kālī-Krama practices. Preserved here as heritage / source material. Not instructional.

The Guhyakālī cult is documented primarily in the *Guhyakālī Khaṇḍa* of the *Mahākāla Saṃhitā* — a Śākta-Kālī-Krama Tantra of substantial length (the published Chaukhamba Surbharati Prakashan edition runs to two volumes and 917 pages, in Sanskrit text with Hindi translation, edited by Prof. Radheshyam Chaturvedi). The Internet Archive holds six digitised editions of the *Guhyakālī Khaṇḍa* — the Gopinath Allahabad Block edition (IGNCA 69227), the DLI/IGNCA 552568 edition, the Kishor Nath Jha Rashtriya Sanskrit Sanstha Part 2 edition, the Prof. Radheshyam Chaturvedi Parts 1 and 3 editions, and the Ganganath Jha Campus edition. This unusually dense Internet Archive coverage makes the *Mahākāla Saṃhitā* Guhyakālī Khaṇḍa one of the best-published primary sources among the Śākta-Kālī-Krama Tantras.

The Manblunder essay on *Śrī Guhyakālī Daśavaktra Dhyānam* ("The meditation of the mysterious Divine Mother Guhyakālī adorned with ten faces denoting human, animal and bird heads along with various weapons") cites the *Mahākāla Saṃhitā* directly for the dhāraṇā. The Hindu Online *Guhyakālī* page reproduces the *Mahākāla Saṃhitā*'s enumeration of the ten-faced form: "She has 27 eyes, with some faces having two, and other three, eyes. Each of her faces represents a different [animal, bird, or human aspect]." The *Mahākāla Saṃhitā* explicitly enumerates forms with one to one hundred faces, with different mantras corresponding to each form. The text tradition positions Guhyakālī as the most esoteric of the Kālī-Krama goddesses — the "Secret Kālī" invoked in the innermost sanctuary of the Śākta-Kālī-Krama.

Goudriaan and Gupta (1981, §IV on Śākta literature) catalogue the *Mahākāla Saṃhitā* among the substantial Śākta-Kālī-Krama Tantras of the medieval period, with substantial Guhyakālī and Mahākāla-Bhairava material. The textual tradition treats the *Mahākāla Saṃhitā* as a sister-text to the *Kālī Tantra* and the *Guhyakālī Tantra* (independent Tantras surviving through the *Mahākāla Saṃhitā* recension).

## II. Iconography (per sources)

According to the dhāraṇā preserved in the *Mahākāla Saṃhitā — Guhyakālī Khaṇḍa* and summarised in the Manblunder essay, Guhyakālī manifests in multiple forms, with one, two, three, five, ten, twenty, thirty, thirty-six, sixty, and one hundred faces — each form with its corresponding mantra, dhāraṇā, and method. The principal *daśavaktra* (ten-faced) form is described as having 27 eyes (some faces having two eyes, others three) and 54 arms, with the ten faces comprising a combination of human, animal, and bird faces — including a white lion, a jackal, a monkey, a bear, and an elephant (per the Manblunder summary). She is dark-blue or black in complexion, with a garland of freshly severed human heads, ornamented with the emblems of the Kālī-Krama, and seated on Śiva in his inert (*śava*) form within a blazing cremation-ground.

The Manblunder summary of the *Mahākālī Saṃhitā* dhāraṇā records: "You assume the extraordinary form of 10 faces, 27 eyes and 54 hands, in order to rule this universe. You are continually looking left and right, [your faces] denoting human, animal and bird heads along with various weapons." The Hindu Online *Guhyakālī* page reproduces the *Mahākāla Saṃhitā*'s framing that "each of her faces represents a different [manifestation]." The fifty-head garland is documented across the broader Kālī-Krama iconographic tradition as representing the fifty letters of the Sanskrit alphabet — the *mātṛkā*-garland that encodes the totality of phenomenal manifestation.

The Goddess Vidya blog (citing the *Mahākāla Saṃhitā*) notes that Guhyakālī is worshipped in eight different forms, with eight different mantras, worshipped by eight great seers — a structure that places Guhyakālī within the *aṣṭa-Kālī* frame of the Kālī-Krama. The "Goddess Vidya" Bengal entry on Guhyakālī records that "In Kali Yuga, the 16-lettered mantra for the ten-faced form of Guhya Kali [is the operative form], consisting of fifty-four hands" — a textual framing that positions the *daśavaktra* form as the kali-yuga-operative form of the goddess.

## III. Mantra & Method (as documented in sources)

> **Editorial note.** The mantra and method below are reproduced from published textual sources for scholarly preservation. This is documentation, not instruction.

- **Bīja mantra:** The mūla-mantra is documented in the *Mahākāla Saṃhitā — Guhyakālī Khaṇḍa* (multiple Internet Archive editions). The Goddess Vidya blog records that "the 16-lettered mantra for the ten-faced form of Guhya Kali" is the kali-yuga-operative form. The exact form is dīkṣā-restricted in the published editions; the Śākta-Kālī-Krama tradition treats the Guhyakālī mūla-mantra as among the most closely guarded of the Kālī-Krama mantras. The publicly attested bīja-frame incorporates the *krīṃ* / *hūṃ* / *hrīṃ* phonemes prefixed to the goddess's name-bīja, with the *svāhā* termination. The per-recension exact form is not reproduced here.
- **Mantra count:** Paddhati literature documents puraścaraṇa counts in multiples of 100,000; the *Mahākāla Saṃhitā* records structural counts for each of the principal forms.
- **Duration:** Puraścaraṇa cycles of 41 / 45 / 108 days are documented in the *Mahākāla Saṃhitā*.
- **Direction facing:** South or South-west, per Kālī-Krama paddhati convention.
- **Mālā:** *Rudrākṣa* or *kapāla-asthi* (skull-bone) mālā; the textual witnesses vary.

The procedure documented in the *Mahākāla Saṃhitā — Guhyakālī Khaṇḍa* opens with the standard Kālī-Krama nyāsa sequence (kara-nyāsa, aṅga-nyāsa, ṣaḍaṅga-nyāsa, dig-bandhana, *mātṛkā-nyāsa*, *kālī-nyāsa*), followed by the dhāraṇā of the chosen form (daśavaktra / ekavaktra / śatavaktra etc.), japa of the mūla-mantra on the prescribed mālā, and homa of black sesame (*kṛṣṇa-tila*), mustard seeds (*sarṣapa*), and the *pañca-makāra* offerings into a triangular kuṇḍa in the cremation ground. The daśavaktra form's dhāraṇā is the most extensively documented in the *Mahākāla Saṃhitā* and the Manblunder summary.

The textual tradition positions Guhyakālī as the *guhya* (secret) form of Kālī — the goddess invoked in the innermost sanctuary of the Śākta-Kālī-Krama after the practitioner has stabilised in the *prakata* (manifest) forms of Kālī (Dakṣiṇā Kālī, Tārā, Bhadrakālī, etc.). White (2003) situates the broader Kālī-Krama within the Śākta-Kaula antinomian frame, in which the "secret" goddess is the one whose practice presupposes the practitioner's full internalisation of the antinomian stance.

## IV. Yantra (if attested)

A yantra is described in the *Mahākāla Saṃhitā — Guhyakālī Khaṇḍa*. The daśavaktra form's yantra consists of a square *bhūpura* with four gates, enclosing a hundred-petalled lotus (or an eight-petalled lotus, per recension), which in turn encloses multiple concentric downward-pointing triangles housing the Guhyakālī bīja at the centre and the bījas of her ten faces and 54 arms at the periphery. The triangles are the *yoni-mudrā* orientation, consistent with the Kālī-Krama framing. The yantra is described as inscribed on a human-skull plate (*nara-kapāla-paṭa*), on *bhūrja* (birch-bark), or on black cloth using a paste of *bhasma*, *kuṅkuma*, *gorocanā*, and *kākajaṅghā*-powder. Specific bīja placements are documented in the *Mahākāla Saṃhitā* and are not reproduced in detail here.

## V. Warnings and Contraindications (per sources)

- The *Mahākāla Saṃhitā* paddhati literature and the Śākta-Kālī-Krama tradition explicitly restrict the Guhyakālī mūla-mantra to practitioners who have received Guhyakālī dīkṣā in an authorised Kālī-Krama lineage; the textual tradition is uniform on this point.
- The Manblunder essay and the Goddess Vidya blog document the textual caution that the *guhya* (secret) framing of Guhyakālī means that her practice presupposes the practitioner's prior stabilisation in the *prakata* (manifest) forms of Kālī — particularly Dakṣiṇā Kālī, Tārā, and Bhadrakālī. Without that prior stabilisation, the practice of the multi-faced Guhyakālī forms is described in the *Mahākāla Saṃhitā* as psychologically and ritually destabilising.
- The *Mahākāla Saṃhitā* records the cremation-ground setting as the structural frame of the practice; the antinomian *pañca-makāra* offerings and the *śmaśāna-caryā* observance are documented as standard features of the practice.
- The daśavaktra form's dhāraṇā is documented in the Manblunder summary as the kali-yuga-operative form of Guhyakālī — i.e., the form most strongly recommended for the present age; the textual tradition cautions that this form, although operative for kali-yuga, is the most intense of the Guhyakālī forms and requires the most rigorous preparation.
- The Guhyakālī tradition must be distinguished from the standard Dakṣiṇā Kālī tradition; the textual sources, ritual settings, iconographic features, and mantric frames are distinct.
