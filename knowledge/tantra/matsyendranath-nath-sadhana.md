# Matsyendranāth / Nāth Sādhana

> The Nāth Siddha tradition attributed to Matsyendranāth (c. 9th–10th c.) — the founder of the Kaula-Yoginī lineage that evolved into the Nāth Sampradāya under his disciple Gorakhnāth. Attested in the *Kaulajñānanirṇaya* (attributed to Matsyendranāth himself), the *Matsyendra Saṃhitā*, the *Akulavīra Tantra*, and the *Jñānasaṅkalpa Tantra*. The path of the yogic-astral-body, the Kuṇḍalinī-awakening, the mantra-and-yantra practice of the Kaula-Yoginī transmission. High caution — an advanced Śākta-Kaula-Yogic rite. Preserved here as heritage / source material. Not instructional.

The Nāth Siddha tradition attributed to Matsyendranāth (also called Macchanda, Mīnapa, Mīnanāth — c. 9th–10th c. CE) is the foundational Kaula-Yogic lineage from which the Nāth Sampradāya later emerged under his disciple Gorakhnāth. The *Kaulajñānanirṇaya* — attributed to Matsyendranāth himself — is the foundational text of the Kaula-Yoginī tradition, surviving in multiple Internet Archive digitisations: the Prabodh Chandra Bagchi Calcutta Sanskrit Series edition (DLI 2015.280214), the 1934 *Kaulajñānanirṇaya and other minor texts of the school of Matsyendranātha: texts from Nepal* (DLI ministry 15875, the Bagchi-edited Nepal-manuscript corpus), the Shyamakant Dwivedi Anand edition, and the Calcutta Sanskrit Series digitisation (uploaded by Yogendra Sharma). The *Matsyendra Saṃhitā* Part 1 (Internet Archive, 2015.32122) is a broader Matsyendranāth-corpus text. Chapter 3 of the *Kaulajñānanirṇaya* is translated into English on the Shivashakti Mandalam site and on the Yogendranath Yogi blog — the translation describes the text as "an ancient text attributed to Siddha Matsyendranāth, said to be the founder of the Natha lineage."

The *Kaulajñānanirṇaya* is structured as a dialogue between Bhairava (Śiva) and the Yoginīs, mediated by Matsyendranāth as the human recipient of the teaching; the text codifies the Kaula doctrines of the *piṭha* (sacred seat), the *cakra* (the yogic body-centres), the *yoginī* (the female cosmic-powers), the *bīja*-mantra, the *mahāmelāpa* (the great Kaula-assembly), and the *kuladharma* (the Kaula-code). The *Akulavīra Tantra* — a Kaula text attributed variously to Matsyendranāth and to the Kashmir-Śaiva Kṣemarāja-commentarial tradition — codifies the doctrine of the *akula* (the transcendental Śiva-principle) and the *kula* (the immanent Śakti-principle); the Kashmir-Śaiva *Swacchanda Tantra* with Kṣemarāja's commentary (Internet Archive, two editions) preserves the parallel Kashmir-Śaiva recension of the same doctrinal complex. The *Jñānasaṅkalpa Tantra* — a Nāth-Kaula text cited in the Kaula-digest literature — codifies the Nāth sādhana of the *jñāna-saṅkalpa* (the will-of-knowledge).

White (2003: ch. 2–4) provides the definitive scholarly treatment of Matsyendranāth and the Kaula-Yoginī tradition; White (1996, *The Alchemical Body*) treats the Nāth alchemical-siddha tradition that developed from Matsyendranāth through Gorakhnāth. Mallinson (2007, *The Khecarīvidyā of Adinātha*) treats the Nāth-Hatha-yoga textual corpus; Mallinson & Singleton (2017, *Roots of Yoga*, Internet Archive stream) is the standard scholarly source-book of the Nāth-Hatha-yoga tradition. Shashibhusan Dasgupta's *Obscure Religious Cults* (1946, 1962) is the foundational Indic-scholarly treatment; Kalyani Mallik's *Nath Sampradaya* (1954) and G. W. Briggs's *Gorakhnath and the Kanphata Yogis* (1938) are the earlier standard works. The International Nath Order's "Shri Matsyendranath: A Nath Founder Pioneer" page provides the modern emic-Nāth framing.

## II. Iconography (per sources)

The iconography of Matsyendranāth himself — as attested in the sculptural and hagiographical corpus — is the iconography of the fish-belly-born Siddha: Matsyendra ("Lord of Fishes") is depicted as a yogin seated in a yogic posture, two-armed, sometimes with a fish or with the lower body of a fish (referencing the legend of his birth from the belly of a fish, where he overheard Śiva's teaching of the Kaula-doctrine to Pārvatī). The Nāth sculptural tradition (especially the Nepalese Matsyendranāth tradition, where he is identified with the Buddhist Padmapāni-Lokanātha and the Newar-Buddhist Bungadyaḥ) preserves a complex iconographic fusion; the Hindu Nāth tradition preserves the simpler iconography of the Siddha-with-staff, matted-hair, ash-covered, and sometimes wearing the *kuṇḍala* (ear-ring, the marker of the Kanphata-"split-eared" Nāth ascetic).

The iconography of the Nāth ascetic himself — as described in the *Kaulajñānanirṇaya* and in the *Gorakṣa Śatakaṃ* of Gorakhnāth — is the iconography of the yogic-astral-body: the practitioner visualises the *cakra*-system (the *mūlādhāra, svādhiṣṭhāna, maṇipūra, anāhata, viśuddha, ājñā, sahasrāra* — the seven bodily centres), the *nāḍī*-system (the *iḍā, piṅgalā, suṣumṇā* — the three bodily channels), the *prāṇa*-system (the five vital breaths), and the Kuṇḍalinī-śakti coiled at the base of the spine. The Nāth-Hatha-yoga textual corpus (the *Gorakṣa Śatakaṃ*, the *Hatha Yoga Pradīpikā*, the *Gheraṇḍa Saṃhitā*) codifies the physical postures (*āsana*), the breath-practices (*prāṇāyāma*), the muscular-locks (*bandha*), the seals (*mudrā*), and the cleansing-practices (*ṣaṭkarma*) by which the practitioner purifies the bodily substrate for the Kuṇḍalinī-awakening.

The Kaula-Yoginī iconography — as attested in the *Kaulajñānanirṇaya* and treated by White (2003: ch. 3) — is the iconography of the female cosmic-powers who are the practitioner's partners in the Kaula-rite. The *Kaulajñānanirṇaya* describes the *yoginī* as a six-or-eight-armed female figure, often holding the severed head and the drinking-vessel, mounted upon an animal-vehicle (*vāhana*), associated with one of the eight directions of the Kaula-maṇḍala. White (2003) emphasises that the Kaula-Yoginī is not merely an iconographic figure but the structural counterpart to the practitioner in the Kaula-rite: the Kuṇḍalinī-śakti within the practitioner's bodily-circuit is the inner-Kaula-Yoginī; the female-partner in the outer Kaula-rite is the outer-Kaula-Yoginī.

## III. Mantra & Method (as documented in sources)

> **Editorial note.** The mantra and method below are reproduced from published textual sources for scholarly preservation. This is documentation, not instruction.

- **Bīja mantra:** The Kaula-Nāth sādhana is not structured around a single mūla-mantra in the way the Śākta Mahāvidyā practices are. The *Kaulajñānanirṇaya* documents a complex of *bīja*-mantras: the *hrīṃ* (the māyā-bīja), *krīṃ* (the Kālī-bīja), *hūṃ* (the fire/weapon-bīja), *aiṃ* (the speech-bīja), and the *Sauḥ* (the tri-bīja of the Kaula-Kashmir-Śaiva tradition). The *Kaulajñānanirṇaya* chapter 3 documents the *liṅga*-characteristics (the *liṅga-lakṣaṇa* — the marks of the inner-Śiva-linga that the practitioner visualises) and the corresponding *bīja*-mantras for each of the yogic body-centres. The specific per-lineage form is dīkṣā-restricted; the publicly attested form is the structural frame of the *bīja*-sequence placed upon the *cakra*-system.
- **Mantra count:** The *Kaulajñānanirṇaya* documents the *japa* of the *bīja*-mantras at the *cakra*-centres, with counts varying by lineage. The Nāth-Hatha-yoga corpus (*Gorakṣa Śatakaṃ*, *Hatha Yoga Pradīpikā*) documents the *prāṇāyāma*-counts (the 4:16:32 ratios of *pūraka, kumbhaka, recaka* — inhalation, retention, exhalation) that structure the Nāth-practice.
- **Duration:** The Nāth-Kaula sādhana is a lifetime-vow; the *Kaulajñānanirṇaya* documents the staged progression through the *bīja*-visualisation, the *cakra*-activation, the Kuṇḍalinī-awakening, and the *mahāmelāpa* (the great Kaula-assembly, dīkṣā-restricted).
- **Direction facing:** East or north, per Nāth-Hatha-yoga convention.
- **Mālā:** *Rudrākṣa* (the principal Nāth-ascetic mālā, often of 108 beads with a large central bead); the *kuṇḍala* (the ear-ring) is the marker of the Kanphata-Nāth dīkṣā.

The procedure documented in the *Kaulajñānanirṇaya* opens with the Kaula preliminaries — the *guru-pūjā*, the *guru-yoga*, the *bīja*-nyāsa upon the bodily-centres, the *cakra*-bhāvanā (visualisation of the *cakra*-system), and the *prāṇāyāma* (the breath-practices that purify the *nāḍī*-system). The *Kaulajñānanirṇaya* chapter 3 (English translation by Yogendranath Yogi and Shivashakti Mandalam) describes the *liṅga*-characteristics — the visualisation of the inner-Śiva-liṅga at the *mūlādhāra-cakra*, the activation of the Kuṇḍalinī-śakti through *bīja*-mantra-japa and *prāṇāyāma*, the ascent of the Kuṇḍalinī through the *cakra*-system, and the union of the Kuṇḍalinī-śakti with the Śiva-bīja at the *sahasrāra-cakra*. The *mahāmelāpa* — the great Kaula-assembly — is the climactic rite of the Kaula-practice, dīkṣā-restricted and not documented in detail in publicly available editions.

The Nāth-Hatha-yoga corpus (*Gorakṣa Śatakaṃ*, *Hatha Yoga Pradīpikā*) documents the *ṣaṭkarma* (the six purificatory practices: *dhauti, basti, neti, trāṭaka, nauli, kapālabhāti*), the *āsana* (the physical postures), the *prāṇāyāma* (the breath-practices), the *mudrā* (the seals, including *khecarī-mudrā* — the tongue-lock, central to the Nāth-practice), the *bandha* (the muscular-locks: *mūla-bandha, jālandhara-bandha, uḍḍiyāna-bandha*), and the *nādānusandhāna* (the contemplation of the inner-sound). White (1996, *The Alchemical Body*) treats the parallel Nāth-alchemical-practice — the *rasāyana* (the alchemical-rejuvenation) and the *dehāvedha* (the bodily-transference) — that complements the Hatha-yoga practice. Mallinson (2007) treats the *Khecarīvidyā* as the principal Nāth-text on the *khecarī-mudrā*. The Mallinson & Singleton *Roots of Yoga* (2017, Internet Archive stream) is the standard scholarly source-book of the Nāth-Hatha-yoga corpus.

## IV. Yantra (if attested)

A *cakra*-maṇḍala is described in the *Kaulajñānanirṇaya* (chapters 4–6) and in the Kashmir-Śaiva *Swacchanda Tantra* with Kṣemarāja's commentary (Internet Archive, two editions). The Kaula-Nāth *cakra*-maṇḍala is the *yoginī-cakra* — the circular arrangement of the eight (or sixty-four, or eighty-one) Yoginīs around the central Bhairava-Bhairavī pair. The *Kaulajñānanirṇaya* documents the eight-direction *yoginī-cakra*: the four cardinal directions house the four principal Yoginīs (associated with the *bījas* *aiṃ, hrīṃ, śrīṃ, klīṃ*); the four intermediate directions house the four secondary Yoginīs; the centre houses the Bhairava-Bhairavī pair in union. The yantra is described as inscribed on gold-plate, copper-plate, or on *bhūrja* (birch-bark) using a paste of *kuṅkuma*, *gorocanā*, and *rakta-candana*; the practitioner visualises the yantra internally upon the *cakra*-system of the bodily-circuit. The outer Kaula-rite — the *mahāmelāpa* — is performed in a constructed circular-maṇḍala-space, dīkṣā-restricted and not documented in detail in publicly available editions. Specific bīja placements are documented in the *Kaulajñānanirṇaya* paddhati and in the *Swacchanda Tantra* with the Kṣemarāja commentary.

## V. Warnings and Contraindications (per sources)

- The *Kaulajñānanirṇaya* explicitly restricts the Kaula-Nāth sādhana to practitioners who have received Kaula-dīkṣā in an authorised Nāth-lineage; the textual tradition is uniform on this point. The *mahāmelāpa* — the climactic Kaula-rite — is dīkṣā-restricted and not documented in detail in publicly available editions.
- White (2003: ch. 4) and Mallinson (2007) document the Nāth-Kaula textual caution that the Kuṇḍalinī-awakening — the central practice of the Nāth-sādhana — is potentially destabilising if undertaken without prior purification of the *nāḍī*-system through the *ṣaṭkarma* and *prāṇāyāma* preliminaries. The *Hatha Yoga Pradīpikā* (1.14–16) explicitly warns that the practitioner who undertakes the Kuṇḍalinī-practice without the preliminary *mitāhāra* (moderate diet) and *yama* (ethical restraints) is destroyed by the practice.
- The *Kaulajñānanirṇaya* and the Kashmir-Śaiva *Swacchanda Tantra* with Kṣemarāja's commentary warn that the Kaula-Yoginī practice — the outer-Kaula-rite with the female partner — is restricted to the *vīra*-class practitioner (the heroic, dīkṣā-restricted practitioner); the *paśu*-class practitioner (the bound, conventional householder) is forbidden from the practice and is directed to the *pratinidhi* (substitution) practice of the inner-Kaula-visualisation alone.
- The Nāth-Kaula commentarial literature (the *Kaulajñānanirṇaya* itself; the *Swacchanda Tantra* with the Kṣemarāja commentary) warns that the *bīja*-mantras of the Kaula-tradition must be received through dīkṣā; incorrect *bīja*-permutation or incorrect *cakra*-placement produces *pratyavāya* (spiritual counter-effect).
- The Nāth-Kaula sādhana must be distinguished from the modern postural-yoga practice (which derives from the Nāth-Hatha-yoga textual corpus but is not itself a Nāth-sādhana); the textual tradition is uniform that the Nāth-sādhana is a comprehensive Kaula-Yogic path of which the Hatha-yoga is only the preliminary-purification stage. Mallinson & Singleton (2017) document the historical transition from the Nāth-Kaula sādhana to the modern postural-yoga practice.
- The Matsyendranāth-Kaula tradition must be distinguished from the Buddhist Sahajayāna and Vajrayāna traditions that developed in parallel (and partially in dialogue with) the Nāth-Kaula tradition; White (2003: ch. 5) treats the Buddhist-Hindu Kaula dialogue. The Hindu Nāth-Kaula sādhana is the specific historical path documented in the *Kaulajñānanirṇaya*, the *Matsyendra Saṃhitā*, and the Kashmir-Śaiva *Swacchanda Tantra* with Kṣemarāja's commentary.
