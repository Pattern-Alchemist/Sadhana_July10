# Chinnamastā Sādhana

> The third of the Daśa Mahāvidyās, Chinnamastā ("she of the severed head") is iconographically depicted as self-decapitated, holding her own severed head while three streams of blood feed her two attendants (Ḍākinī and Varṇinī) and her own mouth. High caution — an advanced Mahāvidyā rite whose iconography encodes a deliberate reversal of the polarity of life and death. Preserved here as heritage / source material. Not instructional.

---

## I. Textual Attestation

- **Primary text:** *Chinnamastā Tantra* (independent Tantra); *Śāradā Tilaka Tantra* (ch. 7, which gives the dhāraṇā of Chinnamastā among the Mahāvidyās); *Prāṇatoṣiṇī Tantra* (commentarial digest of Śāradā Tilaka).
- **Secondary text(s):** *Śākta Praamoda*; *Śakti Saṅgama Tantra* — *Chinnamastā Khaṇḍa* (Hindi ed. Sudhakar Malaviya & Chittaranjan Malaviya, Krishnadas Prachya Vidya Series 34); Buddhist parallel in the *Chinnamundā Vajravārāhī* sādhana cycle.

Chinnamastā is documented as the third Mahāvidyā in the canonical lists of the *Todala Tantra* and the *Śāradā Tilaka*. Her textual home in the Hindu tradition is the *Chinnamastā Tantra* — an independent Śākta Tantra that survives primarily through the recension embedded in the *Śāradā Tilaka* (chapter 7) and through the extensive commentary of Rāmtoṣaṇa Bhaṭṭācārya's *Prāṇatoṣiṇī* (a 16th-17th-century commentary on the Śāradā Tilaka, here using both the 1859 Herambachandra Bandyopadhyay Calcutta edition and the 1898 Jivananda Vidyasagara edition, both digitised on the Internet Archive). The iconography of the self-decapitated goddess feeding herself and her two attendants Ḍākinī and Varṇinī from her own neck-veins is codified in the dhāraṇā of *Śāradā Tilaka* 7.

Benard's 1994 monograph *Chinnamastā: The Aweful Buddhist and Hindu Tantric Goddess* remains the standard scholarly treatment of the textual and iconographic history, and demonstrates that the Hindu Chinnamastā tradition and the Buddhist Chinnamundā tradition are closely related, with textual cross-pollination occurring in the late first-millennium Pāla-era Tantric milieu. Benard traces the earliest documented iconography to the 9th-century and the textual codification to the 10th–12th centuries. White (2003) places the rite within the broader Śākta framework of life-force (*prāṇa*) manipulation, while Shaw (1994) discusses the parallel Buddhist Vajrayoginī-Chinnamundā sādhana that is structurally cognate. Goudriaan and Gupta (1981) note the textual interconnection between the *Chinnamastā Tantra* and the Śāradā Tilaka's seventh chapter.

The Śakti Saṅgama Tantra's *Chinnamastā Khaṇḍa* (Malaviya ed., digitised on the Internet Archive) is a 20th-century compilation that brings together paddhati material from multiple south-Indian Śākta sources, and is one of the few printed primary sources giving the full dhāraṇā, mūla-mantra, yantra, and homa procedure of Chinnamastā in a single document.

## II. Mantra & Method (as documented in sources)

> **Editorial note.** The mantra and method below are reproduced from published textual sources for scholarly preservation. This is documentation, not instruction.

- **Bīja mantra:** The mūla-mantra is documented in *Śāradā Tilaka* ch. 7 with Rāmtoṣaṇa Bhaṭṭācārya's commentary in the *Prāṇatoṣiṇī*. Its exact form varies across textual lineages. The mantra incorporates the *hūṃ* and *kṣ* phonemes characteristic of ugra-devatā bījas, with the *vajravairocanī* / *śrīṃ* bīja-sequence prefixed to the goddess's name-bīja. The specific formulation is not reproduced here, as per-text variants differ.
- **Mantra count:** Paddhati literature documents puraścaraṇa counts in multiples of 100,000. The Śakti Saṅgama Tantra's Chinnamastā Khaṇḍa records a structural puraścaraṇa count; specific numbers are not reproduced here.
- **Duration:** Varies; puraścaraṇa cycles of 40 / 45 days are documented in the Śakti Saṅgama Tantra.
- **Direction facing:** South or West, per paddhati convention (Chinnamastā's directional association in the Śāradā Tilaka paddhati is south or south-west, consistent with her *ugra* classification).
- **Mālā:** Rudrākṣa or red-sandwood (rakta-candana); textual witnesses vary.

The dhāraṇā of *Śāradā Tilaka* 7 (with the *Prāṇatoṣiṇī* commentary) describes the goddess as nude (*digambarī*), dark or red-complexioned, standing on the copulating couple Kāmadeva and Ratī, holding her own severed head in her left hand and a scissors/kartarī in her right, with three streams of blood issuing from her neck — one feeding her own mouth and the other two feeding her attendants Ḍākinī (on the left) and Varṇinī (on the right). The dhāraṇā is followed by mūla-mantra japa and a homa of red flowers, *kuṅkuma*, and *tilā* into a triangular kuṇḍa. Benard (1994) gives a full English rendering of the dhāraṇā with textual notes on the variants between the Hindu and Buddhist traditions.

White (2003, pp. 222–230) reads the iconography as a yogic-circulatory diagram: the ascending blood-stream from the *mūlādhāra* (Kāma-Rati below) to the *sahasrāra* (the severed head above) and back to the central *nāḍī* as the practitioner's own life-force feeding the three *nāḍīs* (iḍā, piṅgalā, suṣumṇā) represented by the three streams. The Prāṇatoṣiṇī commentary explicitly glosses the three streams as *iḍā*, *piṅgalā*, and *suṣumṇā*, confirming the yogic-anatomical reading. The Śakti Saṅgama Tantra's Chinnamastā Khaṇḍa documents the full paddhati — nyāsa, dhāraṇā, japa, homa, tarpaṇa — and is the most accessible single-volume primary source for the rite.

## III. Yantra (if attested)

A triangular yantra inscribed within a square bhūpura, with a central six-petalled lotus enclosing the goddess's bīja, is described in the Śakti Saṅgama Tantra's Chinnamastā Khaṇḍa and in the Prāṇatoṣiṇī commentary on Śāradā Tilaka 7. The triangle is oriented apex-downward (the *yoni-mudrā* orientation), and the three streams of blood are represented by three bījas placed at the three vertices of the triangle. The yantra is traditionally drawn on red silk or birch-bark (*bhūrja*) using a paste of *kuṅkuma* and *gorocanā*. Specific bīja placements are documented in the Śakti Saṅgama Tantra and are not reproduced in detail here.

## IV. Warnings and Contraindications (per sources)

- The Prāṇatoṣiṇī commentary on Śāradā Tilaka 7 explicitly restricts the mūla-mantra to practitioners who have received Chinnamastā dīkṣā; paddhati literature is uniform on this point.
- Benard (1994, pp. 121–135) discusses the historical cautions attaching to the practice: the goddess is *ugra* and the iconography encodes a deliberate inversion of the polarity of life and death, which the textual tradition interprets as requiring extraordinary steadiness from the practitioner.
- White (2003) notes that Chinnamastā's association with the *prāṇa*-agni-kundalinī circuit places her among the most advanced Mahāvidyā practices; the textual tradition treats her as a goddess of *nidrā* and of the *tāmasika* śakti at the sahasrāra, requiring prior accomplishment in gentler Mahāvidyā practice.
- The Śakti Saṅgama Tantra records dietary and purity observances (red foods, abstention from meat and alcohol, red clothing for the duration of the puraścaraṇa) as prerequisites.
- The Buddhist parallel — Chinnamundā Vajravārāhī — is documented by Shaw (1994) and by Benard (1994) as requiring high tantric empowerment in the Cakrasaṃvara or Vajravārāhī cycle; the Hindu and Buddhist traditions historically cross-fertilised, and paddhati literature notes that the practice should not be undertaken without clear lineage instruction.
