# Smāśāna Bhairavī Sādhana

> The Śaiva-Śākta cremation-ground Bhairavī — the goddess of the *smasāna* (cremation ground) textual strand attested in the *Brahmayāmala Tantra* (Picumata), the *Jayadrathayāmala*, and the *Śāradā Tilaka*. She is the Bhairavī of the early Śaiva Mantramārga's Vidyāpīṭha — the cremation-ground goddess of the Kāpālika-Bhairava cultic complex. High caution — an antinomian Śaiva-Śākta rite of the cremation-ground textual tradition. Preserved here as heritage / source material. Not instructional.

The Smāśāna Bhairavī is the Bhairavī form attested in the earliest stratum of the Śaiva Mantramārga's Vidyāpīṭha — the cremation-ground Śākta-Śaiva textual tradition documented in the *Brahmayāmala Tantra* (Picumata) and the *Jayadrathayāmala*. Hatley's 2007 PhD thesis *The Brahmayāmala Tantra and the Early Śaiva Cult of Yoginīs* (University of Pennsylvania, full PDF available at blog.umamaya.com) remains the standard scholarly treatment of the *Brahmayāmala*, and Hatley's subsequent critical edition (*The Brahmayāmalatantra or Picumata*, vol. 1, IPA, 2018) gives the published text of chapters 1–2, 39–40, and 83 — the chapters most centrally documenting the cremation-ground ritual frame. The Cardiff ALT article by Csaba Kiss ("Horror and Wonder in a Putrid Trench: The Brahmayāmala's Rite of the Pit of Power [*siddhigarttāyāga*]") situates the *Brahmayāmala*'s cremation-ground rite within the wider Vidyāpīṭha antinomian frame and notes that the *Brahmayāmala* is "one of the earliest surviving goddess-oriented (i.e. Śākta) tantras of the Śaiva Mantramārga, positioning itself within the Vidyāpīṭha."

Sanderson's foundational 1988 essay "Śaivism and the Tantric Traditions" (PDF available via Angkor Database) traces the Śaiva-Mantramārga structure within which the Bhairavī-cremation-ground cult is positioned. Sanderson documents the Kāpālika background of the early Bhairavī-Bhairava cultic complex: "The Śiva forms of both are visualised as skull-bearing denizens of the cremation grounds... Worshipped within an enclosure of cremation grounds they themselves wear the [Kāpālika emblems]." The Brill 2020 Festschrift *Śaivism and the Tantric Traditions: Essays in Honour of Alexis G.J.S. Sanderson* (ed. Goodall, Hatley, Isaacson, Raman; digitised on Internet Archive) collects the most recent scholarship on the early Śaiva-Śākta textual tradition within which Smāśāna Bhairavī is attested. Hatley's essay "Dressing for Power: On *vrata*, *caryā*, and *vidyāvrata* in the Early Śaiva Tantras" documents the cremation-ground observance (*śmaśāna-caryā*) explicitly: "[If] one wanders in the cremation-ground at night, with a skull in one's hand and a *khaṭvāṅga*, covered in ashes, that is called the cremation-ground observance (*śmaśāna-caryā*)."

The IndiaFacts essay "Dasha Mahavidyas III: Bhairavi" (citing *Śākta Pramoda*) describes the textual Bhairavī as "fond of meat, alcohol and the cremation grounds, where she hunts for consuming the flesh [of the dead]" — the Śākta-Praamoda description is the late-medieval digest witness to the earlier Brahmayāmala / Jayadrathayāmala cremation-ground frame. The Śāradā Tilaka's Bhairavī chapter gives the paddhati-form recension that bridges the early Śaiva Mantramārga frame and the late-medieval Mahāvidyā frame.

## II. Iconography (per sources)

According to the Brahmayāmala textual tradition (as documented by Hatley 2007, 2018) and the *Jayadrathayāmala*, the Smāśāna Bhairavī is described as a cremation-ground Bhairavī — nude or skull-clad (*kapāla-mālā*), ash-bedecked (*bhasma-dhāriṇī*), seated on a corpse (*śavāsana*) or standing in the burning-ground, ornamented with the emblems of the Kāpālika-Bhairava cultic complex: the *kapāla* (skull-bowl), the *khaṭvāṅga* (skull-topped staff), the *muṇḍa-mālā* (garland of severed heads), the *damaru* (skull-drum), and the *śūla* (trident). She is the Bhairavī of the cremation ground — the goddess who dwells where the dead are burnt.

Sanderson (1988) documents the wider Kāpālika iconographic frame within which the Smāśāna Bhairavī is positioned: the Bhairava-Bhairavī couple are "conventionally beautiful but holding such Kāpālika emblems as [the skull-staff and the skull-bowl]" and are "worshipped within an enclosure of cremation grounds." The iconographic structure encodes the antinomian stance of the early Śaiva Mantramārga Vidyāpīṭha: the goddess is beautiful, but she bears the emblems of the cremation ground; she is benign in form, but she is seated on a corpse. The Bheswamata Mandir entry on Bhairavī, citing the Śākta Praamoda tradition, gives the late-medieval Mahāvidyā iconography: "She is shown sitting in the cremation ground on top of a headless corpse. She has four arms in which she holds a sword, a *triśūla*, and the demon's severed head."

The textual tradition distinguishes the Smāśāna Bhairavī from the sixth Mahāvidyā Bhairavī (the latter being the fierce form of Tripurasundarī, treated separately in this archive as the Tripura-Bhairavī entry). The Smāśāna Bhairavī belongs to the earlier Śaiva Mantramārga textual tradition; her iconography is the cremation-ground Bhairavī of the *Brahmayāmala* / *Jayadrathayāmala* / Kāpālika strand. The later Mahāvidyā-Bhairavī of the *Śākta Praamoda* inherits this cremation-ground frame but reorients it within the Mahāvidyā cycle.

## III. Mantra & Method (as documented in sources)

> **Editorial note.** The mantra and method below are reproduced from published textual sources for scholarly preservation. This is documentation, not instruction.

- **Bīja mantra:** The mūla-mantra is documented in the *Brahmayāmala Tantra* (Hatley 2018 critical edition, chapters 39–40) and the *Jayadrathayāmala*. The exact form is dīkṣā-restricted in the published editions; the Vidyāpīṭha tradition treats the Bhairavī mūla-mantra as the most closely guarded of the early Śaiva Mantramārga mantras. The publicly attested bīja-frame incorporates the *hūṃ* / *hrīṃ* phonemes prefixed to the goddess's name-bīja, with the *phaṭ* termination. The specific mūla-mantra is not reproduced here.
- **Mantra count:** Paddhati literature documents puraścaraṇa counts in multiples of 100,000; the *Brahmayāmala* records structural counts for the Vidyāpīṭha-Bhairavī practice.
- **Duration:** Puraścaraṇa cycles of the *śmaśāna-caryā* observance are documented in Hatley ("Dressing for Power") as the duration of the cremation-ground residence.
- **Direction facing:** South or South-west (the *nairṛtya* quarter, associated with Nirṛti, dissolution, and the cremation ground), per Vidyāpīṭha convention.
- **Mālā:** *Rudrākṣa* or *kapāla-asthi* (skull-bone) mālā; the textual witnesses vary.

The procedure documented in the *Brahmayāmala* (Hatley 2018 edition) opens with the standard Vidyāpīṭha nyāsa sequence followed by the dhāraṇā of the Smāśāna Bhairavī in the cremation-ground setting, japa of the mūla-mantra, and offerings (*bali*) of meat (*maṃsa*), alcohol (*madya*), blood (*rudhira*), and the *pañca-makāra* into a triangular *bali-pīṭha* raised in the cremation ground. Hatley's "Dressing for Power" essay documents the *śmaśāna-caryā* observance: the practitioner "wanders in the cremation-ground at night, with a skull in one's hand and a *khaṭvāṅga*, covered in ashes." The Cardiff ALT article by Csaba Kiss documents the *siddhigarttāyāga* — the "Rite of the Pit of Power" — performed in a pit dug in the cremation ground.

Sanderson (1988) situates the Smāśāna Bhairavī practice within the Kāpālika-Bhairava cultic complex, in which the practitioner undertakes the *śmaśāna-caryā* observance as a structural feature of the practice, not merely as an optional setting. The Śāradā Tilaka's Bhairavī chapter gives the late-medieval Mahāvidyā recension that preserves the cremation-ground frame within the digest paddhati.

## IV. Yantra (if attested)

A yantra is described in the *Brahmayāmala* (Hatley 2018 edition, chapters 39–40) and in the *Jayadrathayāmala*. It consists of a square *bhūpura* with four gates, enclosing an eight-petalled lotus, which in turn encloses a downward-pointing triangle (*adho-mukha-trikoṇa*) housing the Bhairavī bīja at the centre and the bījas of the eight Bhairavī-mātṛkās at the cardinal and inter-cardinal points. The triangle is the *yoni-mudrā* orientation, consistent with the Bhairavī framing. The yantra is described as inscribed on a human skull (*nara-kapāla*), on *bhūrja* (birch-bark), or on the cremation-ground ash using a paste of *bhasma*, *kuṅkuma*, and *gorocanā*. Specific bīja placements are documented in the *Brahmayāmala* critical edition and are not reproduced in detail here.

## V. Warnings and Contraindications (per sources)

- The *Brahmayāmala* paddhati literature and the Vidyāpīṭha tradition explicitly restrict the Smāśāna Bhairavī mūla-mantra to practitioners who have received Śaiva-Mantramārga dīkṣā in an authorised Bhairavī-Bhairava lineage; the textual tradition is uniform on this point.
- Hatley (2007, 2018) documents the textual caution that the *śmaśāna-caryā* observance — the cremation-ground residence that is the structural setting of the practice — is itself an antinomian act that requires the practitioner to have completed the Svacchanda-Bhairava and Manthānabhairava preliminaries of the Śaiva Mantramārga. Without those preliminaries, the cremation-ground residence is described in the *Brahmayāmala* as psychologically and ritually destabilising.
- Sanderson (1988) notes that the Kāpālika-Bhairava cultic complex within which the Smāśāna Bhairavī is positioned is the antinomian core of the early Śaiva Mantramārga, and that its practice requires the internalised antinomian stance of the *vīra-sādhaka* — the stance in which the polarity of pure/impure, life/death, fortune/misfortune is dissolved prior to the cremation-ground residence.
- The Cardiff ALT article by Csaba Kiss documents the *siddhigarttāyāga* as the most intense of the *Brahmayāmala*'s cremation-ground rites, and notes the textual cautions attaching to its undertaking.
- The Smāśāna Bhairavī tradition must be distinguished from the Mahāvidyā-Bhairavī (Tripura-Bhairavī) tradition; the textual sources, ritual settings, and iconographic features are distinct.
