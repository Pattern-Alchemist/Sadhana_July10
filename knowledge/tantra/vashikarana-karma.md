# Vaśīkaraṇa Karma

> The second of the six ṣaṭ-karma — the subjugation (*vaśīkaraṇa*) rite for bringing a person, force, or circumstance under the practitioner's influence. High caution — an *ugra*-category ṣaṭ-karma that acts on another's will. Preserved here as heritage / source material. Not instructional.

---

The *Agni Purāṇa* ch. 138 (Wisdom Library translation) catalogues *vaśīkaraṇa* as the second of the *ṣaṭ-karma*, immediately following śānti. The *Prapañcasāra Tantra* (Avalon 1919 edition) preserves the operative *vaśīkaraṇa* paddhati within its mantra-śāstra frame; Shastra Deep's essay on *Prapañcasāra* confirms the text's explicit enumeration of all six ṣaṭ-karma with vaśīkaraṇa named as the second. The Wisdom Library "Satkarman" definition page records that the *Ṣaṭsāhasrasaṃhitā* (an expansion of the *Kubjikāmatatantra*) and the 13th-century *Rasaratnākara* of Nityanātha Siddha both catalogue the same six operations.

The *Mahānirvāṇa Tantra* Chapter VIII — preserved in the Avalon/Woodroffe English translation (1913, Luzac; multiple archive.org digitisations and the Aghori.it full-text mirror) — explicitly prescribes the *kalāśa* (consecrated pot) material for each ṣaṭ-karma: a crystal (*sphaṭika*) kalāśa for *vaśīkaraṇa*, a stone (*pāṣāṇa*) kalāśa for *stambhana*, a clay (*mṛt*) kalāśa for all-purposes including śānti. This kalāśa-specification is one of the most cited single-text witnesses of the operational distinction among the six ṣaṭ-karma and is reproduced in Woodroffe's *Shakti and Shâkta* (1929).

The *Śāradā Tilaka Tantram* (Lakṣmaṇa Deśikendra, c. 10th–11th c., with the *Padārthādarśa* of Rāghavabhaṭṭa) preserves the vaśīkaraṇa-mantra formulations within the text's wider pūjā-prakaraṇa. Four Internet Archive digitisations are available: the Sudhakar Malaviya edition (the standard modern Varanasi printing), the Avalon 1933 translation (Tantric Texts Series 16), the Mukunda Jha Bakshi Series No. 107 Kashi Sanskrit Series edition, and the Chowkhamba Padārthādarśa edition. The *Rudrayāmala Tantra* is cited as the principal Śākta-yāmala source for specific vaśīkaraṇa-mantras in the Śrī Vidyā paddhati literature and in the Scribd-preserved "Super Natural Power Therapy" compilation, which reproduces a Rudrayāmala vaśīkaraṇa-mantra attributed to Rudra (the Rudra-avatāra form of Śiva).

Sanderson (2009, "The Śaiva Age") documents the *ṣaṭ-karma* framework — with vaśīkaraṇa as the paradigmatic *ugra*-rite — as a defining feature of the Mantramārga Śaiva-Śākta corpus from the 7th–8th c. onward. White (2003, *Kiss of the Yoginī*) discusses the *ṣaṭ-karma* within the broader frame of the Kāpālika-Kaula ritual matrix from which the medieval Śaiva-Śākta ṣaṭ-karma textual tradition emerges.

## II. The Category (per sources)

Vaśīkaraṇa-karma is the second of the six ṣaṭ-karma and the first of the five *ugra* (fierce) operations. The etymology is from *vaśa* ("under one's control") + *karaṇa* ("the act of making"); the rite acts on another person's will (*svecchā*), bringing the target under the practitioner's influence. The Hinduism Stack Exchange summary of the *ṣaṭ-karma* framework — drawn from the *Mantra Mahodadhi* and the *Mantra Mahārṇava* paddhati tradition — catalogues vaśīkaraṇa as distinct from *mohana* (infatuation), *ākarṣaṇa* (attraction), and *uccāṭana* (driving away): where *mohana* clouds the target's judgement temporarily and *ākarṣaṇa* draws them physically toward the practitioner, *vaśīkaraṇa* produces a durable subjugation of the will.

The *Mahānirvāṇa Tantra* Ch. VIII distinguishes vaśīkaraṇa from the other ṣaṭ-karma by the kalāśa-material prescription: *sphaṭika* (crystal), the clear luminous medium, is the signature material of vaśīkaraṇa — in contrast to the *pāṣāṇa* (stone) of stambhana, the *mṛt* (clay) of all-purposes-and-śānti, and other prescriptions for the remaining ṣaṭ-karma. The kalāśa-specification operates as a ritual signature: the crystal-clear luminosity of the sphaṭika-medium signals the rite's intent to "clarify" the target's will into alignment with the practitioner's.

Woodroffe (1929, *Shakti and Shâkta*) frames vaśīkaraṇa within the broader category of *abhicāra* — "magical process with intent to destroy or injure... Hiṃsā-karma, or act injurious to others" — but distinguishes the subjugation-rite from the more destructive ṣaṭ-karma (vidveṣaṇa, uccāṭana, māraṇa) by noting that vaśīkaraṇa acts on the will rather than the body, the relationship, or the life of the target. The Shastra Deep essay on *Prapañcasāra* confirms that the *Prapañcasāra Tantra* enumerates vaśīkaraṇa as the second of the six, immediately after śānti and immediately before stambhana — positioning it as the entry-point of the *ugra*-series. The textual tradition consistently frames vaśīkaraṇa as the paradigmatic "influence"-rite: a transitive operation on the target's autonomy, distinct from the field-restoration of śānti and the motion-arrest of stambhana.

## III. Mantra & Method (as documented in sources)

> **Editorial note.** The mantra-forms and counts below are documented from published textual sources for scholarly preservation. This is documentation, not instruction.

- **Bīja mantra:** The *Agni Purāṇa* ch. 138 frame attaches the ṣaṭ-karma operations to "all Mantras" — the practitioner's own dīkṣā-mantra, with the vaśīkaraṇa operation specified by add-on bījas and the *vaśīkaraṇa-nyāsa*. The specific *vaśīkaraṇa-bīja* cited in the *Śāradā Tilaka* paddhati and in the *Mantra Mahodadhi* of Mahīdhara is dīkṣā-restricted in published editions; the per-recension exact form is not reproduced here. The Hinduism Stack Exchange summary of the *ṣaṭ-karma* framework notes that the Śaiva-Śākta paddhati tradition typically uses the *klīṁ* bīja as the generic *vaśya*-bīja, but the operative combination varies by recension.
- **Mantra count:** The *Prapañcasāra* paddhati and the *Śāradā Tilaka* commentarial tradition document puraścaraṇa counts in multiples of 100,000; the *Mantra Mahodadhi* records the vaśīkaraṇa-specific count.
- **Duration:** Cycles of 7, 21, or 41 days are documented in the Śāradā Tilaka paddhati.
- **Direction facing:** East or North per paddhati convention; the *Mahānirvāṇa Tantra* Ch. VIII specifies the kalāśa placement for vaśīkaraṇa with eastward orientation.
- **Kalāśa material:** *Sphaṭika* (crystal) per *Mahānirvāṇa Tantra* Ch. VIII — the canonical single-text witness for the vaśīkaraṇa-rite's signature material.

The procedure documented in the *Agni Purāṇa* ch. 138 (per Wisdom Library's translation) frames vaśīkaraṇa as the second ṣaṭ-karma operation, performed after the standard *ṣaḍaṅga-nyāsa*, *dig-bandhana*, and *guru-pūjā* preliminaries. The practitioner establishes the crystal *kalāśa* (per *Mahānirvāṇa Tantra* Ch. VIII), performs *japa* of the operative mantra with the vaśīkaraṇa-bīja prefixed, and conducts a *homa* with *sweet* offerings (ghee, *gur*-sweetened *pāyasam*, white flowers, *kapittha* or *madhuka* fruit). The *Śāradā Tilaka* paddhati documents the application of the *vaśīkaraṇa-nyāsa* on the practitioner's own body prior to the operation.

The *Rudrayāmala Tantra* — cited in the Scribd-preserved "Super Natural Power Therapy" compilation — preserves a specific Rudra-avatāra vaśīkaraṇa-mantra (the "Rudra Vaśīkaraṇa" formulation) recorded as requiring ten thousand recitations for siddhi. The *Mantra Mahodadhi* of Mahīdhara (17th c.) reproduces multiple vaśīkaraṇa-formulations from the *Rudrayāmala* and other yāmala sources. The per-recension exact forms are dīkṣā-restricted in the published editions.

## IV. Yantra (if attested)

A vaśīkaraṇa-specific yantra is documented in the *Mantra Mahodadhi* of Mahīdhara and in the *Rudrayāmala Tantra* paddhati tradition: a square *bhūpura* with four cardinal gates, an eight-petalled lotus ring, and a central downward-pointing *tri-koṇa* (yoni-triangle) housing the vaśīkaraṇa-bīja at its bindu. The *Śāradā Tilaka* paddhati documents the application of this yantra inscribed on *bhūrja* (birch-bark) or *tāmra-paṭa* (copper-plate) using a paste of *kuṅkuma*, *gorocanā*, and *sphaṭika-bhasma* (crystal-ash, consistent with the *Mahānirvāṇa Tantra* kalāśa-signature). The target's name is inscribed within the *tri-koṇa* around the bīja. Specific bīja placements are dīkṣā-restricted in the published editions and are not reproduced here.

## V. Warnings and Contraindications (per sources)

- The *Śāradā Tilaka* paddhati, the *Prapañcasāra* commentary of Padmapāda, and the *Mantra Mahodadhi* of Mahīdhara all restrict the operative vaśīkaraṇa-mantra to practitioners who have received the relevant dīkṣā in an authorised lineage; the textual tradition is uniform on this point.
- Woodroffe (1929) classifies vaśīkaraṇa within the broader *abhicāra* category — "the Karma described in the Tantras and Atharvaveda... magical process with intent to destroy or injure" — and warns that the *ugra*-ṣaṭ-karma (vaśīkaraṇa, stambhana, vidveṣaṇa, uccāṭana, māraṇa) are subject to the *kāmya*-rite cautions of *Mahānirvāṇa Tantra* Ch. VIII, which restricts their performance to those who have completed the Kaula-dīkṣā and observe the *pañca-tattva* frame.
- Sanderson (2009, "The Śaiva Age") documents the Śaiva-Śākta textual tradition's caution that ṣaṭ-karma performed without correct *mantra-siddhi* (the prior puraścaraṇa-completion of the practitioner's dīkṣā-mantra) rebounds on the practitioner: the textual witnesses describe this as *mantra-vyāghāta* (mantra-collision), in which the ungrounded practitioner's own vital force is seized by the operation.
- The *Rudrayāmala* vaśīkaraṇa-mantra tradition, as preserved in the *Mantra Mahodadhi*, includes explicit *pratibandha*-clauses (binding-clauses) that specify the rite's reversal-conditions: if performed with wrong intention, against an inappropriate target (one's guru, a brāhmaṇa, a kinsman), or without the prescribed observances, the rite is documented to invert — binding the practitioner rather than the target.
- The Śaiva-Śākta commentarial tradition distinguishes vaśīkaraṇa-karma from the modern commercial "vaśīkaraṇa-mantra" literature (a 20th-c. popular genre, exemplified by Baal Kadmon's *Vashikaran Magick* on Amazon); the textual-tradition operation is a *mantra-śāstra* rite requiring prior dīkṣā, not a self-help procedure.
