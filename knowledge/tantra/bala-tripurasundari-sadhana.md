# Bālā Tripurasundarī Sādhana (Bālā Pañcadaśākṣarī)

> The entry-level upāsanā of the Śrī Vidyā tradition, addressed to Bālā Tripurasundarī ("the nine-year-old form" of Lalitā) using the fifteen-syllable *pañcadaśākṣarī* mantra in its Bālā-form. High caution — a Śrī Vidyā dīkṣā-gated mantra; the entry-point of the broader Śrī Vidyā curriculum but nonetheless a vidyā of the Śākta-Mahāvidyā class. Preserved here as heritage / source material. Not instructional.

Bālā Tripurasundarī is the entry-level form of Lalitā Tripurasundarī in the Śrī Vidyā tradition. The textual core is the *Vāmakeśvara Tantra* (also called the *Nityāṣoḍaśikārṇava*), the root Tantra of the Śrī Vidyā school, which Padoux (1990) and Brooks (1990) place in the 9th–11th century Śākta-Śaiva milieu. The *Vāmakeśvara Tantra* codifies the *pañcadaśākṣarī* (fifteen-syllable) mūla-mantra in its three forms — Kādi, Hādi, and Sādi — with the Kādi-vidyā being the dominant recension. The Bālā-form of the pañcadaśākṣarī is the first dīkṣā given in the Śrī Vidyā curriculum, and is documented in the *Paraśurāma Kalpasūtra* (the sūtra-text of the tradition) and in the commentarial tradition associated with the *Bhāvanā Upaniṣad*.

The *Bhāvanā Upaniṣad* is a minor Upaniṣad of the Atharvaveda that encodes the Śrī Cakra (the Śrī Yantra) as the body of the practitioner — each *cakra* of the yantra is mapped to a corresponding limb or *kalā* of the practitioner's body. S. K. Ramachandra Rao's *Tantra of Śrī Cakra* (Satguru Publications) gives a full English translation of the Upaniṣad with Śrī Vidyā commentary, and is digitised on the Internet Archive. The *Paraśurāma Kalpasūtra* (edited with Hindi commentary in Hariom Shukla Shastri's *Śrī Vidyā Sadhana*, vol. 1, Uttar Pradesh Sanskrit Sansthan) is the sūtra-form codification of the Śrī Vidyā practice; its ten khaṇḍas and 337 sūtras cover the full Śrī Vidyā curriculum from Bālā through Pañcadaśī to Ṣoḍaśī, with detailed instructions on the Śrī Cakra pūjā, nyāsa, homa, and the structure of the *mahāpuraścaraṇa*.

Padoux (2017) and Brooks (1990) note that the Śrī Vidyā tradition is the most systematic of the Śākta-Mantramārga lineages, with a tightly bounded dīkṣā-paramparā that limits open transmission of the pañcadaśākṣarī. The Bālā-form is, however, the entry-point and is the only Śrī Vidyā vidyā whose existence is broadly known in the smārta-Śākta world outside the formal lineage.

## II. Mantra & Method (as documented in sources)

> **Editorial note.** The mantra and method below are reproduced from published textual sources for scholarly preservation. This is documentation, not instruction.

- **Bīja mantra:** The Bālā-pañcadaśākṣarī is documented in the *Vāmakeśvara Tantra*, the *Paraśurāma Kalpasūtra*, and the commentarial tradition of the *Bhāvanā Upaniṣad*. Its exact form varies across the Kādi, Hādi, and Sādi recensions, and within each recension across the Bālā, Pañcadaśī, and Ṣoḍaśī levels. The specific syllable-sequences are not reproduced here, as the Śrī Vidyā tradition restricts open recitation of the pañcadaśākṣarī to dīkṣit practitioners and the published editions carry explicit instructions to that effect.
- **Mantra count:** Paddhati literature documents puraścaraṇa counts in multiples of 100,000. The Paraśurāma Kalpasūtra records a structural puraścaraṇa count for the Bālā level (distinct from the Pañcadaśī and Ṣoḍaśī counts); specific numbers are not reproduced here.
- **Duration:** Varies; the Bālā level is structurally an introductory discipline and paddhati literature records shorter puraścaraṇa cycles (typically 41 days) than the higher levels of the curriculum.
- **Direction facing:** East, per Śrī Vidyā convention; the Vāmakeśvara paddhati is explicit on this point.
- **Mālā:** Sphaṭika (rock crystal), per Śrī Vidyā convention; the Vāmakeśvara and Paraśurāma Kalpasūtra paddhatis also permit rudrākṣa or gold.

The procedure documented in the *Paraśurāma Kalpasūtra* (with Hariom Shukla Shastri's Hindi commentary) opens with the standard Śrī Vidyā nyāsa sequence — guru-pādukā, ṣaṭ-cakra-nyāsa, mātṛkā-nyāsa, and the Śrī Cakra-bhāvana of the *Bhāvanā Upaniṣad* — followed by the dhāraṇā of Bālā as a nine-year-old girl (kumārī), red-silk-clad (*aruna-paṭṭa-vastrā*), holding in her four hands the *puśpabāṇa* (flower-arrow), the *āṅkuśa* (goad), the *pāśa* (noose), and the *sugarcane-bow* (ikṣu-cāpa). The dhāraṇā is followed by the Bālā-pañcadaśākṣarī japa and a homa of ghee, *tilā*, and red flowers into a triangular kuṇḍa. The *Bhāvanā Upaniṣad* specifies the body-yantra mapping that is the underlying soteriology of the practice — each of the nine āvaraṇas of the Śrī Cakra is mapped to a corresponding anatomical-energetic layer of the practitioner, so that the japa is performed as the self-recognition of the body as the Śrī Cakra.

Padoux (1990, 2017) frames the Śrī Vidyā practice as the most elaborated of the Śākta "word"-centred (*vāc*) traditions, in which the mantra's bīja-structure is itself a cosmological diagram. Brooks (1990) gives a full anthropological-scholarly account of the contemporary Śrī Vidyā paramparā and its dīkṣā-gating of the pañcadaśākṣarī. S. K. Ramachandra Rao's translation of the *Bhāvanā Upaniṣad* supplies the direct textual witness for the body-yantra soteriology.

## III. Yantra (if attested)

The Śrī Cakra (Śrī Yantra) — the canonical nine-āvaraṇa intersecting-triangle yantra of the Śrī Vidyā tradition — is the primary yantra of Bālā Tripurasundarī sādhana, as documented in the *Vāmakeśvara Tantra*, the *Bhāvanā Upaniṣad*, and the *Paraśurāma Kalpasūtra*. The Śrī Cakra consists of a central *bindu* surrounded by a *tri-koṇa* (downward-pointing triangle), then *aṣṭa-dala* (eight-petalled lotus), *ṣoḍaśa-dala* (sixteen-petalled lotus), three concentric rings of intersecting triangles (the *tri-mekhala* and the *vāsu-koṇa* / *vṛtta*), and finally a three-line *bhūpura*. The Bālā-form may also be worshipped with a simplified *bālā-yantra* of a six-petalled lotus within a square *bhūpura*; both forms are documented in the Paraśurāma Kalpasūtra paddhati. The Śrī Cakra is traditionally drawn on gold, copper, or birch-bark using a paste of *kuṅkuma*, *candanā*, and *gorocanā*. Bīja placements within the nine āvaraṇas are documented in the *Bhāvanā Upaniṣad* and are not reproduced here.

## IV. Warnings and Contraindications (per sources)

- The *Vāmakeśvara Tantra*, the *Paraśurāma Kalpasūtra*, and the entire Śrī Vidyā commentarial tradition (Saubhāgyabhāskara, *Kāmakalāvilāsa* commentary, etc.) uniformly restrict the pañcadaśākṣarī — including its Bālā-form — to practitioners who have received Śrī Vidyā dīkṣā in a recognized paramparā. This restriction is explicit in the published editions (including Hariom Shukla Shastri's *Śrī Vidyā Sadhana*).
- Padoux (1990, 2017) and Brooks (1990) note that the Śrī Vidyā tradition treats the pañcadaśākṣarī as a *vidyā* in the strict technical sense — i.e., a mantra whose efficacy depends on correct transmission — and the commentarial tradition records that "incorrect" recitation (without dīkṣā) is ritually counter-productive.
- The Bālā level is the entry-point of the curriculum precisely because it is the gentlest; paddhati literature nonetheless records purity observances (vegetarian diet, abstention from alcohol, white or red clothing) as prerequisites.
- The *Bhāvanā Upaniṣad* specifies the body-yantra mapping as the central soteriological mechanism; the commentarial tradition notes that this mapping requires prior training in the standard Śaiva-Śākta nyāsas (mātṛkā, ṣaḍadhva) for its correct realisation.
- The Bālā level is structurally preparatory for the Pañcadaśī and Ṣoḍaśī levels; paddhati literature cautions against skipping directly to higher levels without first accomplishing the Bālā puraścaraṇa.
