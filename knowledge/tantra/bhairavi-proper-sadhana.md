# Bhairavī (Proper) Sādhana

> The fifth of the Daśa Mahāvidyās in the canonical Śākta list, Bhairavī (proper) is the Goddess as the teacher (*guru-rūpiṇī*) — distinct from Tripura-Bhairavī (the sixth Mahāvidyā, the fierce form of Tripurasundarī, covered in *tripura-bhairavi-sadhana.md*). Bhairavī proper is the Goddess of *tapas* (austerity) in her universal cosmic form, holding a *mālā* (rosary) and a *pustaka* (book), seated on a corpse or on a lotus. Attested in the *Bhairavī Tantra* (paired with the *Dhūmāvatī Tantra* in the Pt. Rajesh Dīkṣit edition), the *Rudrayāmala Tantra*, the *Śāradā Tilaka*, and the *Śākta Praamoda*. High caution — an advanced Mahāvidyā rite. Preserved here as heritage / source material. Not instructional.

Bhairavī is the fifth Mahāvidyā in the canonical lists of the *Todala Tantra*, *Śāradā Tilaka*, *Mahābhāgavata Purāṇa*, and the *Śākta Praamoda*; she is the Goddess as the female counterpart (*śakti*) of Bhairava — the terrible, transformative, teaching form of Śiva. The *Bhairavī Tantra* survives in modern printed form through Pt. Rajesh Dīkṣit's paired *Bhairavī evam Dhūmāvatī Tantra Śāstra* edition (Internet Archive, direct PDF) — the printed Sanskrit/Hindi text gives the dhyāna-śloka, mūla-mantra, yantra, homa, and puraścaraṇa procedure. The *Rudrayāmala Tantra* (Internet Archive, Sanskrit digitisation) — one of the principal Yamala-class Śākta Tantras — preserves the broader Bhairavī material in its Mahāvidyā sections, alongside the *Tripura-Bhairavī Pañcāṅgam* (separately digitised on Internet Archive) which belongs to the cognate but distinct Tripura-Bhairavī tradition.

The *Śāradā Tilaka Tantra* (Malaviya ed.; Avalon 1933 ed.) preserves the Bhairavī chapter within its ten-Mahāvidyā framework; the Chowkhamba *Padārthādarśa* commentary gives the principal Sanskrit commentarial treatment. The *Śākta Praamoda* of Rājadevanandana (the standard 19th-century Śākta digest, with the Devanandan Singh Bahadur recension) preserves the Bhairavī paddhati within its ten-Mahāvidyā digest. The Internet Archive holds three digitised editions of the *Śākta Praamoda*. The *Daśa Mahāvidyā Tantra-Sāra* of Yogiraj Yashpal (Internet Archive) is a modern Hindi digest consolidating the Bhairavī paddhati from multiple sources.

Kinsley (1997: ch. on Bhairavī) frames her as the teacher-aspect of the Goddess — the *guru-rūpiṇī* whose rosary and book symbolise the *jñāna-śakti* by which the practitioner is taught. Kinsley distinguishes the canonical Bhairavī (fifth Mahāvidyā) from the Tripura-Bhairavī (sixth Mahāvidyā, the fierce form of Tripurasundarī — covered in *tripura-bhairavi-sadhana.md*): the canonical Bhairavī is the Goddess as universal teacher and cosmic-tapas, while the Tripura-Bhairavī is the Goddess as the *ugra*-face of the Śrī Vidyā tripura-sundarī. The two share the *bhairavī*-epithet but are distinct Mahāvidyās with distinct dhyāna-ślokas, mūla-mantras, and yantras. Goudriaan & Gupta (1981) catalogue the *Bhairavī Tantra* among the late-medieval independent Mahāvidyā Tantras. The *Indica Today* essay (drawing on the *Śākta Praamoda* and the *Śāradā Tilaka*) summarises the canonical Bhairavī iconography as "Goddess with the splendour of a thousand suns, three eyes, [holding the mālā and the pustaka]..."

## II. Iconography (per sources)

According to the dhyāna-śloka preserved in the *Bhairavī Tantra* (Dīkṣit ed.) and the *Śāradā Tilaka* (Malaviya ed., Bhairavī chapter), Bhairavī is described as having "the splendour of a thousand suns" — *sahasra-sūrya-sama-prabhā* — red-complexioned (*rakta-varṇā* or *aruṇā*) like the rising sun or like *kuṅkuma* or *rakta-candana*. She has three eyes (*tri-netrā*), four arms (*catur-bhujā*). Her upper right hand holds a *mālā* (rosary), her upper left hand holds a *pustaka* (book — the scripture of jñāna), her lower right hand makes the *varada-mudrā* (boon-granting gesture), and her lower left hand makes the *abhaya-mudrā* (fearlessness gesture). She is ornamented with red garments, a garland of *bandhūka* flowers, a crown of red gems, and a crescent moon upon her forehead. The *Indica Today* essay (citing the *Śākta Praamoda*) summarises the iconography in this form.

The commentarial tradition (summarised in the *Prāṇatoṣiṇī* and in S. K. Ramachandra Rao 1994) reads the mālā-and-book iconography as the *jñāna-śakti* of the Goddess — the rosary symbolising japa (the oral-aural transmission of vidyā), the book symbolising śāstra (the textual-scriptural transmission of vidyā). The textual tradition describes Bhairavī as seated either upon a lotus throne (the saumya-form) or upon a corpse (*śava* — the cremation-ground form), with the corpse being Sadāśiva in his quiescent state, awaiting the awakening of his śakti. The four-arms with the rosary-and-book iconography is the standard form of the *guru-rūpiṇī* Bhairavī.

Kinsley (1997: ch. on Bhairavī) emphasises that the canonical Bhairavī iconography is structurally close to the Tripura-Bhairavī iconography (both red, three-eyed, four-armed, holding rosary-and-book, with varada- and abhaya-mudrās), and that the textual distinction is doctrinal rather than iconographic: Tripura-Bhairavī is the Śrī Vidyā frame's *ugra*-face of Tripurasundarī, whereas the canonical Bhairavī is the Mahāvidyā-list's universal teacher-aspect. The two are to be distinguished by their mūla-mantras, yantras, and textual lineages (the Tripura-Bhairavī in the *Śāradā Tilaka* ch. 11 and the *Paraśurāma Kalpasūtra*; the canonical Bhairavī in the *Bhairavī Tantra* and the *Śāradā Tilaka* ch. on Bhairavī-proper). Kinsley further notes that Bhairavī's name — "the terrible, the formidable" — refers to her capacity to dissolve the practitioner's limited ego through the intensity of the vidyā-transmission, not to any fierce iconography per se.

## III. Mantra & Method (as documented in sources)

> **Editorial note.** The mantra and method below are reproduced from published textual sources for scholarly preservation. This is documentation, not instruction.

- **Bīja mantra:** The mūla-mantra of Bhairavī (proper) is documented in the *Bhairavī Tantra* (Dīkṣit ed.) and in the *Śāradā Tilaka* (Bhairavī chapter). The principal bīja is *hrīṃ* — the māyā-bīja, the seed-syllable of the Goddess's cosmic-teaching-power. The publicly attested structural form (per the *Śākta Praamoda* and the *Indica Today* essay, cross-referenced) is the *hrīṃ*-prefixed name-bīja with the *namaḥ* termination; the publicly attested bīja-frame incorporates the *aiṃ* / *hrīṃ* / *śrīṃ* phonemes — the Śākta triad of speech, māyā, and Lakṣmī-śakti — prefixed to the Goddess's name-bīja. The full mūla-mantra is dīkṣā-restricted in published editions; the per-recension exact form is not reproduced here.
- **Mantra count:** The *Śāradā Tilaka* paddhati documents puraścaraṇa counts of 100,000 / 300,000 / 1,000,000 japa-cycles, with corresponding homa at 1/10th. The *Bhairavī Tantra* (Dīkṣit ed.) records a structural count of 9 lakhs (900,000) for the Bhairavī puraścaraṇa.
- **Duration:** Puraścaraṇa cycles of 21 / 41 / 45 days are documented in the *Śāradā Tilaka* paddhati and in the *Bhairavī Tantra*.
- **Direction facing:** East, per Śākta paddhati convention; the *Bhairavī Tantra* also documents a south-facing variant for the *śmaśāna*-form.
- **Mālā:** *Rudrākṣa* or *sphaṭika* (rock-crystal); the *Śāradā Tilaka* paddhati specifies rudrākṣa for the Bhairavī japa (the mālā-and-book iconography being mirrored in the practitioner's own mālā).

The procedure documented in the *Bhairavī Tantra* (Dīkṣit ed.) opens with the standard Śākta preliminaries — *kara-nyāsa*, *aṅga-nyāsa*, *ṣaḍaṅga-nyāsa*, *dig-bandhana*, *bhūta-śuddhi*, *prāṇa-pratiṣṭhā* — followed by the *Bhairavī-dhyāna* in her saumya- or śava-seated form, japa of the mūla-mantra on the prescribed mālā, and homa of *ghee*, *trimadhura* (curd, milk, and honey), *kuṅkuma*-mixed ghee, and red flowers into a triangular or square kuṇḍa. The *Śāradā Tilaka* paddhati (with the *Prāṇatoṣiṇī* commentary) documents the *pañca-pūjā* and the *ṣoḍaśopacāra* variants. The *Daśa Mahāvidyā Tantra-Sāra* (Yogiraj Yashpal, Internet Archive) reproduces the full pūjā-paddhati.

Kinsley (1997) notes that the Bhairavī worship in living Śākta tradition is positioned as the Mahāvidyā of the *guru-śakti* — the Goddess invoked for the transmission of vidyā from guru to disciple. The textual frame treats her as the Goddess whose rosary is the practitioner's mālā and whose book is the practitioner's śāstra; she is the cosmic teacher whose presence is implicit in every act of vidyā-transmission. The *Indica Today* essay (citing the *Śākta Praamoda*) emphasises that "Bhairavī, the fifth Mahāvidyā, is the embodiment of divine ferocity, discipline, and raw spiritual intensity."

## IV. Yantra (if attested)

A yantra is described in the *Bhairavī Tantra* (Dīkṣit ed.) and in the *Śāradā Tilaka* (Bhairavī chapter). Bhairavī's yantra is structurally a Śākta-Mahāvidyā yantra: a *bhūpura* (square enclosure) of three concentric lines, enclosing an eight-petalled lotus (*aṣṭa-dala*), enclosing a downward-pointing triangle (*tri-koṇa*) housing the *hrīṃ*-bīja at the centre, with the *aiṃ*-bīja at the three corners and the *śrīṃ*-bīja at the mid-points of the three sides. The *śava* of Sadāśiva is visualised within the triangle beneath the Goddess. The yantra is described as inscribed on gold-plate, copper-plate (*tāmra-paṭa*), or on *bhūrja* (birch-bark) using a paste of *kuṅkuma*, *gorocanā*, and *rakta-candana*. Specific bīja placements are documented in the *Bhairavī Tantra* paddhati and in the *Śāradā Tilaka* with the *Prāṇatoṣiṇī* commentary.

## V. Warnings and Contraindications (per sources)

- The *Bhairavī Tantra* (Dīkṣit ed.) and the *Śāradā Tilaka* paddhati literature explicitly restrict the Bhairavī mūla-mantra to practitioners who have received Śākta dīkṣā in an authorised lineage; the textual tradition is uniform on this point.
- Kinsley (1997) and Goudriaan & Gupta (1981) document the Śākta textual caution that Bhairavī — as the *guru-śakti* herself — is to be worshipped only under the supervision of a *guru* who has himself completed the Bhairavī puraścaraṇa. The textual tradition warns that the vidyā-transmission that Bhairavī embodies cannot be appropriated without an actual guru-lineage.
- The *Bhairavī Tantra* records dietary and purity observances for the duration of the puraścaraṇa: red or white clothing, vegetarian diet, abstention from alcohol, celibacy, residence in a quiet hermitage or temple precinct (the saumya-form) or in a cremation-ground precinct (the śava-form, dīkṣā-restricted).
- The Śākta commentarial literature (Rāmtoṣaṇa Bhaṭṭācārya's *Prāṇatoṣiṇī*) warns that the Bhairavī sādhana must be carefully distinguished from the Tripura-Bhairavī sādhana (the sixth Mahāvidyā, covered in *tripura-bhairavi-sadhana.md*); the two Goddesses share the *bhairavī*-epithet but are distinct Mahāvidyās with distinct mūla-mantras, yantras, and textual lineages. Conflating them is described as producing *pratyavāya* (spiritual counter-effect).
- The Bhairavī sādhana is doctrinally subtle because the Goddess is the *guru-śakti*: the practitioner's relationship to her is the practitioner's relationship to the guru-śiṣya paramparā itself. The textual tradition warns against undertaking the Bhairavī sādhana without prior stabilisation in the foundational Śākta preliminaries and without an actual living guru.
- The canonical Bhairavī tradition must be distinguished from the *Vijñāna-Bhairava Tantra* (the Kaśmīra-Śaiva text of 112 dharāṇā-techniques, edited in the *Rudrayāmala*'s Guhya-Rahasya section, Internet Archive) — the latter is a Bhairava-Bhairavī dialogue but is not a Mahāvidyā-paddhati text.
