# Bhuvaneśvarī Sādhana

> The fourth of the Daśa Mahāvidyās, Bhuvaneśvarī ("Mistress of the World") is the Goddess as the sovereign of all existence — she whose body is the *bhuvana* (cosmos). Three-eyed, four-armed, holding the *pāśa* (noose) and the *aṅkuśa* (goad), she is the *māyā-śakti* by which the universe is woven. Attested in the *Śāradā Tilaka*, *Bhuvaneśvarī Tantra*, *Bhuvaneśvarī-Aṣṭakam* of the *Rudrayāmala*, and the *Devī Māhātmya*. High caution — an advanced Mahāvidyā rite of the Śākta corpus. Preserved here as heritage / source material. Not instructional.

Bhuvaneśvarī is the fourth Mahāvidyā in the canonical lists of the *Todala Tantra*, *Śāradā Tilaka*, and the *Mahābhāgavata Purāṇa*; she is the Goddess as *bhuvana-īśvarī* — the sovereign of all worlds, the māyā-śakti by which the cosmos is structured and sustained. The *Śāradā Tilaka Tantra* (ed. Sudhakar Malaviya, Internet Archive) preserves the Bhuvaneśvarī chapter giving the dhāraṇā, mūla-mantra, yantra, and homa procedure. The Avalon 1933 edition (Tantric Texts Series 16, Part 1) preserves the English translation of the opening chapters and the Sri Satguru 1988 multi-scholar translation gives the full text with commentary. The Chowkhamba *Padārthādarśa* edition preserves the principal Sanskrit commentary.

The *Bhuvaneśvarī Tantra* survives in modern printed form through Pt. Rajesh Dīkṣit's *Chhinnamastā evam Bhuvaneśvarī Tantra Śāstra* (Internet Archive) — a paired edition giving both Mahāvidyā-chapters in Sanskrit/Hindi with the dhyāna-ślokas, mūla-mantras, yantras, and pūjā-paddhatis. The *Bhuvaneśvarī-Aṣṭakam* of the *Rudrayāmala Tantra* (Internet Archive) is an eight-verse dhyāna-stotra that gives the canonical iconography: "O Bhuvaneśvarī, the supreme sovereign of all worlds, three-eyed, holding the noose and the goad, red as the rising sun, seated upon the five Brahmās." The *Devī Māhātmya* (Durgā Saptaśatī, c. 5th–6th c.) provides the vaidika-purāṇic antecedent in its framing of the Devī as *yā devī sarvabhūteṣu viśvamātṛ-rūpeṇa saṃsthitā* — "the Goddess who dwells in all beings in the form of the universal Mother" (Devī Māhātmya 11.6), the verse that the Śākta tradition reads as the proto-attestation of Bhuvaneśvarī.

Kinsley (1997: ch. on Bhuvaneśvarī) frames her as the cosmic-sovereign Mahāvidyā, the Goddess whose body is identified with the cosmos itself; he notes her iconographic proximity to Tripurasundarī (both saumya, both holding goad-and-noose, both red-complexioned) but distinguishes her on the basis of the textual emphasis on *bhuvana* (the cosmos as her body) rather than *sundarī* (beauty) or *lalitā* (playfulness). Goudriaan & Gupta (1981) catalogue the *Bhuvaneśvarī Tantra* among the late-medieval independent Mahāvidyā Tantras that survive mainly through the digests. S. K. Ramachandra Rao (1994, *Encyclopaedia of Indian Iconography*) documents the textual iconographic variants. Bisschop (2006) provides the early-Śaiva framing of the *bhuvana* doctrine (the Śaiva-Siddhānta list of 118 bhuvanas, kalās, and tattvas) that underlies the Bhuvaneśvarī iconography.

## II. Iconography (per sources)

According to the dhyāna-śloka preserved in *Śāradā Tilaka* (Malaviya ed., Bhuvaneśvarī chapter) and the *Bhuvaneśvarī-Aṣṭakam* of the *Rudrayāmala* (Internet Archive), Bhuvaneśvarī is described as red-complexioned (*aruṇa-varṇā* or *rakta-candana-saṅkāśā*), the colour of the rising sun or of *rakta-candana*. She has three eyes (*tri-netrā*), four arms (*catur-bhujā*), and is seated on a lotus throne. Her upper right hand holds the *aṅkuśa* (goad), her upper left hand holds the *pāśa* (noose), her lower right hand makes the *varada-mudrā* (boon-granting gesture), and her lower left hand makes the *abhaya-mudrā* (fearlessness gesture). She is ornamented with red garments, a garland of *bandhūka* or *mālatī* flowers, a crown of red gems, and a crescent moon upon her forehead. The *Bhuvaneśvarī-Aṣṭakam* describes her as seated upon the five Brahmās — the five primordial principles (*śiva, sadyojāta, vāmadeva, aghora, tatpuruṣa, īśāna* — the six limbed forms of Sadāśiva, of which the five Brahmās form the seat).

The *Prāṇatoṣiṇī* commentary of Rāmtoṣaṇa Bhaṭṭācārya on the *Śāradā Tilaka* reads the noose and the goad as the *māyā-śakti* by which the Goddess binds (*pāśa*) and guides (*aṅkuśa*) the *paśu* (the bound soul); the noose is the *avidyā-māyā* that produces the appearance of multiplicity, the goad is the *vidyā-māyā* by which the Goddess draws the practitioner back to the recognition of unity. The commentarial tradition (summarised in S. K. Ramachandra Rao 1994) reads her three eyes as the sun, moon, and fire — the three cosmic lights by which the universe is illuminated. The *Mahavidyā Catuṣṭayam* (Datia Swami collection, Internet Archive) reproduces the same dhyāna-śloka and adds a variant in which Bhuvaneśvarī holds a *padma* (lotus) and a *kamaṇḍalu* (water-pot) in place of the varada- and abhaya-mudrās.

Kinsley (1997: ch. on Bhuvaneśvarī) emphasises that the textual iconography is uniformly saumya (benign): unlike Kālī, Tārā, or Chinnamastā, Bhuvaneśvarī bears no weapon of destruction, no severed head, no skull-garland; her power is the power of cosmic ordering, not cosmic dissolution. Kinsley notes her iconographic kinship with Tripurasundarī (the third Mahāvidyā) and with Lakṣmī / Kamalā (the tenth Mahāvidyā) — all three are saumya, all three hold the goad-and-noose, all three are red-complexioned. The textual distinction is doctrinal: Tripurasundarī is the Goddess as beauty-consciousness, Bhuvaneśvarī is the Goddess as cosmic-sovereignty, Kamalā is the Goddess as prosperity-abundance.

## III. Mantra & Method (as documented in sources)

> **Editorial note.** The mantra and method below are reproduced from published textual sources for scholarly preservation. This is documentation, not instruction.

- **Bīja mantra:** The mūla-mantra of Bhuvaneśvarī is documented in *Śāradā Tilaka* (Bhuvaneśvarī chapter) and in the *Bhuvaneśvarī Tantra* (Dīkṣit ed.). The principal bīja is *hrīṃ* — the māyā-bīja, the seed-syllable of cosmic māyā. The publicly attested structural form is the *hrīṃ*-prefixed name-bīja with the *namaḥ* termination; the Manblunder archive and the Wisdom Library entries (cross-referenced) note that the simple bīja-form is "Oṃ Hrīṃ ŚrīṂ Bhuvaneśvarī svāhā" while the full mūla-mantra incorporates additional bījas and is dīkṣā-restricted in published editions. The per-recension exact form is not reproduced here.
- **Mantra count:** The *Śāradā Tilaka* paddhati documents puraścaraṇa counts of 100,000 / 300,000 / 1,000,000 japa-cycles, with corresponding homa at 1/10th. The Tantrasāra of Kṛṣṇānanda records a structural count of 16 lakhs (1,600,000) for the Bhuvaneśvarī puraścaraṇa.
- **Duration:** Puraścaraṇa cycles of 21 / 41 / 45 days are documented in the *Śāradā Tilaka* paddhati and in the *Tantradarśana evam Bhuvaneśvarī Sādhana* of Ashok Kumar Rakesh.
- **Direction facing:** East, per Śākta paddhati convention; the *Prāṇatoṣiṇī* also documents a north-facing variant for the *jñāna-sādhana* form.
- **Mālā:** *Sphaṭika* (rock-crystal) or *rudrākṣa*; the *Śāradā Tilaka* paddhati specifies sphaṭika for the saumya Bhuvaneśvarī form.

The procedure documented in *Śāradā Tilaka* (Bhuvaneśvarī chapter) opens with the standard Śākta preliminaries — *kara-nyāsa*, *aṅga-nyāsa*, *ṣaḍaṅga-nyāsa*, *dig-bandhana*, *bhūta-śuddhi*, *prāṇa-pratiṣṭhā* — followed by the *Bhuvaneśvarī-dhyāna* in the *bindu* of her yantra, japa of the mūla-mantra on the prescribed mālā, and homa of *ghee*, *trimadhura* (curd, milk, and honey), *kuṅkuma*-mixed ghee, and red flowers into a triangular or square kuṇḍa. The *Bhuvaneśvarī-Aṣṭakam* is recited as a dhyāna-stotra before the japa. The *Bhuvaneśvarī Tantra* (Dīkṣit ed.) documents the *pañca-pūjā* (the fivefold offering of *gandha, puṣpa, dhūpa, dīpa, naivedya*) and the *ṣoḍaśopacāra* (sixteenfold service) variants. The *Mahavidyā Catuṣṭayam* (Datia Swami collection) reproduces the full pūjā-paddhati.

Kinsley (1997) notes that the Bhuvaneśvarī worship in living Śākta tradition is positioned within the broader Mahāvidyā-pañcadaśī frame, and that the textual tradition treats her as the Goddess invoked for the cultivation of cosmic-vision (*viśva-darśana*) and the dissolution of the practitioner's identification with the limited bodily ego. The *Tantradarśana evam Bhuvaneśvarī Sādhana* of Ashok Kumar Rakesh (Richa Prakashan Vihar, Internet Archive) is a modern Hindi digest of the paddhati literature and gives the operational sequence in detail.

## IV. Yantra (if attested)

A yantra is described in *Śāradā Tilaka* (Bhuvaneśvarī chapter) and in the *Bhuvaneśvarī Tantra* (Dīkṣit ed.). Bhuvaneśvarī's yantra is structurally similar to the Tripurasundarī-Śrī Cakra but simpler: a *bhūpura* (square enclosure) of three concentric lines, enclosing an eight-petalled lotus (*aṣṭa-dala*), enclosing a downward-pointing triangle (*tri-koṇa*) housing the *hrīṃ*-bīja at the centre. The *hrīṃ*-bīja is the seed of cosmic māyā; the triangle is the *yoni-mūla* of the Goddess; the eight petals house the eight Bhuvaneśvarī-aṅga-devatās. The yantra is described as inscribed on gold-plate, copper-plate (*tāmra-paṭa*), or on *bhūrja* (birch-bark) using a paste of *kuṅkuma*, *gorocanā*, and *rakta-candana*. Specific bīja placements are documented in the *Śāradā Tilaka* paddhati and in the *Prāṇatoṣiṇī* commentary.

## V. Warnings and Contraindications (per sources)

- The *Śāradā Tilaka* paddhati literature and the *Bhuvaneśvarī Tantra* (Dīkṣit ed.) explicitly restrict the Bhuvaneśvarī mūla-mantra to practitioners who have received Śākta dīkṣā in an authorised lineage; the textual tradition is uniform on this point.
- Kinsley (1997) and Goudriaan & Gupta (1981) document the Śākta textual caution that Bhuvaneśvarī — although saumya in iconography — is the *māyā-śakti* herself, the cosmic power by which the universe is sustained; her worship is doctrinally demanding because the practitioner must confront the Goddess as the very structure of cosmic appearance, not merely as a personal deity. The *Prāṇatoṣiṇī* commentary warns that this confrontation can destabilise practitioners who have not stabilised in the foundational Śākta preliminaries.
- The *Bhuvaneśvarī Tantra* (Dīkṣit ed.) records dietary and purity observances for the duration of the puraścaraṇa: red or white clothing, vegetarian diet, abstention from alcohol, celibacy, residence in a quiet hermitage or temple precinct.
- The Śākta commentarial literature (Rāmtoṣaṇa Bhaṭṭācārya's *Prāṇatoṣiṇī*) warns that the noose-and-goad iconography is not merely symbolic: the Goddess's noose binds the *paśu* (bound soul) more tightly if the practitioner undertakes the sādhana with the wrong intention (i.e., for worldly power rather than for vidyā); the commentarial tradition treats the Bhuvaneśvarī sādhana as particularly susceptible to misappropriation because her saumya iconography can mislead the practitioner into treating her as a "safe" deity.
- The Hindu Bhuvaneśvarī tradition must be distinguished from the broader Tripurasundarī-Śrī Vidyā tradition; although the two Goddesses share iconographic features (goad-and-noose, red complexion, lotus throne), they are distinct Mahāvidyās with distinct mūla-mantras, yantras, and commentarial traditions.
