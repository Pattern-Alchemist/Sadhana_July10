# Vidveṣaṇa Karma

> The fourth of the six ṣaṭ-karma — the discord-generation (*vidveṣaṇa*) rite for creating enmity, division, or separation between parties (typically hostile parties, or parties allied against the practitioner). High caution — the first of the "fierce four" ṣaṭ-karma (vidveṣaṇa, uccāṭana, māraṇa, plus the optional mohana). Preserved here as heritage / source material. Not instructional.

The *Agni Purāṇa* ch. 138 (Wisdom Library translation) catalogues *vidveṣaṇa* as the fourth of the *ṣaṭ-karma*, immediately after stambhana. The *Prapañcasāra Tantra* (Avalon 1919 edition) preserves the operative *vidveṣaṇa* paddhati within its mantra-śāstra frame; Shastra Deep's essay on *Prapañcasāra* explicitly enumerates "vidveṣaṇa (causing enmity)" as the fourth of the six. The Wisdom Library "Satkarman" definition page records that the *Ṣaṭsāhasrasaṃhitā* and the *Rasaratnākara* both catalogue the same six operations in the same order.

The *Mahānirvāṇa Tantra* Chapter VIII (Avalon/Woodroffe English translation, Aghori.it and sacred-texts.com) frames vidveṣaṇa among the *kāmya* (desire-motivated) ṣaṭ-karma operations within the Kaula-dīkṣā frame. The Śaiva-Śākta commentarial tradition (Rāghavabhaṭṭa's *Padārthādarśa* on *Śāradā Tilaka*) documents vidveṣaṇa-mantra formulations within the text's wider pūjā-prakaraṇa. Sanderson (2009, "The Śaiva Age") documents the ṣaṭ-karma framework — with vidveṣaṇa as the fourth operation — as a defining feature of the Mantramārga Śaiva-Śākta corpus from the 7th–8th c. onward. The *Brahmayāmala* / *Picumata* (Hatley 2018 critical edition) preserves early attestations of the discord-operation within the Vidyāpīṭha textual corpus.

The *Sāmba Purāṇa* chapter on "Tantric Rituals, Mantra Application, and Ascetic Practices" (Vedapath.app translation) explicitly documents the "Abhicāra Vidhi: The Rituals of Destructive Magic" section, which describes fierce and destructive magical rituals including specialised fire-sacrifices (*homa*) meant to neutralise enemies — the textual frame within which vidveṣaṇa is positioned as the operative discord-rite. Woodroffe (1929, *Shakti and Shâkta*) classifies vidveṣaṇa within the broader *abhicāra* category — "the Karma described in the Tantras and Atharvaveda... magical process with intent to destroy or injure" — but distinguishes the discord-rite from māraṇa (death) by noting that vidveṣaṇa produces *separation* (between allies, between friends, between forces) rather than destruction of the target's life.

The Wisdom Library "Abhicāra" definition page (citing Vātsyāyana's *Kāmasūtra* commentary) frames the *abhicāra*-category operations as the broader ritual-class within which the ṣaṭ-karma are sub-classified; vidveṣaṇa is the discord-member of this sub-classification.

## II. The Category (per sources)

Vidveṣaṇa-karma is the fourth of the six ṣaṭ-karma and the third of the five *ugra* (fierce) operations. The etymology is from *vi-dveṣa* ("separation-enmity," "discord") + *karaṇa* ("the act of"); the rite creates enmity, division, or separation between parties. The Shastra Deep essay on *Prapañcasāra* confirms that the *Prapañcasāra Tantra* enumerates vidveṣaṇa as the fourth of the six, immediately after stambhana and immediately before uccāṭana — positioning vidveṣaṇa as the third of the *ugra*-series and the first of the "fierce four" (vidveṣaṇa, uccāṭana, māraṇa, plus the optional mohana).

The textual tradition consistently frames vidveṣaṇa as a *relationship-disruption* operation: where vaśīkaraṇa bends a single target's will and stambhana freezes a single target's capacity to act, vidveṣaṇa operates on the *bond* between two or more targets — dissolving alliance, friendship, or contract. The Śaiva-Śākta commentarial tradition (Rāghavabhaṭṭa on *Śāradā Tilaka*) documents the typical applications: discord between allied enemies of the practitioner (weakening their coalition), discord between a teacher and student (disrupting an opposing lineage's transmission), discord between business partners, or discord between spouses (the most-cited classical example). Woodroffe (1929) frames vidveṣaṇa within the broader *abhicāra* category but distinguishes the discord-rite from the more destructive ṣaṭ-karma (uccāṭana, māraṇa) by noting that vidveṣaṇa produces separation rather than physical destruction.

The *Brahmayāmala* / *Picumata* (Hatley 2018 critical edition, chapters 1–2, 39–40, 83) preserves early attestations of the vidveṣaṇa-operation within the Vidyāpīṭha Śaiva-Śākta textual corpus, providing the textual-archaeological evidence that vidveṣaṇa as a ṣaṭ-karma predates its systematisation in the medieval paddhati literature. The text documents vidveṣaṇa-formulations operating within the Bhairava-cakra and the yoginī-sādhana frame, in which the discord-rite is performed through the medium of the *Cakra* of the *Yoginīs* — a markedly earlier ritual ecology than the later Mantramārga paddhati form.

Sanderson (2009, "The Śaiva Age") and Sanderson ("Śaivism and the Tantric Traditions") document vidveṣaṇa as one of the standard *abhicāra*-operations performed by the Śaiva-Śākta *ācārya* on behalf of royal patrons from the early-medieval period — the rite of disrupting an enemy-king's alliance-coalition is one of the most cited applications in the epigraphic and textual record.

## III. Mantra & Method (as documented in sources)

> **Editorial note.** The mantra-forms and counts below are documented from published textual sources for scholarly preservation. This is documentation, not instruction.

- **Bīja mantra:** The *Agni Purāṇa* ch. 138 frame attaches the ṣaṭ-karma operations to "all Mantras" — the practitioner's own dīkṣā-mantra, with the vidveṣaṇa operation specified by add-on bījas and the *vidveṣaṇa-nyāsa*. The specific *vidveṣaṇa-bīja* cited in the *Śāradā Tilaka* paddhati and in the *Mantra Mahodadhi* of Mahīdhara is dīkṣā-restricted in published editions; the per-recension exact form is not reproduced here. The Śaiva-Śākta paddhati tradition typically uses *dveṣa*-seed-bījas incorporating the *kṣa* and *dṛ* phonemes, but the operative combination varies by recension.
- **Mantra count:** The *Prapañcasāra* paddhati and the *Śāradā Tilaka* commentarial tradition document puraścaraṇa counts in multiples of 100,000; the *Mantra Mahodadhi* records the vidveṣaṇa-specific count.
- **Duration:** Cycles of 7, 14, or 21 days are documented in the Śāradā Tilaka paddhati.
- **Direction facing:** South per paddhati convention for *ugra*-rites; the *Mahānirvāṇa Tantra* Ch. VIII specifies the kalāśa placement for vidveṣaṇa with southern orientation.
- **Kalāśa material:** The Śaiva-Śākta paddhati tradition records a *loha* (iron / mixed-metal) or *kāṅsya* (bell-metal) kalāśa for vidveṣaṇa — the dense, "split" alloy-medium being the ritual signature of the rite's intent to "split" the bond between the targets.

The procedure documented in the *Agni Purāṇa* ch. 138 (per Wisdom Library's translation) frames vidveṣaṇa as the fourth ṣaṭ-karma operation, performed after the standard *ṣaḍaṅga-nyāsa*, *dig-bandhana*, and *guru-pūjā* preliminaries. The practitioner establishes the prescribed *kalāśa*, performs *japa* of the operative mantra with the vidveṣaṇa-bīja prefixed, and conducts a *homa* with *bitter / sour / sharp* offerings (mustard-oil, *nimba* leaves, sour *tamarind*, salt, *trivṛt* root). The *Śāradā Tilaka* paddhati documents the application of the *vidveṣaṇa-nyāsa* on the practitioner's own body prior to the operation, and the inscription of *both* targets' names within the yantra's central *tri-koṇa* — a distinctive requirement of vidveṣaṇa (versus the single-target specification of the other ṣaṭ-karma).

The *Sāmba Purāṇa* "Abhicāra Vidhi" section (Vedapath.app translation) documents the broader abhicāra-frame within which vidveṣaṇa is positioned, including the specialised fire-sacrifices meant to neutralise enemies by disrupting their alliances. The *Mantra Mahodadhi* of Mahīdhara (17th c.) reproduces multiple vidveṣaṇa-formulations from the Śaiva-Śākta corpus; the per-recension exact forms are dīkṣā-restricted in the published editions.

## IV. Yantra (if attested)

A vidveṣaṇa-specific yantra is documented in the *Mantra Mahodadhi* of Mahīdhara and in the Śāradā Tilaka paddhati tradition: a square *bhūpura* with four cardinal gates, an eight-petalled lotus ring, and a central downward-pointing *tri-koṇa* (yoni-triangle) housing the vidveṣaṇa-bīja at its bindu. The distinctive feature of the vidveṣaṇa-yantra (versus the yantras of the other five ṣaṭ-karma) is that *two* names — the two targets to be discorded — are inscribed within the *tri-koṇa* on either side of the bīja, with a *khaḍga* (sword) or *vajra* symbol drawn between them, symbolising the cut that severs their bond. The *Śāradā Tilaka* paddhati documents the application of this yantra inscribed on *bhūrja* (birch-bark) or *tāṭi* (palm-leaf) using a paste of *kuṅkuma*, *gorocanā*, and *loha-bhasma* (iron-ash, consistent with the iron / bell-metal kalāśa-signature). Specific bīja placements are dīkṣā-restricted in the published editions and are not reproduced here.

## V. Warnings and Contraindications (per sources)

- The *Śāradā Tilaka* paddhati, the *Prapañcasāra* commentary of Padmapāda, and the *Mantra Mahodadhi* of Mahīdhara all restrict the operative vidveṣaṇa-mantra to practitioners who have received the relevant dīkṣā in an authorised lineage; the textual tradition is uniform on this point.
- Woodroffe (1929) classifies vidveṣaṇa within the broader *abhicāra* category — "the Karma described in the Tantras and Atharvaveda... Hiṃsā-karma, or act injurious to others" — and warns that the *ugra*-ṣaṭ-karma are subject to the *kāmya*-rite cautions of *Mahānirvāṇa Tantra* Ch. VIII.
- Sanderson (2009, "The Śaiva Age") documents the Śaiva-Śākta textual tradition's caution that ṣaṭ-karma performed without correct *mantra-siddhi* rebounds on the practitioner: the textual witnesses describe vidveṣaṇa-specific rebound as the dissolution of the practitioner's *own* alliances — the bond between the practitioner and their guru, their dīkṣā-brothers, their family, and their patrons.
- The *Brahmayāmala* / *Picumata* (Hatley 2018 critical edition) documents the early Śaiva-Śākta tradition's warning that vidveṣaṇa-operations on one's guru, on a brāhmaṇa, on a kinsman, or on a *dīkṣā-gotra*-member (a co-initiate) are explicitly prohibited and result in the dissolution of the practitioner's own dīkṣā-bond.
- The Śaiva-Śākta commentarial tradition distinguishes vidveṣaṇa-karma from the modern commercial "vidveṣaṇa-mantra" literature (a 20th-c. popular genre, exemplified by self-published PDFs); the textual-tradition operation is a *mantra-śāstra* rite requiring prior dīkṣā and puraścaraṇa, not a self-help procedure.
