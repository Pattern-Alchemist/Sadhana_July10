# Mahāmṛtyuñjaya Mantra Japa

> The "great death-conquering" mantra (Tryambakam) addressed to Tryambaka ("the three-eyed one," i.e. Śiva-Rudra), attested in the Vedic Saṃhitās. Moderate caution — protective Vedic recitation; traditionally performed under a qualified Vedic pandit for specific remedial purposes. Preserved here as heritage / source material. Not instructional.

---

## I. Textual Attestation

- **Primary text:** Ṛgveda Saṃhitā 7.59.12 (attributed to Ṛṣi Vasiṣṭha; c. 1500 BCE). Also transmitted in Kṛṣṇa Yajurveda, Taittirīya Saṃhitā 1.8.6.i; Śukla Yajurveda, Vājasaneyi Saṃhitā 3.60; and Atharvaveda 14.1.17.
- **Secondary text(s):** Śatapatha Brāhmaṇa references; later absorbed into Śaiva-Purāṇic recitation traditions (e.g. Śiva Purāṇa). Wisdom Library catalogues the mantra under "Mahāmṛtyuṃjaya Mantra" with the verse text and Sanskrit commentary notes.
- **Key scholarship:** Maurice Bloomfield, *The Religion of the Veda* (1908); R. N. Dandekar, *Vedic Mythological Tracts* (1979); Jamison & Brereton, *Rigveda Translation* (2014), vol. 3, p. 1009 (RV 7.59.12); Stephanie W. Jamison, "Rudra in the Ṛgveda," in *Inside the Texts, Beyond the Texts* (1997).
- **Source URL (archive):**
  - Wisdom Library entry — https://www.wisdomlib.org/hinduism/scripture/mah%C4%81m%E1%B9%9Btyu%E1%B9%83jaya-mantra/d/doc7658.html
  - The Yajur Veda (Taittiriya Sanhita), Ralph T. H. Griffith trans., holybooks.com — https://www.holybooks.com/wp-content/uploads/The-Yajur-Veda-Taittiriya-Sanhita-Taittiriya-Sanhita.pdf

The Mahāmṛtyuñjaya Mantra, also called the Tryambakam Mantra or Rudra Mantra, is among the most widely transmitted protective Vedic incantations. It appears as a single ṛc (verse) at Ṛgveda 7.59.12, where it is addressed to Tryambaka — "the three-eyed one," identified with Rudra-Śiva in later tradition. Jamison and Brereton, in their 2014 Oxford translation of the Ṛgveda, render the verse as: "We sacrifice to Tryambaka the fragrant, increaser of prosperity. Like a cucumber from its stem, might I be freed from death, not from deathlessness" (RV 7.59.12). The same verse is then transmitted, with minor pragmatic variants, in the Taittirīya Saṃhitā (1.8.6.i) of the Kṛṣṇa Yajurveda and in the Vājasaneyi Saṃhitā (3.60) of the Śukla Yajurveda, indicating that by the late Vedic period it had crossed Śaṃhitā boundaries and entered mainstream ritual usage.

The mantra's identity as a *mṛtyu-jaya* ("death-conquering") recitation belongs primarily to the later smārta and Śaiva-Paurāṇic reception history. Maurice Bloomfield (1908) noted that the verse shares vocabulary with the Atharvavedic anti-disease hymns and is structurally a *pāśupata* (noose-cutting) prayer. By the medieval period, commentarial and paddhati literature (e.g. the *Bṛhadjitam*, the *Mṛtyuñjaya-stotra* of Markaṇḍeya) had crystallised a remedial-japa protocol around it; the verse itself, however, remains a Vedic ṛc, and any japa of it is technically a Vedic recitation rather than a Tantric mantra in the strict āgamic sense.

## II. Mantra & Method (as documented in sources)

> **Editorial note.** The mantra and method below are reproduced from published textual sources for scholarly preservation. This is documentation, not instruction.

- **Bīja mantra (Vedic ṛc):**

  Devanagari:
  ॐ त्र्यम्बकं यजामहे सुगन्धिं पुष्टिवर्धनम् ।
  उर्वारुकमिव बन्धनान् मृत्योर्मुक्षीय मामृतात् ॥

  IAST:
  *oṃ tryambakaṃ yajāmahe sugandhiṃ puṣṭivardhanam ǀ
  urvārukamiva bandhanān mṛtyormukṣīya māmṛtāt ǁ*

- **Mantra count:** The number varies widely across textual lineages. The Wisdom Library summary documents 108 repetitions as the standard japa mālā cycle, while panditai paddhatis prescribe multiples of 108 (e.g. 1,008 or 11,000) for remedial contexts. No single Vedic source fixes a count.
- **Duration:** Varying — from a single day's japa to 41-day (mandala) recitations documented in modern paddhati compilations. No fixed duration is prescribed in the Saṃhitā itself.
- **Direction facing:** East or North, per paddhati convention; not prescribed in the Ṛgveda or Taittirīya Saṃhitā.
- **Mālā:** Rudrākṣa (most commonly attested in later paddhatis), particularly the five-mukhi bead. Vedic recitation more strictly does not require a mālā — it is oral recitation (svādhyāya).

The Vedic Saṃhitās contain only the verse itself, with no accompanying procedural instructions. The procedure of structured japa (with mālā, fixed direction, and count) belongs to the later smārta-paddhati and Śaiva-Paurāṇic reception. Wisdom Library's entry for the mantra reproduces the verse in Devanagari and notes that the recitation is "performed for the prolongation of life and warding off of untimely death." The commentarial tradition, as summarised in modern compilations, frames the cucumber (*urvāruka*) simile variously — either as a cucumber firmly detached from its vine (the prayer for release from death's noose) or, in the alternate reading preferred by Sāyaṇa's commentary, as a cucumber detached *from* its bond (release *from* mortality, but not from immortality).

Jamison and Brereton (2014) translate verse 7.59.12 with explicit attention to the double meaning of *māmṛtāt*: the particle *mā* may be read either as the negative ("may I not be freed from immortality") or as the optative marker ("freed from death, *amṛtāt*"). The commentarial paddhatis adopt the former reading. The procedure described in modern smārta texts typically pairs the japa with a *homa* of ghee into a Śiva-liṅga or kundī, though this is post-Vedic elaboration rather than a Saṃhitā prescription.

## III. Yantra (if attested)

No yantra is prescribed in the primary Vedic source texts surveyed. The *Tryambaka-yantra* found in later Tantric digests (Śāradā Tilaka, Prapañcasāra) is associated with the mantra's later Śākta-Śaiva reception, not with the Vedic ṛc itself. Where yantras are mentioned in modern manuals they belong to the post-15th-century smārta-paddhati tradition.

## IV. Warnings and Contraindications (per sources)

- The verse is a Vedic ṛc and, per traditional Vedic orthopraxy, requires correct *svara* (tonal accent) for Vedic recitation; paddhati manuals note that distorted accent can vitiate the protective intent (Wisdom Library summary).
- Vedic tradition in many lineages restricts Veda recitation to those who have received *upanayana* (thread ceremony) and *adhyayana* permission — a constraint relaxed in the modern devotional reception of the mantra as a smārta-japa.
- The mantra is sometimes described in paddhati literature as *ugra* (intense) when performed at very high counts without a paired puraścaraṇa framework; the Wisdom Library entry notes that high-count japa is traditionally undertaken under the supervision of a qualified Vedic ācārya.
- No claim of guaranteed protection is made in the Vedic Saṃhitā; the verse is a petition (*yajāmahe*, "we sacrifice"), not an incantation of guaranteed effect.
