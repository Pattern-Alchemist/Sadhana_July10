# Bagalāmukhī Sādhana

> The eighth of the Daśa Mahāvidyās, Bagalāmukhī embodies *stambhana* — the power to paralyse, silence, or immobilise. High caution — an advanced Śākta Mahāvidyā rite traditionally associated with the arrest of hostile force and of inauspicious speech. Preserved here as heritage / source material. Not instructional.

---

## I. Textual Attestation

- **Primary text:** *Bagalāmukhī Tantra*; *Todala Tantra* (ch. 6, which lists the ten Mahāvidyās and their mūla-mantras); *Rudrayāmala* (specifically its *Bagalāmukhī-stotra* section, Uttara-tantra).
- **Secondary text(s):** *Bagalāmukhī Rahasyam* (Pitāmbarā Peeth, Datia, 1st ed. 1964); *Brahmāstra Vidyā evaṃ Bagalāmukhī Sādhana* (Shyāmākaṇṭ Dvivedī 'Ānand', Chowkhamba); *Śākta Praamoda* (mentions Bagalāmukhī among the Mahāvidyā paddhatis); *Phetkāriṇī Tantra* (a sister text traditionally paired with Bagalāmukhī).

Bagalāmukhī is the eighth goddess in the canonical Daśa Mahāvidyā list. Her name is sometimes etymologised as *valgā-mukhī* ("she of the moving or eloquent face") — a reading preserved in the Śukla-yajurvedic commentary tradition — though the more common Śākta reading links her name to the *valgā*/*bagalā* crane and to the iconography of seizing the tongue. Goudriaan and Gupta (1981, pp. 33–37) place the crystallisation of the Mahāvidyā cycle, including Bagalāmukhī, in the post-9th-century Śākta milieu that codified the "ten goddesses" list in the Todala Tantra and the parallel *Mahāvidyā* chapter of the Rudrayāmala. White (2003) discusses the stambhana-prayoga tradition that surrounds her cult and its interface with the broader Śākta-Mantramārga.

The Pitāmbarā Peeth at Datia (Madhya Pradesh) is the most historically significant institutional centre of Bagalāmukhī worship in the modern period, and its 1964 *Bagalāmukhī Rahasyam* (digitised on the Internet Archive) is a printed paddhati preserving the south-Śākta recension of her mūla-mantra, dhāraṇā, yantra, and homa procedure. Shyāmākaṇṭ Dvivedī 'Ānand's *Brahmāstra Vidyā evaṃ Bagalāmukhī Sādhana* (Chowkhamba) is the second printed primary source, pairing the Bagalāmukhī practice with the *brahmāstra* prayoga of the Mahābhārata epic tradition. The Sanskrit digitisation of the *Todala Tantra* and the *Rudrayāmala Tantram* on the Internet Archive (the latter from the Sudhakar Malaviya edition) supply the textual witness for her position in the Mahāvidyā list itself.

## II. Mantra & Method (as documented in sources)

> **Editorial note.** The mantra and method below are reproduced from published textual sources for scholarly preservation. This is documentation, not instruction.

- **Bīja mantra:** The mūla-mantra is documented in the *Todala Tantra* (ch. 6) and in the Pitāmbarā Peeth *Bagalāmukhī Rahasyam*; its exact form varies across textual lineages. The mantra incorporates the *hūṃ* and *phaṭ* phonemes characteristic of ugra-devatā bījas, with the goddess's name-bīja *ba* or *vā* prefixed to a *hṛllekhā* bīja. The specific formulation is not reproduced here, as per-text variants differ and the published editions carry explicit instructions limiting open recitation to dīkṣit practitioners.
- **Mantra count:** Paddhati literature documents puraścaraṇa counts in multiples of 100,000, with structural parallels to other Mahāvidyā puraścaraṇas (Bagalāmukhī's prescribed count is traditionally placed in the range of 1,00,000 to 12,50,000 depending on the prayoga). Specific counts are not reproduced here.
- **Duration:** Varies; puraścaraṇa cycles of 40 / 45 / 48 days are documented in the Pitāmbarā Peeth Rahasyam.
- **Direction facing:** South or East, per paddhati convention (the Pitāmbarā Peeth Rahasyam favours south, consistent with stambhana's association with the *yāmya* direction).
- **Mālā:** Sphaṭika (rock crystal) or rudrākṣa, per the Pitāmbarā Peeth Rahasyam; the textual witnesses do not uniformly specify, and some paddhatis specify a haldi (turmeric) mālā because of Bagalāmukhī's *Pītāmbarī* ("yellow-robed") form.

The procedure documented in the *Bagalāmukhī Rahasyam* (1964) and summarised in Dvivedī's *Brahmāstra Vidyā* opens with the standard Śākta nyāsa sequence, followed by a dhāraṇā of the goddess seated on a golden throne in the midst of an ocean of nectar, golden-complexioned (*pītāmbarī*), holding the tongue of an enemy with her left hand and a mace (*mudgara*) in her right. The dhāraṇā is followed by mūla-mantra japa and a homa of yellow flowers, turmeric, and ghee into a square kuṇḍa. White (2003, pp. 218–222) notes that Bagalāmukhī's stambhana-prayoga is conceptually allied with the Vedic *śamana* and *stambhana* rites of the Atharvaveda, though the paddhati form is a fully developed Śākta-Tantric rite rather than a Vedic remnant.

Sanderson (2007) places the textual codification of Bagalāmukhī in the same Kālī-kula / Śākta-Mantramārga milieu that produced the *Kālī Tantra*, *Mahānirvāṇa Tantra* (which references her as one of the Mahāvidyās), and the *Todala Tantra*'s canonical Mahāvidyā list. Goudriaan and Gupta (1981) note that the *Phetkāriṇī Tantra* — traditionally paired with Bagalāmukhī's cult — codifies the more aggressive *vidveṣaṇa* / *stambhana* prayogas surrounding her worship and is one of the few Tantras explicitly devoted to a single Mahāvidyā's hostile application.

## III. Yantra (if attested)

A square yantra with a central six-petalled lotus enclosing the bīja of the goddess, surrounded by three concentric bhūpura rings, is described in the Pitāmbarā Peeth *Bagalāmukhī Rahasyam* and is paralleled in the Dvivedī *Brahmāstra Vidyā*. The yantra is traditionally drawn on gold, copper, or birch-bark (*bhūrja*) using a paste of turmeric and gorocanā. Specific bīja placements within the six petals are documented in the Rahasyam and vary between the Pitāmbarā Peeth recension and the parallel Chowkhamba recension; the per-petal bīja formulations are not reproduced here.

## IV. Warnings and Contraindications (per sources)

- The *Bagalāmukhī Rahasyam* explicitly restricts the mūla-mantra to practitioners who have received Bagalāmukhī dīkṣā in a recognized Śākta paramparā; the printed paddhati carries a publisher's notice to this effect.
- Dvivedī's *Brahmāstra Vidyā* notes that the stambhana application is to be undertaken only in a confirmed situation of hostile attack or obstruction; performing it without that cause is, per the paddhati, ritually counter-productive.
- White (2003) and Sanderson (2007) note that Bagalāmukhī's stambhana-prayoga historically belonged to the abhicāra category, with all the cautions that attach to such rites in the Śākta tradition — including the possibility of rebound on the practitioner.
- The Pitāmbarā Peeth tradition records dietary and purity observances (yellow foods, abstention from meat and alcohol, white clothing for the duration of the puraścaraṇa) as prerequisites; paddhati literature notes that violations are considered to vitiate the practice.
- The goddess's *ugra* nature requires, in the paddhati tradition, parallel protective recitation (e.g., Narasiṃha-kavaca or a Śākta-kavaca) to shield the practitioner.
