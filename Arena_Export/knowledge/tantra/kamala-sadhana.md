# Kamalā Sādhana

> The tenth of the Daśa Mahāvidyās, Kamalā ("She of the Lotus") — also called Kamalātmikā — is the tantric form of Lakṣmī: golden-complexioned, seated on a lotus, bathed by four elephants with their trunks. The benevolent, prosperity-bestowing form among the Mahāvidyās. Attested in the *Kamalā Mahāvidyā* (Goswami Prahlad Giri), *Śākta Praamoda*, *Daśa Mahāvidyā Tantra-Sāra*, and the *Mahābhāgavata Purāṇa*. Moderate caution — a saumya Mahāvidyā rite. Preserved here as heritage / source material. Not instructional.

Kamalā is the tenth Mahāvidyā in the canonical lists of the *Todala Tantra*, *Śāradā Tilaka*, *Mahābhāgavata Purāṇa*, and the *Śākta Praamoda*; she is the tantric form of Lakṣmī, the Goddess of prosperity, abundance, and auspiciousness. The *Kamalā Mahāvidyā* of Goswami Prahlad Giri (Chowkhamba edition, Internet Archive) is the principal standalone printed Tantra of Kamalā worship, giving the dhyāna-śloka, mūla-mantra, yantra, homa, and puraścaraṇa procedure. The *Śākta Praamoda* of Rājadevanandana (the standard 19th-century Śākta digest, with the Devanandan Singh Bahadur recension) preserves the Kamalā paddhati within its ten-Mahāvidyā framework. The Internet Archive holds three digitised editions of the *Śākta Praamoda*: the Rājadevanandana edition, the Devanandan Singh Bahadur edition, and the DLI 2015.407147 edition.

The *Daśa Mahāvidyā Tantra-Sāra* of Yogiraj Yashpal (Internet Archive) is a modern Hindi digest that consolidates the Kamalā paddhati from multiple sources; the *Mahavidyā Sadhna* compilation of Dr. Narayan Dutt Shrimali (Internet Archive) reproduces sādhana-extracts from the pages of *Mantra-Tantra-Yantra Vigyan* magazine. The *Mahābhāgavata Purāṇa* — the late-purāṇic source — gives the canonical Mahāvidyā list with Kamalā as tenth, identifying her explicitly with Lakṣmī.

Kinsley (1997: ch. on Kamalā) frames her as the most recognisable of the Mahāvidyās to the non-Śākta public — because her iconography is essentially the iconography of Lakṣmī — but emphasises that the Śākta frame reorients her function: where the Vaiṣṇava Lakṣmī is the consort of Viṣṇu, the tantric Kamalā is the Mahāvidyā in her own right, the Goddess of prosperity as one face of the Devī's cosmic sovereignty. Kinsley notes that the *Mahābhāgavata Purāṇa* explicitly identifies Kamalā with Lakṣmī, but also distinguishes her by her tantric sādhana-frame (with mantra, yantra, and puraścaraṇa that have no Vaiṣṇava parallel). Goudriaan & Gupta (1981) catalogue the *Kamalā Mahāvidyā* among the late-medieval independent Mahāvidyā Tantras.

## II. Iconography (per sources)

According to the dhyāna-śloka preserved in the *Kamalā Mahāvidyā* (Goswami Prahlad Giri ed., Internet Archive) and the *Śākta Praamoda*, Kamalā is described as *kamalāsanā* — seated on a fully-opened lotus — and as *gaurī* or *hemābhā* — golden-complexioned, the colour of gold, of the rising sun. She has four arms: the lower right hand makes the *varada-mudrā* (boon-granting gesture), the lower left hand makes the *abhaya-mudrā* (fearlessness gesture), the upper right hand holds a lotus (*padma*), and the upper left hand holds a lotus. She is ornamented with a crown of gold, a garland of lotus petals, red silk garments, and golden jewellery; she is bathed by four white elephants (*gaja-catuṣṭaya*) standing in the four directions, who pour water or nectar (*amṛta*) upon her from their trunks. The dhyāna-śloka is reproduced in the Manblunder essay on Kamalātmikā (citing the *Śākta Praamoda* and the *Kamalā Mahāvidyā*): "The Goddess Kamalā, golden-complexioned, seated on the lotus, bathed by four elephants with their trunks pouring nectar, holding lotuses in her upper hands, the lower hands granting boons and fearlessness."

The commentarial tradition (summarised in the *Daśa Mahāvidyā Tantra-Sāra* and in the Manblunder essay) reads the four elephants as the four Vedas, the four puruṣārthas (dharma, artha, kāma, mokṣa), or the four directions of cosmic sovereignty; the lotus as the cosmic-womb from which the universe emerges; the golden complexion as the luminous-abundance of the Goddess's grace. Kinsley (1997: ch. on Kamalā) emphasises that the iconography is uniformly saumya and explicitly auspicious — the only Mahāvidyā besides Bālā Tripurasundarī whose dhyāna-śloka contains no fierce, terrifying, or cremation-ground imagery.

Kinsley (1997) further notes the iconographic kinship with the Vaiṣṇava *Gaja-Lakṣmī* form (the four-elephants-bathing-Lakṣmī motif that goes back to the early-historic Buddhist and Hindu sculpture, c. 2nd c. BCE–2nd c. CE); the Śākta Kamalā is the tantric re-orientation of this ancient iconography. The *Mahābhāgavata Purāṇa* explicitly identifies Kamalā with Lakṣmī but locates her within the Mahāvidyā frame as the Goddess of prosperity who is also the Goddess of cosmic dissolution (since all prosperity is transient) — the Mahāvidyā paradox that the saumya Goddess is nonetheless the cosmic-power of dissolution-as-renewal.

## III. Mantra & Method (as documented in sources)

> **Editorial note.** The mantra and method below are reproduced from published textual sources for scholarly preservation. This is documentation, not instruction.

- **Bīja mantra:** The mūla-mantra of Kamalā is documented in the *Kamalā Mahāvidyā* (Goswami Prahlad Giri ed.) and in the *Śākta Praamoda*. The principal bīja is *śrīṃ* — the Lakṣmī-bīja, the seed-syllable of prosperity, abundance, and royal sovereignty. The publicly attested structural form (per the Manblunder essay and the Wisdom Library entries, cross-referenced) is the *śrīṃ*-prefixed name-bīja with the *namaḥ* or *svāhā* termination: "Oṃ Śrīṃ Hrīṃ Śrīṃ Kamalāyai Namah." The full mūla-mantra incorporates additional bījas (including *aiṃ* for the Vedic-speech-aspect, *hrīṃ* for the māyā-aspect, *śrīṃ* for the Lakṣmī-aspect) and is dīkṣā-restricted in published editions. The per-recension exact form is not reproduced here.
- **Mantra count:** The *Śākta Praamoda* paddhati documents puraścaraṇa counts of 100,000 / 300,000 / 1,000,000 japa-cycles, with corresponding homa at 1/10th. The *Kamalā Mahāvidyā* records a structural count of 16 lakhs (1,600,000) for the full puraścaraṇa.
- **Duration:** Puraścaraṇa cycles of 11 / 21 / 41 days are documented in the *Kamalā Mahāvidyā* and in the *Daśa Mahāvidyā Tantra-Sāra*.
- **Direction facing:** East, per Śākta paddhati convention; the *Śākta Praamoda* also documents a north-facing variant.
- **Mālā:** *Sphaṭika* (rock-crystal) or *tulasī* (basil — appropriate to the Lakṣmī-aspect); the *Śākta Praamoda* specifies sphaṭika for the Kamalā japa.

The procedure documented in the *Kamalā Mahāvidyā* (Goswami Prahlad Giri ed.) opens with the standard Śākta preliminaries — *kara-nyāsa*, *aṅga-nyāsa*, *ṣaḍaṅga-nyāsa*, *dig-bandhana*, *bhūta-śuddhi*, *prāṇa-pratiṣṭhā* — followed by the *Kamalā-dhyāna* upon the lotus throne with the four elephants, japa of the mūla-mantra on the prescribed mālā, and homa of *ghee*, *trimadhura* (curd, milk, and honey), *śatī*-flowers or *lotus*-petals, and *kuṅkuma*-mixed ghee into a square kuṇḍa (the square kuṇḍa is prescribed because Kamalā's yantra is a square-frame, not a triangle — see Section IV). The *Śākta Praamoda* documents the *pañca-pūjā* and the *ṣoḍaśopacāra* variants; the *Daśa Mahāvidyā Tantra-Sāra* reproduces the full pūjā-paddhati.

Kinsley (1997) notes that the Kamalā worship in living Śākta tradition is positioned as the saumya Mahāvidyā to be undertaken after the practitioner has stabilised in the more demanding forms (Kālī, Tārā, Chinnamastā); the textual tradition treats her as the Goddess who integrates the dissolution-experiences of the fierce Mahāvidyās into the prosperity-and-abundance of the integrated cosmos. The *Mahavidyā Sadhna* compilation (Internet Archive) reproduces the operational sequence for the modern practitioner.

## IV. Yantra (if attested)

A yantra is described in the *Kamalā Mahāvidyā* (Goswami Prahlad Giri ed.) and in the *Śākta Praamoda*. Kamalā's yantra is a square-frame yantra (not a triangular-frame yantra like the fierce Mahāvidyās): a *bhūpura* (square enclosure) of three concentric lines, enclosing an eight-petalled lotus (*aṣṭa-dala*), enclosing a sixteen-petalled lotus (*ṣoḍaśa-dala*), enclosing a central *bindu* housing the *śrīṃ*-bīja. The four corners of the *bhūpura* house the four elephant-bījas (the four gaja-śaktis who bathe the Goddess). The yantra is described as inscribed on gold-plate, copper-plate (*tāmra-paṭa*), or on *bhūrja* (birch-bark) using a paste of *kuṅkuma*, *gorocanā*, *rakta-candana*, and *sphaṭika* powder. Specific bīja placements are documented in the *Kamalā Mahāvidyā* paddhati and in the *Śākta Praamoda*.

## V. Warnings and Contraindications (per sources)

- The *Kamalā Mahāvidyā* and the *Śākta Praamoda* paddhati literature explicitly restrict the Kamalā mūla-mantra to practitioners who have received Śākta dīkṣā in an authorised lineage; the textual tradition is uniform on this point. Although Kamalā is saumya in iconography, she is nonetheless a Mahāvidyā — one of the ten cosmic forms of the Devī — and her worship is dīkṣā-gated.
- Kinsley (1997) and Goudriaan & Gupta (1981) document the Śākta textual caution that the Kamalā sādhana is doctrinally subtle because the Goddess's saumya appearance can mislead the practitioner into treating her as a "safe" or "easy" deity; the commentarial tradition treats her as no less cosmic in power than the fierce Mahāvidyās, and warns against the spiritual complacency that her auspicious iconography can produce.
- The *Mahābhāgavata Purāṇa*'s identification of Kamalā with Lakṣmī requires careful contextualisation: the Vaiṣṇava Lakṣmī and the Śākta Kamalā share iconography but have distinct sādhana-frames, mantras, and yantras. The Śākta commentarial literature warns against conflating the two traditions.
- The *Kamalā Mahāvidyā* records dietary and purity observances for the duration of the puraścaraṇa: golden or yellow clothing, vegetarian diet, abstention from alcohol, celibacy, residence in a clean and well-kept hermitage or temple precinct (the saumya Kamalā sādhana requires a more conventionally "pure" environment than the fierce Mahāvidyās, whose cremation-ground imagery inverts conventional purity).
- The Śākta commentarial literature warns that the Kamalā sādhana undertaken with worldly-intention (for material prosperity alone, rather than for the integration of prosperity into the practitioner's vidyā) is doctrinally incomplete and is described as producing *alpa-phala* (limited fruit); the textual frame treats material prosperity as a by-product of the sādhana, not its goal.
