# Sudarśana Chakra Sādhana

> A Pāñcarātra-Āgamic Śrī Vaiṣṇava rite addressed to Sudarśana — the discus of Viṣṇu, personified as the *āyudhapuruṣa* ("weapon-person") Chakraratin — primarily as a protective (*rakṣā*) and *abhicāra*-counter practice, often paired with Nārasiṃha upāsanā. Moderate caution — a Pāñcarātra practice historically transmitted under Ācārya supervision but more broadly accessible than Mahāvidyā rites. Preserved here as heritage / source material. Not instructional.

## I. Textual Attestation

- **Primary text:** *Ahirbudhnya Saṃhitā* (a Pāñcarātra Āgama, c. 200–450 CE; specifically chapters 39–44, which give the Sudarśana-Mantra, dhāraṇā, and homa-procedure). The text is preserved in Sanskrit edition (Sudhakar Malaviya, Chowkhamba, digitised on the Internet Archive); F. Otto Schrader's *Introduction to the Pāñcarātra and the Ahirbudhnya Saṃhitā* (Adyar Library, 1916) remains the standard scholarly introduction.

Sudarśana is the discus (*cakra*) of Viṣṇu and the chief *āyudhapuruṣa* (personified-weapon) of the Pāñcarātra Āgama. The Ahirbudhnya Saṃhitā — a Pāñcarātra text of approximately 3,880 verses, dated by Schrader (1916) to c. 200–450 CE on internal theological and linguistic grounds — is the principal textual source for the Sudarśana upāsanā. Its 60 chapters are framed as a dialogue between the sage Bharadvāja and the ṛṣi Durvāsas, with the long Sudarśana section (chs. 39–44) giving the dhāraṇā, mūla-mantra, homa-procedure, and yantra of the deity. Schrader's 1916 Adyar Library introduction remains the standard scholarly reference for the textual-historical questions surrounding the Saṃhitā; the Sanskrit–Hindi edition by Sudhakar Malaviya (Chowkhamba, digitised on the Internet Archive) supplies the primary text.

The Ahirbudhnya Saṃhitā presents Sudarśana as *Chakravartin* — the cosmic ruler-form of the discus — and identifies him with Nārasiṃha in the dual-deity Sudarśana-Nārasiṃha worship that is the dominant South Indian Śrī Vaiṣṇava form of the practice. Schrader (1916, pp. 95–104) discusses the structural identification of Sudarśana with Kṛṣṇa's discus-weapon, with the Pāñcarātra theology of the *vyūha*-emanation of Viṣṇu, and with the Vaiṣṇava protective magic (*rakṣā*) tradition. The Ahirbudhnya Saṃhitā explicitly frames the Sudarśana practice as protective and counter-abhicāra: the discus is the *abhicāra* destroying hostile mantra-prayogas. Gupta (1972, 1981) and Gonda (1970) place the Pāñcarātra Āgama as a whole in the early centuries CE, with the Ahirbudhnya Saṃhitā representing the more developed theological stratum of the tradition.

The *Sudarśana Kavaca* is a later medieval stotra-form protective text used in the Śrī Vaiṣṇava paddhati; it is structurally cognate with the Śaiva *Nārasiṃha-kavaca* of the Mantramārga. The Ahirbudhnya Saṃhitā itself contains a Sudarśana-kavaca section that is the textual model for the later standalone kavaca literature.

## II. Mantra & Method (as documented in sources)

> **Editorial note.** The mantra and method below are reproduced from published textual sources for scholarly preservation. This is documentation, not instruction.

- **Bīja mantra:** The Sudarśana mūla-mantra is documented in the Ahirbudhnya Saṃhitā (chs. 39–44). Its exact form varies across the Pāñcarātra recensions; the Ahirbudhnya Saṃhitā form is the principal textual witness. The mantra incorporates the *kṣa* phoneme characteristic of the Sudarśana-Nārasiṃha vidyā (the *kṣrauṃ* bīja of Nārasiṃha being the conceptual seed of both deities), with the discus-name-bīja *cakra* or *hrīṃ* prefixed. The specific formulation is not reproduced here, as the Pāñcarātra tradition — like the Śākta and Śaiva — restricts open recitation of the mūla-mantra to practitioners who have received *samaya-dīkṣā* in a recognised Śrī Vaiṣṇava paramparā.
- **Mantra count:** The Ahirbudhnya Saṃhitā documents puraścaraṇa counts in multiples of 100,000; the standard Sudarśana puraścaraṇa count is recorded in the Ahirbudhnya paddhati tradition. Specific numbers are not reproduced here.
- **Duration:** Varies; puraścaraṇa cycles of 41 / 45 days are documented in the Śrī Vaiṣṇava paddhati tradition.
- **Direction facing:** East or North, per Pāñcarātra convention (the Ahirbudhnya paddhati favours east, consistent with the *vaiṣṇava* directional association).
- **Mālā:** Tulasi (basil) or sphaṭika (rock crystal), per Śrī Vaiṣṇava convention; the Ahirbudhnya Saṃhitā paddhati permits both.

The procedure documented in the Ahirbudhnya Saṃhitā (chs. 39–44) opens with the standard Pāñcarātra nyāsa sequence — *kara-nyāsa*, *aṅga-nyāsa*, the *vyūha-nyāsa* (mapping the practitioner's body to the *vyūha*-emanations Vāsudeva, Saṅkarṣaṇa, Pradyumna, Aniruddha), and the *digbandhana* — followed by the dhāraṇā of Sudarśana as a many-armed deity with the discus as his body, fiery-complexioned (*agni-prabhā*), with multiple hands holding the discus, the conch, the mace, and the lotus, surrounded by a halo of flame. The dhāraṇā is followed by the mūla-mantra japa and a homa of ghee, sesame, and *apūpa*-cakes into a square kuṇḍa. Schrader (1916, pp. 95–104) summarises the procedure and notes the dual-deity Sudarśana-Nārasiṃha identification, in which the discus-form and the man-lion form are visualised as a single protective deity — the discus being the weapon and Nārasiṃha being the wielder.

The Ahirbudhnya Saṃhitā explicitly frames the practice as protective (*rakṣā*) and counter-abhicāra: the discus is described as *sarvābhicāra-ghātī* ("destroyer of all hostile magic") and the dhāraṇā includes a section in which the practitioner visualises the discus spinning in front of him to cut through hostile mantras and obstructive entities. The *Sudarśana Kavaca* section of the Ahirbudhnya Saṃhitā is the textual model for the later standalone kavaca literature and is recited in the Śrī Vaiṣṇava paddhati tradition as a daily protective rite. Gupta (1972, *Lakṣmī Tantra*) discusses the parallel Sudarśana material in the *Lakṣmī Tantra* and notes the systematic structural cognates between the Pāñcarātra upāsanā and the Śaiva-Mantramārga practice of the same period.

## III. Yantra (if attested)

A circular yantra with a central eight-petalled lotus enclosing the Sudarśana bīja, surrounded by three concentric *cakra*-rings (representing the spinning of the discus) and a square *bhūpura*, is described in the Ahirbudhnya Saṃhitā (chs. 41–42). The eight petals contain the bījas of the eight directional forms of the discus. The yantra is traditionally drawn on gold, copper, or birch-bark using a paste of *candanā* and *saffron*. Specific bīja placements are documented in the Ahirbudhnya Saṃhitā paddhati tradition (preserved in the Sudhakar Malaviya edition) and are not reproduced here. The dual-deity Sudarśana-Nārasiṃha yantra is the dominant South Indian Śrī Vaiṣṇava form of the practice.

## IV. Warnings and Contraindications (per sources)

- The Ahirbudhnya Saṃhitā restricts the Sudarśana mūla-mantra to practitioners who have received *samaya-dīkṣā* in a recognized Śrī Vaiṣṇava paramparā; this restriction is explicit in the paddhati tradition and is preserved in the printed editions.
- Schrader (1916) notes that the Ahirbudhnya Saṃhitā explicitly frames the Sudarśana practice as *ugra* in its counter-abhicāra function — the discus is, in the textual tradition, the *abhihantuṛ* (slayer of hostile forces) and paddhati literature cautions against undertaking the practice without a confirmed cause (i.e., a confirmed hostile prayoga against the practitioner).
- Gupta (1972) notes the structural cognates between the Pāñcarātra upāsanā and the Śaiva-Mantramārga; the Pāñcarātra tradition nonetheless treats itself as soteriologically distinct from the Śaiva-Mantramārga, and paddhati literature cautions against mixing the two without explicit Ācārya permission.
- The Ahirbudhnya Saṃhitā records purity observances (vegetarian diet, abstention from alcohol, white clothing) as prerequisites for the puraścaraṇa; violations are considered to vitiate the practice.
- The *Sudarśana Kavaca* — the stotra-form text derived from the Ahirbudhnya Saṃhitā — is more widely recited than the mūla-mantra and is treated as a protective recitation accessible to smārta practitioners without formal *samaya-dīkṣā*.
