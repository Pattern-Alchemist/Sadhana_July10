# Uccāṭana Karma

> The fifth of the six ṣaṭ-karma — the driving-away (*uccāṭana*) rite for forcing a hostile force to depart from a place, role, or relationship. High caution — the second of the "fierce four" *ugra*-ṣaṭ-karma (vidveṣaṇa, uccāṭana, māraṇa, plus the optional mohana). Preserved here as heritage / source material. Not instructional.

The *Agni Purāṇa* ch. 138 (Wisdom Library translation) catalogues *uccāṭana* as the fifth of the *ṣaṭ-karma*, immediately after vidveṣaṇa and immediately before māraṇa. The *Prapañcasāra Tantra* (Avalon 1919 edition) preserves the operative *uccāṭana* paddhati within its mantra-śāstra frame; Shastra Deep's essay on *Prapañcasāra* confirms the canonical six-act enumeration with uccāṭana positioned fifth. The Wisdom Library "Satkarman" definition page records that the *Ṣaṭsāhasrasaṃhitā* and the *Rasaratnākara* both catalogue the same six operations in the same order.

The *Mahānirvāṇa Tantra* (Avalon / Woodroffe 1913 English translation) — Chapter 26 of the Woodroffe commentary titled "Shakta Sadhana (The Ordinary Ritual)," preserved on sacred-texts.com — explicitly names "driving away or Uccatana, and killing or Marana when performed for a malevolent purpose" as the *kāmya*-karmas within the broader Kaula frame. The Aghori.it full-text mirror of the *Mahānirvāṇa Tantra* confirms the kalāśa-material prescriptions for the six ṣaṭ-karma in Chapter VIII.

The *Śāradā Tilaka Tantram* (Lakṣmaṇa Deśikendra, c. 10th–11th c., with Rāghavabhaṭṭa's *Padārthādarśa* commentary) preserves uccāṭana-mantra formulations within the text's wider pūjā-prakaraṇa. Four Internet Archive digitisations are available: the Sudhakar Malaviya edition, the Avalon 1933 translation (Tantric Texts Series 16), the Mukunda Jha Bakshi Series No. 107 Kashi Sanskrit Series edition, and the Chowkhamba Padārthādarśa edition. The *Brahmayāmala* / *Picumata* (Hatley 2018 critical edition) preserves early attestations of the uccāṭana-operation within the Vidyāpīṭha Śaiva-Śākta textual corpus.

Sanderson (2009, "The Śaiva Age") documents uccāṭana as one of the standard *abhicāra*-operations performed by the Śaiva-Śākta *ācārya* on behalf of royal patrons from the early-medieval period — the rite of driving away an enemy-king's minister, an opposing general, or a hostile official is among the most-cited applications in the epigraphic and textual record. The escholarship.org article on "Raising the Dead and Saving Them: Transformations in Funerary Ritual in Hindu Tantrism" (EBHR / eScholarship, UC) frames the ṣaṭ-karma within the broader ritual-ecology of medieval Śaiva-Śākta practice.

## II. The Category (per sources)

Uccāṭana-karma is the fifth of the six ṣaṭ-karma and the fourth of the five *ugra* (fierce) operations. The etymology is from *ut-ccāṭana* ("driving up-and-away," "uprooting," "expulsion"); the rite forces a hostile force — a person, a spirit, a planetary influence, or an institutional position-holder — to depart from a place, role, or relationship. The Shastra Deep essay on *Prapañcasāra* confirms that the *Prapañcasāra Tantra* enumerates uccāṭana as the fifth of the six, immediately after vidveṣaṇa and immediately before māraṇa — positioning uccāṭana as the second of the "fierce four" (vidveṣaṇa, uccāṭana, māraṇa, plus the optional mohana).

The textual tradition consistently frames uccāṭana as an *expulsion* operation: where vidveṣaṇa operates on the bond *between* two targets and māraṇa destroys the target's life, uccāṭana operates on the target's *presence* — forcing them to leave a specified location, role, or relationship. The target remains alive, but is no longer present where they were. The Grokipedia entry on Abhicāra, citing the standard Śaiva-Śākta textual tradition, describes uccāṭana as "the act of expulsion or driving away... one of the six karmas (*ṣaṭkarmāṇi*) within the broader category of Abhicāra rituals in Hindu Tantric traditions."

Woodroffe (1929, *Shakti and Shâkta*) frames uccāṭana within the broader *abhicāra* category — "the Karma described in the Tantras and Atharvaveda... magical process with intent to destroy or injure" — but distinguishes the expulsion-rite from māraṇa (death) by noting that uccāṭana produces *departure* rather than death. The Śaiva-Śākta commentarial tradition (Rāghavabhaṭṭa on *Śāradā Tilaka*) documents typical applications: expulsion of a hostile official from a court-post, expulsion of a hostile tenant from a property, expulsion of a hostile spirit (*bhūta-graha*) from a possessed person, expulsion of an enemy-army from a territory, or expulsion of a hostile monk from a monastery. The *Brahmayāmala* / *Picumata* (Hatley 2018 critical edition) preserves early attestations of the uccāṭana-operation, including the specialised form of expelling a *vetāla* or *bhūta* from a haunted place.

Sanderson (2009) documents the medieval Śaiva-Śākta *ācārya*'s deployment of uccāṭana on behalf of royal patrons as one of the principal *abhicāra*-services offered to kings: the rite of driving away an enemy-king's minister or general is among the standard textual applications, and the commentarial tradition (e.g. *Mantra Mahodadhi* of Mahīdhara) records specific uccāṭana-mantras targeting institutional positions rather than named individuals.

## III. Mantra & Method (as documented in sources)

> **Editorial note.** The mantra-forms and counts below are documented from published textual sources for scholarly preservation. This is documentation, not instruction.

- **Bīja mantra:** The *Agni Purāṇa* ch. 138 frame attaches the ṣaṭ-karma operations to "all Mantras" — the practitioner's own dīkṣā-mantra, with the uccāṭana operation specified by add-on bījas and the *uccāṭana-nyāsa*. The specific *uccāṭana-bīja* cited in the *Śāradā Tilaka* paddhati and in the *Mantra Mahodadhi* of Mahīdhara is dīkṣā-restricted in published editions; the per-recension exact form is not reproduced here. The Śaiva-Śākta paddhati tradition typically uses *phat*-seed-bījas incorporating the *ṭ* and *cch* phonemes, but the operative combination varies by recension.
- **Mantra count:** The *Prapañcasāra* paddhati and the *Śāradā Tilaka* commentarial tradition document puraścaraṇa counts in multiples of 100,000; the *Mantra Mahodadhi* records the uccāṭana-specific count.
- **Duration:** Cycles of 7, 14, or 21 days are documented in the Śāradā Tilaka paddhati.
- **Direction facing:** South or South-west per paddhati convention for *ugra*-rites; the *Mahānirvāṇa Tantra* Ch. VIII specifies the kalāśa placement for uccāṭana with southern orientation.
- **Kalāśa material:** The Śaiva-Śākta paddhati tradition records a *tāmra* (copper) or *kāṁsyaka* (bell-metal) kalāśa for uccāṭana, with a strict prescription that the kalāśa be *cracked* or *pierced* — the broken-vessel medium being the ritual signature of the rite's intent to "puncture" the target's tenure in their place.

The procedure documented in the *Agni Purāṇa* ch. 138 (per Wisdom Library's translation) frames uccāṭana as the fifth ṣaṭ-karma operation, performed after the standard *ṣaḍaṅga-nyāsa*, *dig-bandhana*, and *guru-pūjā* preliminaries. The practitioner establishes the prescribed (cracked or pierced) *kalāśa*, performs *japa* of the operative mantra with the uccāṭana-bīja prefixed, and conducts a *homa* with *bitter / sour / sharp* offerings (mustard-oil, *nimba* leaves, sour *tamarind*, salt, *trivṛt* root, *guḍa*-alcohol in some recensions). The *Śāradā Tilaka* paddhati documents the application of the *uccāṭana-nyāsa* on the practitioner's own body prior to the operation, and the inscription of the target's name within the yantra's central *tri-koṇa* with a directional-arrow symbol (indicating the direction of forced departure).

The *Mantra Mahodadhi* of Mahīdhara (17th c.) reproduces multiple uccāṭana-formulations from the Śaiva-Śākta corpus, including institutional-position-targeted forms (where no specific named individual is given, but rather the current occupant of a specified post). The per-recension exact forms are dīkṣā-restricted in the published editions. Woodroffe (1929) notes that the uccāṭana-rite is the only one of the *ugra*-ṣaṭ-karma that admits of a "soft" variant — the rite of *uccāṭana* applied to a *bhūta-graha* (a possessing spirit) on behalf of a possessed patient, which the Śaiva-Śākta commentarial tradition frames as a therapeutic rather than aggressive application.

## IV. Yantra (if attested)

An uccāṭana-specific yantra is documented in the *Mantra Mahodadhi* of Mahīdhara and in the Śāradā Tilaka paddhati tradition: a square *bhūpura* with four cardinal gates, an eight-petalled lotus ring, and a central downward-pointing *tri-koṇa* (yoni-triangle) housing the uccāṭana-bīja at its bindu. The distinctive feature of the uccāṭana-yantra (versus the yantras of the other five ṣaṭ-karma) is a directional-arrow (*bāṇa*) symbol drawn through the *tri-koṇa* pointing outward from the bindu toward the gate corresponding to the direction of intended departure — typically the southern gate (the direction of Yama, death, and exile). The Scribd-preserved "Yantra Chin Ta Man I" compilation documents a specific uccāṭana-yantra form featuring a "downward pointing pentagram, in the middle of which is an inverse triangle which contains the target" — the inverted-pentagram / inverse-triangle motif being the ritual signature of the downward-and-away departure. The *Śāradā Tilaka* paddhati documents the application of this yantra inscribed on *bhūrja* (birch-bark) or *tāṭi* (palm-leaf) using a paste of *kuṅkuma*, *gorocanā*, and *tāmra-bhasma* (copper-ash, consistent with the copper / bell-metal kalāśa-signature). Specific bīja placements are dīkṣā-restricted in the published editions and are not reproduced here.

## V. Warnings and Contraindications (per sources)

- The *Śāradā Tilaka* paddhati, the *Prapañcasāra* commentary of Padmapāda, and the *Mantra Mahodadhi* of Mahīdhara all restrict the operative uccāṭana-mantra to practitioners who have received the relevant dīkṣā in an authorised lineage; the textual tradition is uniform on this point.
- Woodroffe (1929) classifies uccāṭana within the broader *abhicāra* category — "Hiṃsā-karma, or act injurious to others" — and warns that the *ugra*-ṣaṭ-karma are subject to the *kāmya*-rite cautions of *Mahānirvāṇa Tantra* Ch. VIII, which restricts their performance to those who have completed the Kaula-dīkṣā and observe the *pañca-tattva* frame.
- Sanderson (2009, "The Śaiva Age") documents the Śaiva-Śākta textual tradition's caution that ṣaṭ-karma performed without correct *mantra-siddhi* rebounds on the practitioner: the textual witnesses describe uccāṭana-specific rebound as the *practitioner's own* expulsion — from their residence, their post, their lineage, or their livelihood.
- The *Brahmayāmala* / *Picumata* (Hatley 2018 critical edition) documents the early Śaiva-Śākta tradition's warning that uccāṭana-operations on one's guru, on a brāhmaṇa, on a kinsman, or on a *dīkṣā-gotra*-member (a co-initiate) are explicitly prohibited and result in the practitioner's own expulsion from the Śaiva-Śākta lineage.
- The therapeutic variant of uccāṭana — the rite applied to a *bhūta-graha* (possessing spirit) on behalf of a possessed patient — is permitted in the commentarial tradition only with the explicit consent of the patient (or their family, if the patient is unconscious) and under the supervision of a qualified *vaidya* (physician); the *Mantra Mahodadhi* tradition records this as the only "soft" application of uccāṭana.
- The Śaiva-Śākta commentarial tradition distinguishes uccāṭana-karma from the modern commercial "uccāṭana-mantra" literature (a 20th-c. popular genre); the textual-tradition operation is a *mantra-śāstra* rite requiring prior dīkṣā and puraścaraṇa, not a self-help procedure.
