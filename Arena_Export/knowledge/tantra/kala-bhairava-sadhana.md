# Kāla Bhairava Aṣṭākṣara Sādhana

> A Śaiva-Bhairava rite addressed to Kāla Bhairava — the "Time-Bhairava" form of Śiva as lord of death and of Kāśī — using an eight-syllable (*aṣṭākṣara*) mantra attested in the Svacchanda Tantra and accompanied by the Kāla Bhairavāṣṭakam hymn attributed to Ādi Śaṅkarācārya. High caution — a Bhairava rite of the Śaiva Mantramārga, traditionally restricted to dīkṣit practitioners. Preserved here as heritage / source material. Not instructional.

---

## I. Textual Attestation

- **Primary text:** *Svacchanda Tantra* (a Śaiva-Bhairava Tantra, c. 6th–8th c., of the Northern Śaiva Siddhānta / Mantramārga corpus; the deity Svacchanda-Bhairava is the canonical form of Bhairava in this tradition); *Kāla Bhairavāṣṭakam* (hymn attributed to Ādi Śaṅkarācārya, preserved in the *Complete Works of Śrī Śaṅkarācārya*, 20-vol. Śrī Vani Vilas Press, Srirangam, 1910 ed.).
- **Secondary text(s):** *Bhairavāṣṭakam* / *Tīkṣṇadaṃṣṭra Kāla Bhairavāṣṭakam* (separate recensions, digitised on Internet Archive); commentarial material in the Tantrāloka of Abhinavagupta (which draws on Svacchanda); *Kāśī Khaṇḍa* of the Skanda Purāṇa (mythological frame for Kāla Bhairava as *kṣetrapāla* of Kāśī).
- **Key scholarship:** Alexis Sanderson, "Śaivism and the Tantric Traditions" (1988) and "The Śaiva Exegesis of Kashmir" (2007); Mark S. G. Kāla Bhairava is one of the eight Bhairavas (the *aṣṭabhairava* cycle) of the Śaiva Mantramārga. The Svacchanda Tantra — the principal textual source for the Bhairava cult of Kashmir Śaivism — codifies Svacchanda-Bhairava as the supreme Bhairava form, with the eight Bhairavas (Asitāṅga, Ruru, Caṇḍa, Krodha, Unmatta, Kapālī, Bhīṣaṇa, and Saṃhāra) as his emanations; Kāla Bhairava is the protective *kṣetrapāla* form most widely worshipped in the post-Tantric Śaiva world, especially at Kāśī (Varanasi). Sanderson's "Śaivism and the Tantric Traditions" (1988) places the Svacchanda Tantra at the head of the *Mantrapīṭha* — the Bhairava division of the Śaiva canon — and notes that the cult of Svacchanda-Bhairava was the dominant form of Śaiva worship in Kashmir from the 7th century onwards. Dyczkowski's *Canon of the Śaivāgama* (1992) gives a full textual-history of the Svacchanda Tantra.

The *Kāla Bhairavāṣṭakam* ("eight verses on Kāla Bhairava") attributed to Ādi Śaṅkarācārya is preserved in the 1910 20-volume *Complete Works of Śrī Śaṅkarācārya* (Sri Vani Vilas Press, Srirangam), digitised on the Internet Archive. The attribution to Śaṅkara is traditional and cannot be definitively confirmed; the hymn is, however, the standard liturgical text for Kāla Bhairava worship across post-15th-century smārta Śaiva orthopraxy. The eight-syllable Bhairava mantra (the *aṣṭākṣara*) of the Svacchanda tradition is documented in the Svacchanda Tantra's *mantra-pāda* chapters; its specific form is preserved in the Svacchanda manuscript tradition indexed in the Muktabodha digital library.

## II. Mantra & Method (as documented in sources)

> **Editorial note.** The mantra and method below are reproduced from published textual sources for scholarly preservation. This is documentation, not instruction.

- **Bīja mantra:** The Svacchanda-Bhairava *aṣṭākṣara* (eight-syllable) mantra is documented in the Svacchanda Tantra's *mantra-pāda*. Its exact form varies across textual lineages. The mantra is structured around the Bhairava bīja *haṃ* / *kṣa* phonemes and incorporates the aghora-mantra element; the per-recension formulation is preserved in the Svacchanda manuscript tradition. The specific formulation is not reproduced here, as per-text variants differ and the published editions carry explicit instructions limiting open recitation to dīkṣit practitioners.
- **Mantra count:** Paddhati literature documents puraścaraṇa counts in multiples of 100,000; the Svacchanda tradition places Bhairava puraścaraṇa counts in the lakha range. Specific numbers are not reproduced here.
- **Duration:** Varies; puraścaraṇa cycles of 41 / 45 days are documented in the Tantrāloka's discussion of Bhairava practice (Abhinavagupta's commentary on Svacchanda).
- **Direction facing:** South or East, per paddhati convention (Bhairava's directional association in the Svacchanda tradition is south).
- **Mālā:** Rudrākṣa, per Śaiva convention; some Svacchanda recensions permit kapāla (skull-bead) mālā for advanced practitioners.

The Svacchanda Tantra codifies the Bhairava-Mantramārga procedure: standard Śaiva nyāsa sequence (kara-nyāsa, aṅga-nyāsa, Bhairava-nyāsa, Kṣetrapāla-nyāsa), followed by the dhāraṇā of Svacchanda-Bhairava with his five faces, four arms, and his śaktis (Aghorā etc.), then the *aṣṭākṣara* mūla-mantra japa, and a homa of ghee, sesame, and *kuśa* grass into a square or circular kuṇḍa. Sanderson (2007) notes that the Svacchanda-Bhairava cult is structurally the Śaiva-Bhairava counterpart of the Kālī-Kula of the Śākta tradition, and that the paddhati forms of the two are deeply cognate.

The *Kāla Bhairavāṣṭakam* of (attributed) Śaṅkarācārya is a stotra rather than a mūla-mantra, and is used in the smārta-Śaiva tradition as the liturgical accompaniment to the Bhairava-japa. The hymn opens with the verse "deva-stuti-praśasti" of Kāla Bhairava as the lord of Kāśī, and is documented in the 1910 Vani Vilas Press edition of the *Complete Works of Śrī Śaṅkarācārya*. The recitation of the *aṣṭakam* is paired, in the smārta tradition, with the Bhairava-aṣṭākṣara japa on Sundays and on the lunar day of Bhairavāṣṭamī.

## III. Yantra (if attested)

A square yantra with a central eight-petalled lotus enclosing the Bhairava bīja, surrounded by four concentric bhūpura rings, is described in the Svacchanda Tantra paddhati tradition. The eight petals contain the bījas of the eight Bhairavas (the *aṣṭabhairava* cycle). The yantra is traditionally drawn on copper, gold, or birch-bark using a paste of *bhasma* and *candanā*. Specific bīja placements within the eight petals are documented in the Svacchanda manuscript tradition (indexed in the Muktabodha digital library) and are not reproduced here.

## IV. Warnings and Contraindications (per sources)

- The Svacchanda Tantra explicitly restricts the Bhairava-aṣṭākṣara to practitioners who have received Bhairava-dīkṣā in a recognized Śaiva paramparā; paddhati literature is uniform on this point.
- Sanderson (1988, 2007) notes that the Bhairava-Mantramārga historically carried severe social and ritual risk; the Svacchanda tradition itself documents extensive prerequisites (including prior Sādhāśiva-dīkṣā) before the practitioner is eligible for Bhairava-japa.
- Dyczkowski (1987) notes that the Svacchanda-Bhairava practice is structurally allied with the Krama / Kālī-Kula practices and shares their *ugra* character; the textual tradition treats it as an advanced Śaiva practice not to be undertaken without prior grounding in the milder Sadāśiva / Parameśvara practices of the Śaiva Siddhānta.
- The Kāla Bhairava form, as *kṣetrapāla*, is traditionally held to be propitiated at Kāśī (Varanasi) and paddhati literature notes that "improper" Kāla Bhairava practice (i.e., without proper dīkṣā) is ritually inauspicious — this caution is recorded in the *Kāśī Khaṇḍa* of the Skanda Purāṇa.
- The hymn-form *Kāla Bhairavāṣṭakam* is more widely recited than the mūla-mantra; the hymn is treated as a devotional text accessible to smārta practitioners without Bhairava-dīkṣā.
