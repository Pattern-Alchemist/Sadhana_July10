# Lakulīśa Pāśupata Sādhana

> The Pāśupata Śaiva tradition attributed to Lakulīśa (c. 2nd c. CE) — the ascetic-only Śaiva path of the *atimārga*, outside the householder Mantramārga. Attested in the *Pāśupata Sūtras* with the *Pañcārthabhāṣya* of Kauṇḍinya (c. 4th–5th c. CE), and complemented by the *Gaṇakārikā*, the *Ratnaṭīkā*, and the *Mṛgendra Āgama*. The path proceeds through stages of deliberate ascetic transgression, ritual laughter, and the cultivation of divine pervasion. High caution — extreme austerities; an ascetic-only Śaiva path not to be undertaken by householders. Preserved here as heritage / source material. Not instructional.

The Pāśupata Śaiva tradition is the oldest historically-attested Śaiva sectarian order, attributed to Lakulīśa (c. 2nd c. CE, traditionally the 28th and last avatāra of Śiva per the *Liṅga Purāṇa*). The *Pāśupata Sūtras* — a corpus of approximately 170 sūtras in five *kāṇḍa*-sections — survive with the sole complete commentary, the *Pañcārthabhāṣya* of Kauṇḍinya (c. 4th–5th c. CE), edited in the Trivandrum Sanskrit Series 143 by Ananta Kṛṣṇa Śāstrī (Travancore, 1940). The Internet Archive holds three digitised editions of the TSS 143 text: the primary TSS 143 digitisation, an alternate-identifier digitisation, and an English translation under the title *Pashupata Sutra Bhagavatpad Shri Kaundinya*. The modern Sanskrit edition by Acharya Chakrapani Trivedi (Shaiva Bharati Varanasi #54, Jangamwadi Math Collection) is also on Internet Archive. The *Pañcārtha-bhāṣya* commentary is the foundational philosophical exposition of the Pāśupata doctrine of the *pañcārtha* — the five categories of *kārya, kāraṇa, yoga, vidhi, duḥkhānta* (effect, cause, union, procedure, end-of-suffering).

The Lakulīśa-Pāśupata order is historically attested from the 2nd century CE onwards through inscriptional evidence — the Karavana-Mahātmya inscriptions (Karvan, near Baroda, c. 5th–6th c. CE), the Nāsik cave inscriptions, and the Ekliṅgjī-Lakulīśa iconographic corpus. The order is the principal source of the early-medieval Śaiva atimārga (the "outer path" of asceticism outside the householder Mantramārga); Sanderson (1985, 2009) definitively establishes the Pāśupata atimārga as the historical source from which the Saiddhāntika Mantramārga later emerged. Ingalls (1962) — the foundational comparative study — frames the Pāśupata asceticism as structurally cognate to the Cynic tradition of deliberate dishonour-seeking, while refraining from positing a genetic relation. The Wisdom Library essays "Locating the Lakulīśa-Pāśupata rites in the world of Saivite rituals" and "Traces of Lakulīśa-Pāśupata order in North India" provide the modern scholarly digest.

The *Gaṇakārikā* of Haradatta-cārya (c. 8th c.) with the *Ratnaṭīkā* of Bhāsarvajña (c. 9th–10th c.) is the principal later-Pāśupata digest; the *Mṛgendra Āgama* (Vidyāpāda and Yoga-pāda, attributed to the sage Mṛgendra, c. 9th–10th c.) is the Mantramārga-digest of Pāśupata doctrine. The *Liṅga Purāṇa*'s account of Lakulīśa as the 28th avatāra of Śiva provides the purāṇic-sanctioned mythological frame; the *Śiva Purāṇa*'s Vāyavīya-saṃhitā gives the Lakulīśa-avatāra narrative in detail. The *Mahābhāṣya* of Patañjali (c. 2nd c. BCE) mentions the *Śiva-bhāgavata* ascetics — the proto-Pāśupata ascetic milieu from which the Lakulīśa-Pāśupata order later crystallised.

## II. Iconography (per sources)

The iconography of Lakulīśa himself (not the Goddess) is attested in the inscriptional and sculptural corpus — the Karvan-Kayāvarohana iconography (c. 5th–6th c.) and the Ekliṅgjī-Lakulīśa sculptural tradition. Lakulīśa is depicted as a seated yogin, two-armed, holding a *lakula* (club or staff — the etymology of his name: *lakula* [staff] + *īśa* [lord]), with his penis erect (*urdhva-liṅga* or *urdhvareta* — the symbol of his transmuted sexual energy), flanked by his four principal disciples Kuśika, Gārgya, Kauruṣa, and Mitraka. The Wisdom Library essay "Locating the Lakulīśa-Pāśupata rites" notes that the Lakulīśa sculptural form is "in a human form, symbolising ignorance [the body as the inert substratum], flanked by his four disciples" — the human form being a doctrinal statement of the Pāśupata conception of the guru as the embodied vehicle of Śiva's teaching, not as a transcendent being.

The iconography of the Pāśupata ascetic himself — as described in the *Pāśupata Sūtras* and the *Pañcārthabhāṣya* of Kauṇḍinya — is the iconography of deliberate dishonour: the ascetic (*avadhūta*) wears his hair matted (*jaṭā*), is covered in ashes (*bhasman* — the third-brahman-ash), carries a staff (*lakula*), and cultivates the appearance of a madman, a drunk, or a beggar. Ingalls (1962: 287–292) describes this as the "deliberate seeking of dishonour" (*avamāna-icchā*) — the Pāśupata ascetic intentionally provokes public contempt in order to transfer his own karma to those who insult him, while the insults themselves are interpreted as the ascetic's penance. The *Pāśupata Sūtras* (1.1–1.9) prescribe the stages of this cultivation: the *avyakta* stage (the ascetic dwells inconspicuously in a Śiva-temple), the *vyakta* stage (the ascetic makes himself conspicuous through laughter, song, dancing, and deliberate misbehaviour in public), and the *bhedopāya* stage (the ascetic provokes dishonour to transfer karma).

The iconographic frame of the Pāśupata ascetic is thus the inverse of the Śākta-Mahāvidyā iconography: where the Mahāvidyā Goddess is the cosmic-power-as-image (to be visualised by the practitioner), the Pāśupata ascetic is the practitioner-as-anti-image (to be made visible in public as a deliberate dishonour). The textual tradition treats this anti-iconography as itself the sādhana: the cultivation of the appearance of madness is the practice by which the ascetic's ego is dissolved.

## III. Mantra & Method (as documented in sources)

> **Editorial note.** The method below is reproduced from published textual sources for scholarly preservation. This is documentation, not instruction.

- **Bīja mantra / principal mantra:** The Pāśupata-vrata is not a mantra-centric sādhana in the way the Śākta Mahāvidyā practices are. The *Pāśupata Sūtras* (1.2–1.5) and the *Pañcārthabhāṣya* of Kauṇḍinya document the principal Pāśupata-mantra: the five-syllable *Śiva-pañcākṣara* (*namaḥ śivāya* — "obeisance to Śiva") and the *Aghora-mantra* (*aghorebhyo'tha ghorebhyo aghora-ghora-tārebhyas sarvabhūtebhyaḥ sarvataḥ sarvebhyo namaḥ sarva-bhūtebhyaḥ*). The specific mantra-form is documented in the *Mṛgendra Āgama* Vidyāpāda. The per-recension exact form is dīkṣā-restricted in the Pāśupata textual tradition.
- **Mantra count:** The Pāśupata-vrata is not structured around fixed japa-counts in the way the Śākta puraścaraṇa is. The *Pāśupata Sūtras* (2.1–2.10) document the *jñāna-vrata* (knowledge-observance) and the *vrata* (the vow) as continuing-practices rather than count-bound cycles.
- **Duration:** The Pāśupata-vrata is a lifetime-vow; the *Pāśupata Sūtras* document the staged progression through the *avyakta* (inconspicuous), *vyakta* (conspicuous), and *bhedopāya* (dishonour-cultivating) stages — typically of years' duration each — culminating in the *pratisṛti* stage (the descent into a Śiva-temple as the final act of dissolution).
- **Direction facing:** The Pāśupata ascetic is prescribed to dwell in a Śiva-temple (the *avyakta* stage) and then to move outward into the village and marketplace (the *vyakta* stage); direction-facing is not prescribed in the fixed manner of the Śākta practices.
- **Mālā:** The *lakula* (staff) is the principal ritual-instrument of the Pāśupata ascetic; a *rudrākṣa*-mālā is also documented for the *Śiva-pañcākṣara* japa. The *bhasman* (ashes) is the principal ritual-substance.

The procedure documented in the *Pāśupata Sūtras* (1.1–5.6) and the *Pañcārthabhāṣya* of Kauṇḍinya proceeds through the staged cultivation of the Pāśupata-vrata. The *avyakta* (inconspicuous) stage opens the practice: the ascetic dwells in a Śiva-temple, performs the morning-and-evening *bhasman*-application, recites the *Śiva-pañcākṣara* and the *Aghora-mantra*, and cultivates the inner contemplation of the *pañcārtha* (the five categories of Pāśupata metaphysics). The *vyakta* (conspicuous) stage follows: the ascetic moves outward into the village and marketplace, and engages in deliberate public misbehaviour — laughter, singing, dancing, snoring, mimicry of bodily functions — as the embodied expression of his contempt for samsāric convention. The *bhedopāya* (the method of provoking dishonour) is the third stage: the ascetic intentionally provokes public contempt through his appearance, his behaviour, and his words, in order to transfer his own accumulated karma to those who insult him; the insults themselves are interpreted as the ascetic's penance, while the insulters' merit is transferred to the ascetic.

The *pratisṛti* (the return to the temple) is the fourth stage: the ascetic returns to the Śiva-temple, where his karma has been fully transferred, and undertakes the final contemplation of Rudra as the *kāraṇa* (cause) of all *kārya* (effect). The *duḥkhānta* (the end of suffering) is the doctrinal terminus of the practice — the Pāśupata state of liberation in which the *paśu* (the bound soul) attains the *pati-śakti-sāmīpya* (proximity to Śiva-the-Lord) without complete identity (the Pāśupata doctrine is dualistic-tinged, not pure advaita). Ingalls (1962) describes this staged cultivation as the structural inversion of the Brahmanical purity-code: where the Brahmanical householder cultivates honour-and-purity, the Pāśupata ascetic cultivates dishonour-and-impurity as the means of spiritual dissolution.

Sanderson (1985, 2009) emphasises that the Pāśupata atimārga is structurally distinct from the Saiddhāntika Mantramārga: the atimārga is the ascetic-only path of deliberate dishonour; the Mantramārga is the householder-and-ascetic path of mantra-and-ritual. The Saiddhāntika tradition later absorbed elements of the Pāśupata doctrine (the *pañcārtha* metaphysics, the *bhasman*-application) but rejected the deliberate-dishonour practice as incompatible with the householder-ethic. The Wisdom Library essay "Locating the Lakulīśa-Pāśupata rites in the world of Saivite rituals" documents the historical transition from the atimārga-Pāśupata frame to the Mantramārga-Saiddhāntika frame.

## IV. Yantra (if attested)

No yantra is prescribed in the primary Pāśupata source texts surveyed. The Pāśupata tradition is not a yantra-centric tradition in the way the Śākta Mahāvidyā tradition is; the *Pāśupata Sūtras* and the *Pañcārthabhāṣya* of Kauṇḍinya do not document a yantra-practice. The principal ritual-instrument of the Pāśupata ascetic is the *lakula* (staff), the *bhasman* (ashes), and the *rudrākṣa*-mālā; the ritual-space is the Śiva-temple (in the *avyakta* and *pratisṛti* stages) and the village-or-marketplace (in the *vyakta* and *bhedopāya* stages). The later-Pāśupata digests (the *Gaṇakārikā* with the *Ratnaṭīkā*, and the *Mṛgendra Āgama*) do document mandala-practices for the Mantramārga-Pāśupata frame, but these are not part of the atimārga-Pāśupata sādhana of Lakulīśa himself.

## V. Warnings and Contraindications (per sources)

- The *Pāśupata Sūtras* (1.1) and the *Pañcārthabhāṣya* of Kauṇḍinya explicitly restrict the Pāśupata-vrata to brahmacārin ascetics who have completed the Vedic student-stage and who have formally taken Pāśupata-dīkṣā from an authorised guru; the textual tradition is uniform on this point. The *bhedopāya* stage (the deliberate cultivation of dishonour) is described in the *Pañcārthabhāṣya* as the most demanding stage, suitable only for those who have completed the *avyakta* stage.
- Ingalls (1962) and Sanderson (1985, 2009) emphasise the Pāśupata textual caution that the *vyakta* and *bhedopāya* stages — the deliberate public misbehaviour and the cultivation of dishonour — are not accessible to householders, who are bound by the Brahmanical purity-and-honour code. The textual tradition warns that a householder who attempts the *vyakta* stage will lose his social-and-ritual standing without gaining the spiritual benefit of the ascetic practice.
- The *Pāśupata Sūtras* warn that the deliberate-provocation-of-dishonour (the *bhedopāya*) must be undertaken only by an ascetic who has fully internalised the *pañcārtha*-metaphysics; an ascetic who undertakes the *bhedopāya* without prior stabilisation in the *avyakta*-stage contemplation risks spiritual regression rather than progression. Kauṇḍinya's *Pañcārthabhāṣya* treats this as a serious textual caution.
- The Pāśupata tradition's commentarial literature (the *Ratnaṭīkā* of Bhāsarvajña) warns that the karma-transfer mechanism — by which the ascetic's own karma is transferred to those who insult him — operates only for the genuine Pāśupata ascetic in the genuine *bhedopāya*-stage; an ascetic who cultivates dishonour for its own sake (rather than as a structural means of karma-transfer) gains no spiritual benefit.
- The Lakulīśa-Pāśupata atimārga must be distinguished from the later Mantramārga-Pāśupata (the Saiddhāntika tradition, which absorbed elements of the Pāśupata doctrine) and from the Kālāmukha and Kāpālika orders (which shared some ascetic practices but had distinct doctrinal frames); the atimārga-Pāśupata sādhana of Lakulīśa is the specific historical path documented in the *Pāśupata Sūtras* with the *Pañcārthabhāṣya* of Kauṇḍinya.
- The extreme-austerity character of the Pāśupata-vrata — the deliberate dishonour, the ashes, the staff, the temple-dwelling, the staged cultivation of madness — places this sadhana in the highest caution-level category. The textual tradition is uniform that this is an ascetic-only path, not adaptable to householder-practice without distortion.
