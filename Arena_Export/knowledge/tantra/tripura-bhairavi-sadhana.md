# Tripura Bhairavī Sādhana

> The sixth of the Daśa Mahāvidyās, Tripura Bhairavī is the fierce (*ugra*) form of Tripurasundarī — the goddess of *tapas* (spiritual austerity) and the transformative fire of practice, blending the beauty-consciousness of Sundarī with the purifying fire of Bhairavī. High caution — an advanced Śrī Vidyā Mahāvidyā rite positioned within the Śrī Cakra frame. Preserved here as heritage / source material. Not instructional.

Tripura Bhairavī is the sixth Mahāvidyā in the canonical lists of the *Todala Tantra* and the *Śāradā Tilaka*. Her primary textual home is chapter 11 of the *Śāradā Tilaka Tantra* — the Tripura-Bhairavī chapter — which gives the dhāraṇā, mūla-mantra, yantra, and homa procedure. The *Śāradā Tilaka* survives in multiple printed editions, all digitised on the Internet Archive: the Sudhakar Malaviya edition (the standard modern Varanasi edition), the Arthur Avalon 1933 translation (Tantric Texts Series 16), and the Srividya Tantram PDF transcription of the Tripura-Vidya verses (verses 7–85, which include the Tripura-Bhairavī chapter). The Śrī Vidyā Tantram PDF edition reproduces the operational verse on Tripura-Bhairavī homa: "When homa is done with Tripura-Bhairavī mantra, by offering *trimadhura* [curd, milk, and honey], the person lives for 100 years, free from diseases."

The *Paraśurāma Kalpa Sūtra* — the principal sūtra-text of the Śrī Vidyā tradition, traditionally attributed to Paraśurāma and commentated by Rāmeśvara (with the Nīrakṣīra-Viveka sub-commentary of Paramahaṃsa Miśra) — gives the Śrī Vidyā ritual-sūtra framework within which Tripura-Bhairavī is positioned as the *ugra*-face of Tripurasundarī. The Internet Archive holds four digitised editions of the *Paraśurāma Kalpa Sūtra*: the Sampurnanand Sanskrit University edition (with the Vṛtti and Nīrakṣīra-Viveka), the S. Y. Dave edition (with Rāmeśvara commentary), the Gaekwad Oriental Series edition, and the Umanandanātha Part II. The text contains 10 khaṇḍas and 337 sūtras and includes the worship of Lalitā Devi along with her related deities; Tripura-Bhairavī appears in the *ugra*-devatā frame.

Padoux (1990) and Brooks (1990) provide the standard scholarly framing of the Śrī Vidyā tradition within which Tripura-Bhairavī is positioned. Brooks (1990, *The Secret of the Three Cities*) traces the Śrī Vidyā textual genealogy from the *Vāmakeśvara Tantra* / *Nityāṣoḍaśikārnava* through the *Śāradā Tilaka* and the *Paraśurāma Kalpa Sūtra* to the early-modern South-Indian Śrī Vidyā commentarial tradition (Bhāskararāya, Nāgabhatta, Umanandanātha). Goudriaan and Gupta (1981) catalogue the *Tripura Bhairavī Tantra* among the late-medieval independent Mahāvidyā Tantras that survive mainly through the digests.

## II. Iconography (per sources)

According to the dhāraṇā preserved in *Śāradā Tilaka* chapter 11 and summarised in the commentarial tradition (Rāmtoṣaṇa Bhaṭṭācārya's *Prāṇatoṣiṇī* on the *Śāradā Tilaka*), Tripura Bhairavī is described as red-complexioned (*rakta-varṇā* or *aruṇā*), the colour of the rising sun, of *kuṅkuma*, of *rakta-candana*. She has three eyes, four arms, is seated on a lotus throne, and is ornamented with red garments, a garland of *mālatī* or *bandhūka* flowers, and a crown of red gems. Her four hands hold a *mālā* (rosary), a *pustaka* (book), a *varada*-mudrā (boon-granting gesture), and a *abhaya*-mudrā (fearlessness gesture). She is the goddess of *tapas* — the spiritual austerity whose fire purifies the practitioner.

The commentary tradition, as summarised by S. K. Ramachandra Rao in his *Srividya* (1998) and by Brooks (1990), distinguishes Tripura-Bhairavī from her benign cognate Tripurasundarī: where Sundarī is the *saumya*-form of the goddess dwelling in the *bindu* of the Śrī Cakra, Bhairavī is the *ugra*-form dwelling in the *tri-koṇa* (downward-pointing triangle) of the Śrī Cakra, the fire-aspect of the Goddess. The textual framing consistently treats Tripura-Bhairavī as the *tapas-śakti* of Tripurasundarī — the operational fire by which the practitioner's *tapas* (austerity) is kindled and sustained. The Svatantra Institute essay on Tripura-Bhairavī, drawing on the *Śāradā Tilaka* and *Tripura-Bhairavī Rahasya* tradition, notes that she is "the fierce form of Tripura Sundarī and closely related to the fifth Mahāvidyā, Bhairavī."

The Devipuram Amritananda Śrī Vidyā Online Forum entry on Tripura-Bhairavī, citing the *Śāradā Tilaka* and the *Tripura-Bhairavī Rahasya* tradition, describes her as the embodiment of "sacred intensity, tapas, and the transformative fire of spiritual practice." The textual tradition emphasises that her *ugra*-aspect is the fire of practice, not the fire of dissolution: she is the goddess who kindles and sustains the practitioner's *tapas*, in contrast to Kālī who is the goddess of dissolution-and-renewal.

## III. Mantra & Method (as documented in sources)

> **Editorial note.** The mantra and method below are reproduced from published textual sources for scholarly preservation. This is documentation, not instruction.

- **Bīja mantra:** The mūla-mantra is documented in *Śāradā Tilaka* chapter 11, with the commentaries of Rāmtoṣaṇa Bhaṭṭācārya (*Prāṇatoṣiṇī*) and of Bhāskararāya (in his *Śrī Vidyā* commentary cycle). The exact form is dīkṣā-restricted in published editions; the Śrī Vidyā tradition treats the mūla-mantra as the most closely guarded of the Śrī Cakra mantras. The publicly attested bīja-frame incorporates the *aiṃ* / *hriṃ* / *śriṃ* phonemes — the Śrī Vidyā triad — prefixed to the goddess's name-bīja, with the *namaḥ* termination. The per-recension exact form is not reproduced here.
- **Mantra count:** The *Paraśurāma Kalpa Sūtra* and its Rāmeśvara commentary document puraścaraṇa counts in multiples of 100,000; the structural count for Tripura-Bhairavī is recorded in the *Śāradā Tilaka* paddhati.
- **Duration:** Puraścaraṇa cycles of 21 / 41 / 45 days are documented in the *Śāradā Tilaka* and in the *Tripura-Bhairavī Rahasya*.
- **Direction facing:** East, per Śrī Vidyā paddhati convention (the Śrī Cakra frame is uniformly east-oriented).
- **Mālā:** *Sphaṭika* (rock-crystal) or *red-coral* (*pravāla*); the textual witnesses vary.

The procedure documented in *Śāradā Tilaka* chapter 11 opens with the standard Śrī Vidyā nyāsa sequence (kara-nyāsa, aṅga-nyāsa, ṣaḍaṅga-nyāsa, dig-bandhana, *pañca-pūjā*), followed by the dhāraṇā of Tripura-Bhairavī in the *tri-koṇa* of the Śrī Cakra, japa of the mūla-mantra on the prescribed mālā, and homa of *trimadhura* (a mixture of curd, milk, and honey), *ghee*, and red flowers into a square or triangular kuṇḍa. The *Śāradā Tilaka* verse on the homa with *trimadhura* is explicitly documented: "When homa is done with Tripura-Bhairavī mantra, by offering *trimadhura* made with curd, milk, and honey, the person lives for 100 years, free from diseases." The *Paraśurāma Kalpa Sūtra* and its commentaries document the *cakra-nyāsa* and *mātṛkā-nyāsa* preliminaries.

Padoux (1990) and Brooks (1990) note that Tripura-Bhairavī's worship in living Śrī Vidyā tradition is uniformly positioned within the Śrī Cakra frame, and that the textual tradition treats her as the *ugra*-face of the Goddess invoked after the practitioner has stabilised in the Tripurasundarī-Bālā practice. The *Tripura-Bhairavī Rahasya* (a paddhati text cited in Śrī Vidyā digest literature) records the *aṣṭa-māsika* (eight-month) puraścaraṇa variant.

## IV. Yantra (if attested)

A yantra is described in *Śāradā Tilaka* chapter 11 and in the *Tripura-Bhairavī Rahasya*. Tripura-Bhairavī's yantra is the Śrī Cakra itself, with the worship directed specifically to the *tri-koṇa* (downward-pointing triangle) enclosing the *bindu*. The *tri-koṇa* houses the goddess's bīja at its centre, with the bījas of her *aṅga*-devatās at the cardinal points. The Śrī Cakra is described as inscribed on gold-plate, copper-plate (*tāmra-paṭa*), or on *bhūrja* (birch-bark) using a paste of *kuṅkuma*, *gorocanā*, and *rakta-candana*. Specific bīja placements are documented in the *Śāradā Tilaka* paddhati and in the *Tripura-Bhairavī Rahasya* and are not reproduced in detail here.

## V. Warnings and Contraindications (per sources)

- The *Śāradā Tilaka* paddhati literature and the *Paraśurāma Kalpa Sūtra* with Rāmeśvara commentary explicitly restrict the Tripura-Bhairavī mūla-mantra to practitioners who have received Śrī Vidyā dīkṣā in an authorised lineage; the textual tradition is uniform on this point.
- Padoux (1990) and Brooks (1990) document the Śrī Vidyā textual caution that Tripura-Bhairavī — as the *ugra*-face of Tripurasundarī — is to be undertaken only after the practitioner has stabilised in the *saumya* Tripurasundarī-Bālā practice. Without that prior stabilisation, the *ugra*-fire of Tripura-Bhairavī is described in the commentarial literature as potentially destabilising.
- The *Tripura-Bhairavī Rahasya* records dietary and purity observances for the duration of the puraścaraṇa: white or red clothing, vegetarian diet, abstention from alcohol, celibacy, and residence in a quiet hermitage or temple precinct.
- The Śrī Vidyā tradition's commentarial literature (Bhāskararāya, Nāgabhatta, Umanandanātha) warns that the Śrī Cakra frame is itself a powerful ritual structure, and that the Tripura-Bhairavī invocation within it should be undertaken only under the supervision of a *guru* who has himself completed the Śrī Vidyā puraścaraṇa.
- The Hindu Tripura-Bhairavī tradition must be distinguished from the broader Mahāvidyā-Bhairavī tradition; her worship is Śrī Vidyā-framed and dīkṣā-gated.
