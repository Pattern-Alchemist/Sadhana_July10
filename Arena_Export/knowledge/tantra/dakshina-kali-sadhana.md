# Dakṣiṇa Kālī Sādhana

> The first of the Daśa Mahāvidyās and the foundational Kālī form, Dakṣiṇa Kālī stands upon the recumbent body of Śiva, four-armed, holding a severed head and a cleaver, garlanded with fifty skulls, her tongue extended. The principal textual witnesses are the *Karpūrādi-Stotra*, *Kālī Tantra*, *Todala Tantra*, and *Mahānirvāṇa Tantra*. High caution — an advanced Mahāvidyā rite of the Śākta-Kālī-Kula corpus. Preserved here as heritage / source material. Not instructional.

Dakṣiṇa Kālī is the first Mahāvidyā in the canonical list of the *Todala Tantra* (1.10–13), the *Bṛhad Nīla Tantra*, and the *Mahānirvāṇa Tantra*; she is the primary form of the Goddess Kālī as worshipped in Śākta Tantra — *dakṣiṇa* signifying both her auspicious, right-foot-forward posture (the right side being the *dakṣiṇa* side) and her capacity to receive the *dakṣiṇā* (offering) of the practitioner's ahaṅkāra. The *Karpūrādi-Stotra*, a 22-verse hymn attributed in its colophon to Mahākāla (Śiva-Bhairava), is the foundational stotra of Dakṣiṇa Kālī sādhana: Woodroffe's translation (Tantric Texts Series 9, 1922; reprint 1953 with the commentary of Swami Vimalānanda) is the standard published Western-language witness and explicitly identifies the Stotra as giving the *mantroddhāra* (mantra-extraction) of the 22-syllable Dakṣiṇa-Kālikā mantra. The 1953 HolyBooks PDF reproduces the same edition; the sacred-texts.com online text mirrors the Woodroffe translation.

The *Kālī Tantra* — a medieval Śākta Tantra of the Kālī-Kula corpus — gives the dhyāna-śloka, mūla-mantra, nyāsa, yantra, homa, and puraścaraṇa frame for Dakṣiṇa Kālī. Two Internet Archive digitisations preserve the printed Sanskrit/Hindi text: the Chowkhamba Krishnadas Academy edition with the Hindi commentary of Prof. Lakṣmīdatta Śāstrī Mālavīya, and the Rajesh Dīkṣit *Kālī Tantra Śāstra* edition. The *Todala Tantra* (Internet Archive, Sanskrit/Hindi) opens with its canonical enumeration of the ten Mahāvidyās and gives the Dakṣiṇa Kālī chapter as its first vidyā. The *Mahānirvāṇa Tantra* — edited and translated by Arthur Avalon / Woodroffe (Luzac 1913; reprinted and digitised on DLI 2015.345306; sacred-texts.com online text) — frames Kālī worship within the broader Manvaṅmānava-Saṃhitā-style householder-Śākta code, treating Dakṣiṇa Kālī as the supreme iṣṭa-devatā in the Mahānirvāṇa-vidhi.

Kinsley's *Tantric Visions of the Divine Feminine* (1997) devotes its first chapter to Dakṣiṇa Kālī as the foundational Mahāvidyā; Kinsley (1997: ch. 1) emphasises that she is the only Mahāvidyā whose iconic form is canonical across the entire Śākta corpus — standing on Śiva, four arms, garland of severed heads (muṇḍamālā), extended tongue, holding the severed head (chinna-muṇḍa) and the cleaver (khadga). Goudriaan & Gupta (1981: 96–104) catalogue the *Kālī Tantra*, *Karpūrādi-Stotra*, and *Todala Tantra* among the principal medieval Śākta texts attesting the Dakṣiṇa Kālī form. White (2003: ch. 4) traces the pre-tantric antecedents of Kālī in the early Śaiva-Buddha-Siddha milieu. McDermott (2001) provides the standard scholarly treatment of the Bengali Kālī devotional and ritual tradition.

## II. Iconography (per sources)

The dhyāna-śloka of Dakṣiṇa Kālī, as preserved in the *Kālī Tantra* (Mālavīya ed.) and reproduced in the Woodroffe translation of the *Karpūrādi-Stotra* (TT Series 9), describes her as *karala-vadanaṃ ghoraṃ mukta-keśīṃ caturbhujām* — "terrible of face, with dishevelled hair, four-armed." The first right hand holds the *khadga* (cleaver/sword), the second right hand makes the *varada-mudrā* (boon-granting gesture); the first left hand holds a *chinna-muṇḍa* (severed head), the second left hand makes the *abhaya-mudrā* (fearlessness gesture). She wears a *muṇḍa-mālā* (garland of fifty severed heads, corresponding to the fifty Sanskrit phonemes / mātṛkā-akṣara), a girdle of severed arms around her waist, and stands upon the recumbent body of Śiva (the *śava* of pure consciousness, inert without her śakti). Her tongue is extended, catching the drops of blood from the severed head; her complexion is *śyāma* (dark, like a rain-cloud at night); she is naked (*digambarī*) save for the sky as garment, and is seated on a *śava* upon a *smaśāna* (cremation-ground) or upon a white lotus.

The *Karpūrādi-Stotra* verse 2 (Woodroffe trans.) gives the classic mantroddhāra frame: "O Mother, the syllables *krīṃ krīṃ krīṃ hūṃ hūṃ hrīṃ hrīṃ* … *dakṣiṇe kālike* … *krīṃ krīṃ krīṃ hūṃ hūṃ hrīṃ hrīṃ svāhā*" — the 22-syllable mantra of Dakṣiṇa Kālī, which Woodroffe glosses as the *bīja* triad of *krīṃ* (Kālī-bīja), *hūṃ* (the fire/weapon-bīja), and *hrīṃ* (the māyā-bīja). The Stotra also describes the yantra-placement of her mantras. The commentary of Vimalānanda (in Woodroffe 1953) gives the symbolic-anatomical reading: the *śava* upon which she stands is the *parama-śiva* in its quiescent (*niskala*) state; the *khadga* is the *vidyā-śakti* that severs the bonds (*pāśa*) of the *paśu*; the *chinna-muṇḍa* is the *ahaṅkāra* offered back to the Goddess.

Kinsley (1997: ch. 1) surveys the iconography as attested in the *Karpūrādi-Stotra* and *Kālī Tantra* and notes that Dakṣiṇa Kālī's right-foot-forward posture distinguishes her from the left-foot-forward Smāśāna Kālī / Guhyakālī forms (the latter covered in the *Mahākāla Saṃhitā* Guhyakālī Khaṇḍa; see separate file *guhyakali-sadhana.md*). The *Mahānirvāṇa Tantra* (Avalon 1913 ed.) gives a more saumya iconography for the householder-Kālī worshipper. Goudriaan & Gupta (1981) catalogue the textual variants.

## III. Mantra & Method (as documented in sources)

> **Editorial note.** The mantra and method below are reproduced from published textual sources for scholarly preservation. This is documentation, not instruction.

- **Bīja mantra:** The 22-syllable Dakṣiṇa-Kālikā mantra as given in *Karpūrādi-Stotra* v. 2 (Woodroffe/Vimalānanda ed.): *krīṃ krīṃ krīṃ hūṃ hūṃ hrīṃ hrīṃ dakṣiṇe kālike krīṃ krīṃ krīṃ hūṃ hūṃ hrīṃ hrīṃ svāhā*. The Manblunder archive of Kālī-mantras (citing Woodroffe) reproduces this same form. The Kālī-bīja is *krīṃ*; the hṛdaya-bīja is *hūṃ*; the śakti-bīja is *hrīṃ*; the kiḷaka is the name-bīja *dakṣiṇe kālike*; the astra is *svāhā*. The exact mantra-form varies across the Kālī-Kula textual lineages (*Kālī Tantra*, *Todala Tantra*, *Mahākāla Saṃhitā* preserve slightly different bīja-permutations); the per-recension exact form is dīkṣā-restricted in published editions. The publicly attested Woodroffe/Vimalānanda form is given above for textual-reference purposes.
- **Mantra count:** *Kālī Tantra* and *Todala Tantra* document puraścaraṇa counts of 100,000 / 300,000 / 600,000 / 1,000,000 japa-cycles, with corresponding homa at 1/10th of the japa count (10,000 / 30,000 / 60,000 / 100,000 offerings). *Karpūrādi-Stotra* v. 21 promises siddhi at "lakh upon lakh" (*lakṣa-lakṣa*) of recitations.
- **Duration:** Puraścaraṇa cycles of 21 / 41 / 45 days; the *Kālī Tantra* documents a one-māsika (month-long), aṣṭa-māsika, and aṣṭādaśa-māsika (eighteen-month) sequence.
- **Direction facing:** South (the direction of Yama-Dakṣiṇāgni and of the cremation-ground) per *Kālī Tantra*; alternatively east per the *Mahānirvāṇa Tantra* householder-vidhi.
- **Mālā:** *Rudrākṣa* (preferably 108-bead, with the sumeru of crystal or silver); the *Kālī Tantra* also documents a *sphaṭika* (rock-crystal) mālā for the *śāntika* variant and a human-bone (*narāsthi*) mālā for the *uccāṭana* variant (the latter is dīkṣā-restricted).

The procedure documented in the *Kālī Tantra* (Mālavīya ed.) opens with the standard Śākta preliminaries — *kara-nyāsa*, *aṅga-nyāsa*, *ṣaḍaṅga-nyāsa*, *dig-bandhana*, *bhūta-śuddhi*, *prāṇa-pratiṣṭhā* — followed by the dhyāna of Dakṣiṇa Kālī upon the Śiva-śava, japa of the 22-syllable mūla-mantra on the prescribed mālā, and homa of *ghee*, *kuṅkuma*-mixed ghee, *kapittha* (wood-apple) leaves, red *japā* flowers, and *bilva* leaves into a triangular kuṇḍa (the triangle pointing downward, sacred to the yoni of Kālī). The *Karpūrādi-Stotra* v. 10–11 specifies the homa of *karpara* (camphor — the *karpūra* that names the Stotra) mixed with ghee as the supreme offering; Woodroffe (TT Series 9) glosses this as both the literal material offering and the symbolic offering of the *ahaṅkāra*-camphor that burns away the ego.

The *Mahānirvāṇa Tantra* (Avalon 1913: ch. 6) documents the householder-vidhi in which the Kālī-pūjā is performed with the five *makāra* in their substituted (*pratinidhi*) forms for the non-vīrācārī practitioner; Avalon's commentary preserves the standard Śākta-Tantric reformulation by which the Kālī-pūjā becomes accessible to the *gṛhastha*. Kinsley (1997: ch. 1) summarises the textual range from the *Karpūrādi-Stotra*'s purely mantric-stotra frame through the *Kālī Tantra*'s full pūjā-paddhati to the *Mahānirvāṇa Tantra*'s householder recension.

## IV. Yantra (if attested)

The *Kālī Tantra* and the *Todala Tantra* describe the Dakṣiṇa Kālī yantra: a *bhu-puṣa* (square bhūpura) of three concentric lines, enclosing an eight-petalled lotus (*aṣṭa-dala*), enclosing a downward-pointing triangle (*tri-koṇa*) housing the *krīṃ*-bīja at the centre, surrounded by the *hūṃ*-bīja at the three corners and the *hrīṃ*-bīja at the mid-points of the three sides. The *śava* of Śiva is visualised within the triangle beneath the Goddess. The yantra is described as inscribed on gold-plate, copper-plate (*tāmra-paṭa*), or on *bhūrja* (birch-bark) with a paste of *kuṅkuma*, *gorocanā*, and *rakta-candana*. The *Karpūrādi-Stotra* (v. 19) refers to the yantra-nyāsa. Specific bīja placements are documented in the *Kālī Tantra* paddhati and in the Mālavīya Hindi commentary.

## V. Warnings and Contraindications (per sources)

- The *Kālī Tantra* (Mālavīya ed., intro.) and the commentarial tradition cited by Woodroffe (TT Series 9, intro.) explicitly restrict the 22-syllable Dakṣiṇa-Kālikā mūla-mantra to practitioners who have received Śākta dīkṣā in an authorised Kālī-Kula lineage; the textual tradition is uniform on this point.
- Kinsley (1997: ch. 1) and Goudriaan & Gupta (1981) note that the *Kālī Tantra* classifies Dakṣiṇa Kālī among the *ugra*-devatās; the Mahāvidyā frame treats her as the most foundational but also the most demanding of the ten forms. Kinsley emphasises her "fierce benevolence" — she does not withhold siddhi, but she also does not withhold the dissolution of the practitioner's attachments.
- The *Mahānirvāṇa Tantra* (Avalon 1913: ch. 6) prescribes dietary and purity observances for the householder-vidhi: vegetarian diet, abstention from alcohol, celibacy during the puraścaraṇa cycle, white clothing, residence in a quiet hermitage or temple precinct, and the avoidance of inauspicious contact.
- The *Kālī Tantra* warns that japa performed without the prescribed nyāsa, dhyāna, and homa is "as fruitless as a cloud without rain"; the commentarial tradition further warns that incorrect bīja-permutation produces *pratyavāya* (spiritual counter-effect).
- The *Karpūrādi-Stotra* itself, in verses 13–17, describes the Goddess as the dissolution of all dualities (life/death, auspicious/inauspicious, pure/impure); the commentarial tradition (Vimalānanda in Woodroffe 1953) interprets these verses as a textual warning against undertaking the practice without prior stabilisation in the foundational Śākta-Tantric preliminaries (the ṣaḍaṅga-nyāsa, mātṛkā-nyāsa, and guru-yoga).
- The Hindu Dakṣiṇa Kālī tradition must be distinguished from the Buddhist *Kālacakra-Kālī* and from the *Smāśāna Kālī* / *Guhyakālī* / *Bhadrakālī* forms (the latter covered in separate files *guhyakali-sadhana.md* and *smasana-bhairavi-sadhana.md*); the Dakṣiṇa Kālī form is the saumya-ugra Mahāvidyā form, the others are the mahā-ugra ritual variants.
