# Tārā (Ugra / Bhuvaṇeśvarī) Sādhana

> The second of the Daśa Mahāvidyās, the Hindu Tārā ("she who ferries across") is Ugra-Tārā — dark-blue or black-complexioned, standing on a corpse (*śava*), adorned with a garland of severed heads, holding a chopper and a skull-cup. Distinct from the Buddhist Green Tārā / White Tārā. High caution — an advanced Mahāvidyā rite of the Śākta cremation-ground tradition. Preserved here as heritage / source material. Not instructional.

The Hindu Tārā is the second Mahāvidyā in the canonical lists of the *Todala Tantra* and the *Śāradā Tilaka*. Her primary textual home is the *Tārā Tantra* — an independent Śākta Tantra surviving principally through the recension embedded in the *Bṛhad Nīla Tantra* (the great Nīla-Tantra, ed. Kak & Shastri, Srinagar 1938; also ed. Madhusudan Kaul, Chaukhamba). The *Bṛhad Nīla Tantra* is the most extensive single published textual witness of the Hindu Tārā cult: it contains the *Tārā-rahasya* ("Secret of Tārā") attributed to Brahmāṇḍagiri, the mūla-mantra, the dhāraṇās of her three principal forms (Ekajaṭā, Ugratārā, Nīlasarasvatī), the yantra, and the homa procedures. The Internet Archive holds four digitised editions of the *Bṛhad Nīla Tantra* (the Kak & Shastri Srinagar 1938, the DLI/JaiGyan, the Madhusudan Kaul Chaukhamba, and a separate Madhusudan Kaul DLI witness).

The *Kālivilāsa Tantra* (ed. Parvati Charana Tarkatirtha, digitised from the University of Toronto Robarts collection) is the other principal primary source; it documents the Tārā-cult in its Kaula frame and is one of the few published Śākta Tantras that explicitly locates the Hindu Tārā within the Śmaśāna-Bhairavī ritual ecology. The *Ekajaṭā Tārā Sādhana Tantra* of S.N. Khandelwal (2001) is a modern printed paddhati that brings together manuscript material from multiple Śākta lineages.

Kinsley (1997) devotes a chapter to Tārā (pp. 92–106 of the UC Press edition) and treats the Hindu-Buddhist distinction at length: the Hindu Tārā, although historically cognate with the Buddhist Tārā who appears in the Mañjuśrīmūlakalpa and the Sādhanamālā, develops within the Śākta-Tantra frame as a Mahāvidyā of the cremation-ground, distinct in iconography (standing on a corpse, wearing the garland of severed heads, holding chopper and skull-cup) and in ritual frame (śmaśāna-setting, kṛṣṇa-pakṣa-candra observances) from her Buddhist cognate. The Hindu-Buddhist textual cross-pollination occurred in the late first-millennium Pāla-era Tantric milieu, but the two traditions diverged institutionally and iconographically in the second millennium.

## II. Iconography (per sources)

According to the dhāraṇā preserved in the *Bṛhad Nīla Tantra* (Kak & Shastri 1938 ed.) and summarised in Kinsley (1997, pp. 94–99), Hindu Tārā is described as dark-blue or black-complexioned (*kṛṣṇa* / *nīla*), standing on a supine corpse (*śavāsana*) identified with Śiva in his inert (*jaḍa*) form. She is nude or minimally clad (*digambarī*), wearing a garland of freshly severed human heads (*nara-muṇḍa-mālā*), her belly distended with the weight of the world she ferries across, her tongue extended in the gesture of *kīrtimukha*-ravenousness. She holds in her two principal hands a chopper (*kartarī*) and a skull-cup (*kapāla*); the secondary hands, in four-armed forms, hold a sword (*khaḍga*) and a lotus (*padma*) or a blue lotus (*utpala*).

The commentary tradition, summarised by Kinsley (1997), records three principal forms: (i) *Ekajaṭā* — "she of the single matted lock", two-armed, dark, standing on a corpse, the form most strongly associated with the *Bṛhad Nīla Tantra*'s *Tārā-rahasya*; (ii) *Ugratārā* — "the Fierce Tārā", four-armed, blue-black, standing on a corpse within a blazing cremation-ground, the form most strongly associated with the Mahāvidyā cycle; (iii) *Nīlasarasvatī* — "the Blue Sarasvatī", the form most strongly associated with the *jñāna* (wisdom) aspect, holding the *vīṇā* and the manuscript. The *Bṛhad Nīla Tantra* gives separate dhāraṇās for each.

The Pragyata essay "In Search of Bhagavatī Tārā" (citing the *Nīla Tantra* and the *Phetkāriṇī Tantra*) documents that Tārā is to be worshipped in a Śiva temple with an *ekaliṅga*, in a cremation ground, or in a forest hermitage — a textual stipulation that distinguishes her from the Brahminical Lakṣmī / Sarasvatī / Durgā paradigm of temple worship. The Hindu Tārā's *śava-asana* (corpse-seat) is the iconographic feature that most sharply distinguishes her from the Buddhist Tārā, who is depicted seated on a lotus in benign posture. The Hindu Tārā's belly-distension, her extended tongue, and her skull-cup-and-chopper iconography place her iconographically within the Kālī-Tārā-Chinnamastā cluster of the Mahāvidyā cycle, all of whom share the *ugra*-Mahāvidyā iconographic vocabulary.

## III. Mantra & Method (as documented in sources)

> **Editorial note.** The mantra and method below are reproduced from published textual sources for scholarly preservation. This is documentation, not instruction.

- **Bīja mantra:** The mūla-mantra is documented in the *Bṛhad Nīla Tantra* (Kak & Shastri 1938; Madhusudan Kaul), in the *Kālivilāsa Tantra*, and in the *Ekajaṭā Tārā Sādhana Tantra*. The exact form varies across textual lineages; the publicly attested bīja-frame incorporates the *hriṃ* / *hūṃ* / *phreṃ* phonemes prefixed to the *tāre* name-bīja, with the *svāhā* termination. The *Bṛhad Nīla Tantra*'s *Tārā-rahasya* treats the full mūla-mantra as dīkṣā-restricted; the per-recension exact form is not reproduced here.
- **Mantra count:** Paddhati literature documents puraścaraṇa counts in multiples of 100,000; the *Bṛhad Nīla Tantra* records structural counts for each of the three principal forms.
- **Duration:** Puraścaraṇa cycles of 21 / 41 / 45 days are documented in the *Kālivilāsa Tantra* and in the *Ekajaṭā Tārā Sādhana Tantra*.
- **Direction facing:** East or North, per paddhati convention (Tārā's directional association in the *Bṛhad Nīla Tantra* is *pūrva* — east — for the Ugratārā form).
- **Mālā:** *Rudrākṣa* or *sphaṭika* (rock-crystal); the textual witnesses vary.

The procedure documented in the *Bṛhad Nīla Tantra* opens with the standard nyāsa sequence followed by the dhāraṇā of the chosen form (Ekajaṭā, Ugratārā, or Nīlasarasvatī), japa of the mūla-mantra on the prescribed mālā, and homa of blue or dark-coloured flowers, *nīlotpala* (blue lotus), *durvā*-grass, and *kuṅkuma* into a triangular kuṇḍa. The *Kālivilāsa Tantra* documents the śmaśāna-frame variant, in which the homa is performed in a cremation ground at midnight during the *kṛṣṇa-pakṣa* (dark fortnight). The *Tārā-rahasya* of Brahmāṇḍagiri (embedded in the *Bṛhad Nīla Tantra*) records the fivefold *tārā-pañca-tattva* offering frame.

Kinsley (1997, pp. 100–106) notes that Hindu Tārā worship in living Śākta tradition is concentrated in the eastern Indian (Bengali, Assamese, Odia) Śākta-pīṭha network — the Tarapīṭha in West Bengal (associated with the saint Bāmākhyepā) is the principal living centre. The textual tradition cautions that the mūla-mantra, when undertaken without the śmaśāna-frame and without dīkṣā, is described in the *Bṛhad Nīla Tantra* as producing only the *ugra*-rebound without the *jñāna*-fruit. The Hindu Tārā's worship is structurally a Mahāvidyā rite of the cremation-ground, distinct in ritual setting from the Buddhist Tārā's soteriology of compassionate ferrying.

## IV. Yantra (if attested)

A yantra is described in the *Bṛhad Nīla Tantra* (Kak & Shastri 1938) and in the *Kālivilāsa Tantra*. It consists of a square *bhūpura* with four gates, enclosing an eight-petalled lotus (*aṣṭadala-padma*), which in turn encloses a downward-pointing triangle (*adho-mukha-trikoṇa*) housing the *tāre*-bīja at the centre and the bījas of her *aṅga*-devatās at the cardinal points. The triangle is the *yoni-mudrā* orientation, consistent with Tārā's Mahāvidyā framing. The yantra is described as inscribed on blue or black silk, on *bhūrja* (birch-bark), or on a copper-plate (*tāmra-paṭa*) using a paste of *kuṅkuma*, *gorocanā*, and *nīla*-pigment. Specific bīja placements are documented in the *Bṛhad Nīla Tantra*'s *Tārā-rahasya* section and are not reproduced in detail here.

## V. Warnings and Contraindications (per sources)

- The *Bṛhad Nīla Tantra*'s *Tārā-rahasya* explicitly restricts the mūla-mantra to practitioners who have received Tārā-dīkṣā in an authorised Śākta lineage; the textual tradition is uniform on this point.
- Kinsley (1997, pp. 100–106) documents the textual caution that Tārā's japa, undertaken without the śmaśāna-frame and without dīkṣā, is said in the *Bṛhad Nīla Tantra* to produce the *ugra*-rebound without the *jñāna*-fruit — i.e., to manifest the goddess's fierce aspect without the wisdom-ferrification.
- The *Kālivilāsa Tantra* records the śmaśāna-frame stipulation: japa of the mūla-mantra is to be performed in a cremation ground at midnight during the *kṛṣṇa-pakṣa* (dark fortnight); practitioners are to be clad in red or black garments and to have completed the *pañca-tattva* preliminaries.
- The *Ekajaṭā Tārā Sādhana Tantra* (Khandelwal 2001) records dietary and purity observances for the duration of the puraścaraṇa: vegetarian or non-vegetarian food per recension, abstention from alcohol during the Ekajaṭā form, dark or red clothing, and secluded residence.
- The Hindu Tārā tradition must be distinguished from the Buddhist Tārā (Green Tārā / White Tārā) traditions; the textual and iconographic sources are distinct, and paddhati literature warns that conflating the two lineages produces ritual disorientation.
