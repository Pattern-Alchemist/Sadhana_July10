# Ṣoḍaśī / Tripurasundarī (Pañcadaśākṣarī) Sādhana

> The third of the Daśa Mahāvidyās and the principal Goddess-form of Śrī Vidyā, Ṣoḍaśī ("the Sixteen-Year-Old") is the full pañcadaśākṣarī (fifteen-syllable) form of Tripurasundarī / Lalitā — distinct from the bālā-pañcadaśī form covered in *bala-tripurasundari-sadhana.md*. Attested in the *Vāmakeśvara Tantra* (= *Nityāṣoḍaśikārṇava*), the *Bhāvanā Upaniṣad*, the *Paraśurāma Kalpasūtra*, and the *Lalitā Sahasranāma* with the *Saubhāgya-Bhāskara* commentary of Bhāskararāya. High caution — an advanced Śrī Vidyā Mahāvidyā rite positioned within the Śrī Cakra frame. Preserved here as heritage / source material. Not instructional.

Ṣoḍaśī / Tripurasundarī is the third Mahāvidyā in the canonical lists of the *Todala Tantra*, *Śāradā Tilaka*, and the *Mahābhāgavata Purāṇa*; she is also the principal Goddess-form of the entire Śrī Vidyā tradition. The *Vāmakeśvara Tantra* — of which the surviving published core is the *Nityāṣoḍaśikārṇava* (with the *Artharatrāvalī* of Vidyāraṇya, c. 14th c., and the *Vimarśinī* of Jayaratha, c. 13th c.) — is the foundational śruti-text of the Śrī Vidyā tradition. The Internet Archive preserves six digitised editions of the *Nityāṣoḍaśikārṇava*: the Anandāśrama Sanskrit Series 56 of 1908 (the principal printed witness, with the Setubandha and Bhāskararāma commentaries), the Sampurnanand Sanskrit University edition of V. Rāja Vallabha Dvivedī 1985 (with two commentaries), the Shitalāprasād Upādhyāya edition, and the DLI Yog-Tantra-Granth-Mālā edition. The *Vāmakeśvarī Mātam* with Sampath Sharma's English translation of Jayaratha's *Vivarana* commentary is also on Internet Archive. Finn's *The Kulachudamani Tantra and the Vamakeshwar Tantra* (Internet Archive) provides the secondary Western-language treatment.

The *Bhāvanā Upaniṣad* — a Śākta Upaniṣad that codifies the Śrī Cakra-bhāvanā — gives the structural frame for the pañcadaśākṣarī (15-syllable) mūla-mantra of Tripurasundarī. The mantra's three *kūṭa*-segments (the Vāgbhava-Kūṭa, the Kāmarāja-Kūṭa, and the Śakti-Kūṭa) are coded into the Upaniṣadic verse-form; the Śrī Vidyā tradition reads the Upaniṣad as the vaidika-sanctioned form of the Śrī Vidyā mantra. (Note: the *Bhāvanā Upaniṣad* was already cited in *bala-tripurasundari-sadhana.md* — the present file extends the analysis to the full pañcadaśākṣarī and the Mahā-Ṣoḍaśī as distinct from the bālā-pañcadaśī.) The *Paraśurāma Kalpasūtra* — the principal sūtra-text of the Śrī Vidyā tradition, traditionally attributed to Paraśurāma and commentated by Rāmeśvara (with the Nīrakṣīra-Viveka sub-commentary of Paramahaṃsa Miśra) — gives the Śrī Vidyā ritual-sūtra framework: ten khaṇḍas and 337 sūtras. The Internet Archive holds four digitised editions: the Sampurnanand edition, the S. Y. Dave edition, the Gaekwad Oriental Series edition, and the Umanandanātha Part II.

The *Lalitā Sahasranāma* with the *Saubhāgya-Bhāskara* commentary of Bhāskararāya (ed. R. Ananthakrishna Sastry, Adyar 1925, Internet Archive) is the principal stotra-commentary of the Śrī Vidyā tradition; Bhāskararāya's commentary on names 327–333 (the *pañcadaśī* names) gives the standard commentarial treatment of the pañcadaśākṣarī-mantra. Padoux (1990) and Brooks (1990) provide the standard scholarly framing of the Śrī Vidyā textual genealogy; S. K. Ramachandra Rao (1998) gives the encyclopaedic handbook treatment; Madhu Khanna (1979) treats the Śrī Cakra-yantra. Goudriaan & Gupta (1981) catalogue the *Vāmakeśvara Tantra* and *Paraśurāma Kalpasūtra* as the principal texts.

## II. Iconography (per sources)

According to the dhyāna-śloka preserved in the *Vāmakeśvara Tantra* / *Nityāṣoḍaśikārṇava* (Anandāśrama 56 ed., verse 1.7) and summarised in the *Artharatrāvalī* commentary of Vidyāraṇya and the *Vimarśinī* of Jayaratha, Ṣoḍaśī / Tripurasundarī is described as a sixteen-year-old girl (*ṣoḍaśī-bālā* — the etymology of her name), red-complexioned like the rising sun (*aruṇa-varṇā*) or the *kuṅkuma* flower, seated on a lotus throne that rests upon the recumbent body of Śiva (the *sadāśiva* of the Śrī Vidyā ontology). She has four arms: the upper right hand holds the *aṅkuśa* (goad), the upper left hand holds the *pāśa* (noose), the lower right hand makes the *varada-mudrā* (boon-granting), and the lower left hand makes the *abhaya-mudrā* (fearlessness). She is ornamented with red garments, a garland of *bandhūka* flowers, a crown of red gems, and a crescent moon upon her forehead. The *Lalitā Sahasranāma* names 12–96 (with the *Saubhāgya-Bhāskara* commentary) describe her as *smerā-mukhī* (smiling-faced), *kāmeśvarī* (mistress of desire's lord), *lalitā* (the playful one), *tripura-sundarī* (the beauty of the three cities — the trailokya of physical, subtle, and causal worlds).

The commentarial tradition (Vidyāraṇya's *Artharatrāvalī*; Jayaratha's *Vimarśinī*; Bhāskararāya's *Saubhāgya-Bhāskara*) reads the sixteen-year-old form (*ṣoḍaśī*) as the symbolic embodiment of the *pañcadaśākṣarī* mantra plus the *Mahā-ṣoḍaśī* sixteenth syllable — i.e., the practitioner's transition from the pañcadaśī (15-syllable) to the ṣoḍaśī (16-syllable) is the transition from the Goddess as pañcadaśī-bālā to the Goddess as Mahā-ṣoḍaśī (the supreme mantra-form). The *Vāmakeśvara Tantra* (1.10–11) describes her as seated in the *bindu* of the Śrī Cakra — the centre of the *tri-koṇa* (downward-pointing triangle) enclosing the *bindu*, surrounded by the eight-petalled lotus, the sixteen-petalled lotus, and the outer three lines of the *bhūpura*. The Śrī Cakra itself is the yantra of Ṣoḍaśī (see Section IV below).

S. K. Ramachandra Rao (1998: ch. on Tripurasundarī) and Brooks (1990: ch. 4) note the textual distinction between the *saumya*-form of Tripurasundarī (the 16-year-old beauty with goad-and-noose) and the *ugra*-form of Tripura-Bhairavī (the same Goddess in her fire-aspect, covered in *tripura-bhairavi-sadhana.md*). The Śrī Vidyā paddhati uniformly treats Ṣoḍaśī as the *saumya* Mahāvidyā — the Goddess of aesthetic rasa and consciousness-as-beauty — distinct from the ugra Mahāvidyā forms. Kinsley (1997: ch. on Tripurasundarī) describes her as "the most benign of the Mahāvidyās in iconography, the most demanding in sadhana."

## III. Mantra & Method (as documented in sources)

> **Editorial note.** The mantra and method below are reproduced from published textual sources for scholarly preservation. This is documentation, not instruction.

- **Bīja mantra:** The pañcadaśākṣarī (15-syllable) mūla-mantra of Tripurasundarī is documented in the *Vāmakeśvara Tantra* / *Nityāṣoḍaśikārṇava* (1.7–9), the *Bhāvanā Upaniṣad*, and the *Paraśurāma Kalpasūtra* (with the Rāmeśvara commentary). The mantra is structured in three *kūṭa* (peak) segments: the Vāgbhava-Kūṭa (head-kūṭa), the Kāmarāja-Kūṭa (heart-kūṭa), and the Śakti-Kūṭa (root-kūṭa). The publicly attested structural form (per the Manblunder essay and the Śrī Vidyā paddhati) is: Vāgbhava-Kūṭa = *ka + e + ī + la + hrīṃ*; Kāmarāja-Kūṭa = *ha + sa + ka + ha + la + hrīṃ*; Śakti-Kūṭa = *sa + ka + la + hrīṃ*. The mantra exists in three principal lineages: the Kādi-Vidyā (mantra begins with *ka*), the Hādi-Vidyā (begins with *ha*), and the Sādi-Vidyā (begins with *sa*); the Kādi-Vidyā is the most widespread, with the Hādi-Vidyā preserved in the Kaśmīra and South-Indian recensions. The exact form is dīkṣā-restricted in published editions; the per-recension exact form is not reproduced here. The Mahā-Ṣoḍaśī (16-syllable) form adds a fourth *śrīṃ* bīja at the head of the Kāmarāja-Kūṭa; it is given in the *Kāmeśvara-Sūtra* portion of the *Vāmakeśvara Tantra* and in the *Paraśurāma Kalpasūtra* with the Rāmeśvara commentary.
- **Mantra count:** The *Paraśurāma Kalpasūtra* and its Rāmeśvara commentary document puraścaraṇa counts: 4 lakhs (400,000) for the Kādi-Vidyā; 6 lakhs for the Hādi-Vidyā; 9 lakhs for the Sādi-Vidyā. Each is accompanied by homa at 1/10th, tarpaṇa at 1/10th of homa, and abhiṣeka at 1/10th of tarpaṇa (the *daśāṃśa* rule).
- **Duration:** Puraścaraṇa cycles of 21 / 41 / 45 / 48 days are documented in the *Paraśurāma Kalpasūtra*; the Śrī Vidyā paddhati records an *aṣṭa-māsika* (eight-month) cycle and a *saṃvatsara* (one-year) cycle.
- **Direction facing:** East, per Śrī Vidyā paddhati convention (the Śrī Cakra frame is uniformly east-oriented).
- **Mālā:** *Sphaṭika* (rock-crystal) for the saumya Tripurasundarī form; *rudrākṣa* is also documented. The *Nityāṣoḍaśikārṇava* with the Vidyāraṇya commentary specifies the sphaṭika-mālā of 108 beads with a silver or gold sumeru.

The procedure documented in the *Vāmakeśvara Tantra* / *Nityāṣoḍaśikārṇava* (1.1–5) opens with the standard Śrī Vidyā preliminaries — *kara-nyāsa*, *aṅga-nyāsa*, *ṣaḍaṅga-nyāsa*, *mātṛkā-nyāsa*, *dig-bandhana*, *bhūta-śuddhi*, *prāṇa-pratiṣṭhā* — followed by the *guru-pūjā*, the *Gaṇapati-pūjā*, the *Śrī Cakra-bhāvanā* (visualisation of the nine-āvaraṇa Śrī Cakra with its 43+1 devatās), and the *Tripurasundarī-dhyāna* in the *bindu* of the Śrī Cakra. Japa of the pañcadaśākṣarī mūla-mantra is performed on the prescribed mālā, followed by homa of *ghee*, *trimadhura* (curd, milk, and honey), *kuṅkuma*-mixed ghee, and red flowers into a triangular or square kuṇḍa. The *Bhāvanā Upaniṣad* (verse-sequence 1–8) gives the Śrī Cakra-bhāvanā in coded verse form. The *Paraśurāma Kalpasūtra* (sūtras 1.1–3.5) gives the ritual-sūtra frame in 10 khaṇḍas.

Padoux (1990) and Brooks (1990) document the Śrī Vidyā commentarial emphasis on the mantra's three-kūṭa structure as the physiological-symbolic correspondence of the practitioner's three bodily centres (head, heart, root); the *Vimarśinī* of Jayaratha and the *Saubhāgya-Bhāskara* of Bhāskararāya are the standard commentarial witnesses. The Śrī Vidyā tradition's textual frame treats Ṣoḍaśī worship as uniformly positioned within the Śrī Cakra frame, with the practitioner's progression through the nine āvaraṇas (enclosures) of the Śrī Cakra being the structural sequence of the sādhana. The Śrī Vidyā Tantram PDF transcription of the Tripura-Vidya verses of the *Śāradā Tilaka* (verses 7–85) reproduces the operational verse on Tripurasundarī homa: "When homa is done with Tripurasundarī mantra, by offering *trimadhura* made with curd, milk, and honey, the practitioner attains the grace of the Goddess."

## IV. Yantra (if attested)

The Śrī Cakra (also called the Śrī Yantra) is the canonical yantra of Ṣoḍaśī / Tripurasundarī, described in detail in the *Vāmakeśvara Tantra* / *Nityāṣoḍaśikārṇava* (1.10–13), the *Bhāvanā Upaniṣad*, the *Paraśurāma Kalpasūtra* (Khaṇḍa 4), and the *Lalitā Sahasranāma* (names 832–996, with Bhāskararāya's *Saubhāgya-Bhāskara* commentary). The Śrī Cakra consists of nine concentric āvaraṇas (enclosures): (1) the outermost three-lined *bhūpura* (square enclosure, four cardinal gates); (2) a sixteen-petalled lotus (*ṣoḍaśa-dala*); (3) an eight-petalled lotus (*aṣṭa-dala*); (4–7) four interlocking triangles — fourteen small triangles, then ten, then ten, then eight; (8) the *tri-koṇa* (the downward-pointing central triangle enclosing the *bindu*); (9) the *bindu* (the central point, the abode of Tripurasundarī). The four upward-pointing triangles are the Śiva-cakra; the five downward-pointing triangles are the Śakti-cakra; their intersection produces the 43 devatā-positions of the Śrī Cakra. The Śrī Cakra is described as inscribed on gold-plate, copper-plate (*tāmra-paṭa*), or on *bhūrja* (birch-bark) using a paste of *kuṅkuma*, *gorocanā*, *rakta-candana*, and *sphaṭika* powder; the *Paraśurāma Kalpasūtra* with Rāmeśvara commentary documents the bīja-placements of all 43+1 devatās. Specific bīja placements are documented in the *Nityāṣoḍaśikārṇava* paddhati and in Madhu Khanna's *Yantra* (1979).

## V. Warnings and Contraindications (per sources)

- The *Vāmakeśvara Tantra* paddhati literature and the *Paraśurāma Kalpasūtra* with Rāmeśvara commentary explicitly restrict the pañcadaśākṣarī mūla-mantra to practitioners who have received Śrī Vidyā dīkṣā in an authorised lineage; the textual tradition is uniform on this point. The *Nityāṣoḍaśikārṇava* with the Vidyāraṇya commentary treats the mūla-mantra as the most closely guarded of the Śrī Cakra mantras.
- Padoux (1990) and Brooks (1990) document the Śrī Vidyā textual caution that the Śrī Cakra frame is itself a powerful ritual structure, and that the Tripurasundarī invocation within it should be undertaken only under the supervision of a *guru* who has himself completed the Śrī Vidyā puraścaraṇa. Brooks (1990: ch. 5) emphasises that the *nava-āvaraṇa-pūjā* (nine-enclosure worship) is a hierarchical sequence whose skip-stages are not permissible.
- The *Paraśurāma Kalpasūtra* records dietary and purity observances for the duration of the puraścaraṇa: white or red clothing, vegetarian diet, abstention from alcohol, celibacy, residence in a quiet hermitage or temple precinct, and the avoidance of inauspicious contact.
- Bhāskararāya's *Saubhāgya-Bhāskara* commentary on the *Lalitā Sahasranāma* (names 327–333) warns that incorrect recitation of the pañcadaśī — particularly the wrong *kūṭa*-sequence or the wrong *bīja*-permutation — produces *pratyavāya* (spiritual counter-effect); the commentarial tradition treats the Kādi/Hādi/Sādi lineages as distinct dīkṣā-traditions whose mantras are not interchangeable.
- The Śrī Vidyā tradition's commentarial literature (Bhāskararāya, Nāgabhaṭṭa, Umanandanātha) distinguishes the full pañcadaśākṣarī / Mahā-Ṣoḍaśī form from the bālā-pañcadaśī form (covered in *bala-tripurasundari-sadhana.md*); the bālā form is the introductory form, while the full Ṣoḍaśī form is the advanced form, to be undertaken only after stabilisation in the bālā practice.
- The Hindu Tripurasundarī tradition must be distinguished from the broader Mahāvidyā-Tripura-Bhairavī tradition (covered in *tripura-bhairavi-sadhana.md*); the Ṣoḍaśī form is saumya and Śrī Cakra-framed, the Tripura-Bhairavī form is ugra and Śrī-Cakra-tri-koṇa-framed.
