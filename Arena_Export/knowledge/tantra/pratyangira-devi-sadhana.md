# Pratyaṅgirā Devī Sādhana

> A fierce protective Śākta rite addressed to Pratyaṅgirā (also Atharvaṇa Bhadrakālī, Narasiṃhī, Siṃhamukhī), invoked to counter hostile mantra-prayogas and "reverse" (*viprīta*) inauspicious force. High caution — advanced Śākta rite associated with the Atharvaṇa layer and with abhicāra-counter (pratyangirāṇa) practice. Preserved here as heritage / source material. Not instructional.

---

## I. Textual Attestation

- **Primary text:** *Pratyaṅgirā Kalpa* (Tantra manuscripts preserved at the Dharmārth Trust, Raghunāth Temple, Jammu — Internet Archive digitisation); *Atharvānokta Pratyaṅgirā Vidhāna* (Tantra manuscript, same collection).
- **Secondary text(s):** *Viprīta Pratyaṅgirā Vidhānam eva Viprīta Pratyaṅgirā Stotram* (Pt. Ramji Sharma ed.); references to the goddess in the *Brahmayāmala* and *Jayadrathayāmala*; attestations in *Mātaṅgī Pārameśvarī* literature.
- **Key scholarship:** Shaman Hatley, "The Brahmayāmalatantra and Early Śaiva Cult of Yoginīs" (PhD diss., University of Pennsylvania, 2007); André Padoux, *Vāc: The Concept of the Word in Selected Hindu Tantras* (SUNY, 1990); Alexis Sanderson, "The Śaiva Exegesis of Kashmir" (2007); David Gordon White, *Kiss of the Yoginī* (Chicago, 2003).

Pratyaṅgirā Devī is one of the most formidable goddesses of the Śākta-Atharvaṇa layer. Her very name encodes the protective function: she is *prati-aṅgirā*, "the one who reverses the aṅgirā" — the latter being the Atharvanic incantation of hostile magic. The traditional identification with *Atharvaṇa Bhadrakālī* places her firmly within the Atharvaveda-linked magical-protective complex, a placement that mirrors the broader historical pattern by which Atharvanic materials were absorbed into the Śākta-Tantric textual corpus. Shaman Hatley's work on the *Brahmayāmalatantra* documents the late first-millennium Śākta-Bhairava milieu in which such fierce goddesses were codified as independent vidyā-deities, and Sanderson's "Śaiva Exegesis of Kashmir" places the broader Śākta-Mantramārga in the same period.

The manuscripts preserved at the Raghunāth Temple Trust (Jammu), now digitised on the Internet Archive, supply direct textual witness for the *Pratyaṅgirā Kalpa* and the *Atharvānokta Pratyaṅgirā Vidhāna*. These are paddhati-style ritual manuals containing the *dhāraṇī*, *bīja*, and *nyāsa* specifications for the goddess's invocation. They are devanagari transcripts of medieval originals, and reflect the north-Indian Śākta textual lineage that flows from the *Jayadrathayāmala* / *Brahmayāmala* corpus down to the modern Pitāmbarā Peeth and Kāmākṣya recensions. The *Viprīta Pratyaṅgirā Stotram* (Pt. Ramji Sharma ed.) is a printed ritual manual that documents the *viprīta* ("reversed" or "counter-") form of the practice used as a counter-prayoga to hostile abhicāra.

## II. Mantra & Method (as documented in sources)

> **Editorial note.** The mantra and method below are reproduced from published textual sources for scholarly preservation. This is documentation, not instruction.

- **Bīja mantra:** The exact form varies across textual lineages. The manuscripts of the *Pratyaṅgirā Kalpa* and *Atharvānokta Pratyaṅgirā Vidhāna* preserve bīja-compounds built around *hūṃ* and *kṣaṃ* phonemes, with Narasiṃha-linked *kṣrauṃ* and *phaṭ* terminations. The widely circulated *Atharvaṇa Bhadrakālī Mahāmantra* used in south-Indian temple recensions (e.g., Śrī Paṇḍuranga temple, Maharashtra) is documented in the modern Manblunder compilation; its specific form is not reproduced here, as the per-text formulation varies.
- **Mantra count:** Varies by lineage and prayoga-type. Manuscripts of the *Pratyaṅgirā Kalpa* document puraścaraṇa counts in multiples of the lakh (100,000), with structural parallels to other ugra-devatā puraścaraṇas. Specific counts are prayoga-dependent and are not reproduced here.
- **Duration:** Not fixed in the primary manuscripts; structured by puraścaraṇa cycles (typically 40-day or 48-day mandates) in paddhati usage.
- **Direction facing:** East or South, per paddhati convention; the manuscripts surveyed do not uniformly prescribe a direction.
- **Mālā:** Rudrākṣa is commonly attested in printed paddhatis; the primary manuscripts do not specify.

The procedure, as documented in the *Pratyaṅgirā Kalpa* manuscript and summarised in the Manblunder compilation of the *Śrī Atharvaṇa Bhadrakālī Pratyaṅgirā Mahāmantra Japa*, opens with the standard Śākta nyāsa sequence (kara-nyāsa, aṅga-nyāsa, gurupādukā, dhyāna of the goddess with her lion face and four or eight arms), followed by mūla-mantra japa and a homa of ghee with kuṅkuma and tila into a triangular kundī. The *Viprīta Pratyaṅgirā Vidhānam* (Pt. Ramji Sharma ed.) specifically records the counter-prayoga variant, in which the recitation is structured *viprīta* ("in reverse"), to be performed when the practitioner is the target of another's abhicāra. Sanderson (2007) and Hatley (2007) both note that such pratyaṅgirāṇa ("reversal") rites occupy a distinct ritual subcategory within the broader Śākta-Mantramārga and were historically transmitted only within tightly bounded guruparamparās.

The dhāraṇās in the manuscripts specify a goddess-form that is usually lion-faced (*siṃhamukhī*) or, in the Narasiṃhī variant, with a man-lion face, holding scissors and a noose (sometimes a skull-staff and a sword). The dhāraṇā connects her to the Narasiṃha avatāra mythology, identifying her as the śakti that emerged from Narasiṃha's own anger to pacify his own destructive ugra form — a self-pacifying recursion that is the mythic charter for her use as a counter-prayoga.

## III. Yantra (if attested)

A triangular yantra with a lion-faced bīja at its centre, surrounded by a protective ring of *phaṭ*-terminations, is described in the *Pratyaṅgirā Kalpa* manuscript tradition. As the manuscripts are devanagari transcripts of medieval paddhati material, specific dimensions and bīja placements vary across the textual witnesses and are not reproduced in detail here. The Śrī Paṇḍuranga temple Pratyaṅgirā Devī Mahāyāga paddhati (Maharashtra tradition) describes a similar triangular yantra used in the homa-kuṇḍa.

## IV. Warnings and Contraindications (per sources)

- The manuscripts of the *Pratyaṅgirā Kalpa* and the *Viprīta Pratyaṅgirā Vidhānam* frame this as an *ugra-devatā* rite; paddhati literature consistently restricts its transmission to those who have received dīkṣā from a qualified guru in a Śākta or Śaiva-Śākta lineage.
- The *viprīta* ("reverse") practice is explicitly framed by Pt. Ramji Sharma's edition as a counter-prayoga and is therefore only to be performed in the context of a confirmed hostile prayoga against the practitioner; performing it without that cause is, per the paddhati, ritually inauspicious.
- Hatley (2007) and Sanderson (2007) note that pratyaṅgirāṇa rites historically carried severe social and ritual risk, including the possibility that the counter-prayoga would rebound on the practitioner if procedural errors occurred.
- The goddess's identification with Narasiṃhī (the man-lion śakti) places her in the *ugra* category of Śākta deities; paddhati texts prescribe additional protective nyāsas (e.g., Narasiṃha-kavaca) around her recitation.
- The Atharvaṇa-Bhadrakālī layer is historically associated with the Atharvaveda's abhicāra materials; this places the practice under the traditional cautions that apply to Atharvanic rites.
